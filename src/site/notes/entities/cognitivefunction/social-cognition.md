<h1>Social Cognition</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Social cognition refers to the mental processes that underlie social interactions — the ability to perceive, interpret, and respond to the intentions, emotions, and behaviors of others. It includes theory of mind (mentalizing), empathy, emotion recognition, moral reasoning, and social decision-making. The &quot;social brain network&quot; is a distributed set of brain regions that evolved to process social information, and deficits in this network underlie conditions from autism to psychopathy.</p>
<h2>Key Components</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Theory of mind (mentalizing)</strong> — Attributing mental states to others (medial prefrontal cortex, temporal-parietal junction)</li>
<li><strong>Empathy</strong> — Sharing and understanding others' emotions (anterior insula, anterior cingulate cortex)</li>
<li><strong>Facial emotion recognition</strong> — Decoding emotional expressions (fusiform face area, amygdala)</li>
<li><strong>Social decision-making</strong> — Cooperation, trust, fairness judgments (ventromedial prefrontal cortex)</li>
<li><strong>Moral reasoning</strong> — Evaluating right and wrong in social contexts (ventromedial PFC, temporal-parietal junction)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Autism spectrum disorder</strong> — Theory of mind deficits, reduced social reciprocity</li>
<li><strong>Frontotemporal dementia</strong> — Loss of empathy, social disinhibition</li>
<li><strong>Schizophrenia</strong> — Impaired mentalizing and emotion recognition</li>
<li><strong>Psychopathy</strong> — Reduced empathy with intact cognitive theory of mind</li>
<li><strong>Social anxiety disorder</strong> — Heightened threat detection in social contexts</li>
</ul>
