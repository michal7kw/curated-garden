<h1>Language Processing</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Language processing encompasses the neural systems responsible for producing and comprehending spoken and written language. It is a uniquely human cognitive capacity involving phonological, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic processing levels. The classical model identifies Broca's area (inferior frontal gyrus) for production and Wernicke's area (posterior superior temporal gyrus) for comprehension, connected by the arcuate fasciculus — though modern neuroscience recognizes a far more distributed network.</p>
<h2>Key Components</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Phonological processing</strong> — Sound discrimination and manipulation (temporal cortex)</li>
<li><strong>Syntactic processing</strong> — Grammar and sentence structure (left inferior frontal gyrus)</li>
<li><strong>Semantic processing</strong> — Meaning extraction (anterior temporal lobe, angular gyrus)</li>
<li><strong>Pragmatic processing</strong> — Context-dependent meaning (right hemisphere, prefrontal cortex)</li>
<li><strong>Speech production</strong> — Motor planning and execution (Broca's area, motor cortex, cerebellum)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Broca's aphasia</strong> — Non-fluent speech with preserved comprehension (left inferior frontal damage)</li>
<li><strong>Wernicke's aphasia</strong> — Fluent but meaningless speech (left posterior temporal damage)</li>
<li><strong>Dyslexia</strong> — Impaired phonological processing and reading</li>
<li><strong>Primary progressive aphasia</strong> — Neurodegenerative language loss</li>
<li><strong>Autism spectrum</strong> — Pragmatic language impairments</li>
</ul>
