<h1>Spatial Navigation</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Spatial navigation is the cognitive capacity to determine and maintain a course through space. It relies on a remarkable system of specialized neurons: place cells in the hippocampus (encoding specific locations), grid cells in the entorhinal cortex (providing a metric coordinate system), head direction cells (acting as an internal compass), and border cells (encoding environmental boundaries). The 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was awarded for the discovery of these cells. Spatial navigation is one of the earliest cognitive functions impaired in Alzheimer's disease, making it a potential early biomarker.</p>
<h2>Key Components</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Allocentric navigation</strong> — Map-based, world-centered orientation (hippocampus, entorhinal cortex)</li>
<li><strong>Egocentric navigation</strong> — Self-centered, route-based orientation (parietal cortex, caudate nucleus)</li>
<li><strong>Place cells</strong> — Hippocampal neurons firing at specific locations</li>
<li><strong>Grid cells</strong> — Entorhinal neurons forming hexagonal spatial maps</li>
<li><strong>Path integration</strong> — Dead reckoning using self-motion cues (entorhinal cortex)</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive mapping</strong> — Building internal representations of environments (hippocampus)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Alzheimer's disease</strong> — Spatial disorientation is an early symptom (entorhinal cortex atrophy)</li>
<li><strong>Mild cognitive impairment</strong> — Navigation tests may detect pre-dementia decline</li>
<li><strong>Epilepsy</strong> — Temporal lobe seizures disrupt place cell function</li>
<li><strong>Developmental topographical disorientation</strong> — Lifelong navigation impairment</li>
<li><strong>Aging</strong> — Normal age-related decline in allocentric navigation</li>
</ul>
