<h1>Motor Planning</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Motor planning (also called motor programming) is the cognitive process of preparing voluntary movements before their execution. It involves selecting the appropriate muscles, determining force and timing, sequencing complex actions, and predicting sensory consequences. This function bridges cognition and action — it is not merely reflexive movement but the intentional planning that precedes it. The premotor cortex, supplementary motor area (SMA), basal ganglia, and cerebellum form the core motor planning circuit.</p>
<h2>Key Components</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Action selection</strong> — Choosing which movement to perform (premotor cortex, basal ganglia)</li>
<li><strong>Sequencing</strong> — Ordering sub-movements into coherent actions (SMA, pre-SMA)</li>
<li><strong>Timing</strong> — Precise temporal coordination (cerebellum, basal ganglia)</li>
<li><strong>Force calibration</strong> — Appropriate effort for the task (primary motor cortex, cerebellum)</li>
<li><strong>Internal models</strong> — Forward predictions of movement outcomes (cerebellum)</li>
<li><strong>Motor learning</strong> — Acquisition and refinement of motor skills (striatum, cerebellum)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Parkinson's disease</strong> — Impaired movement initiation and sequencing (basal ganglia dopamine depletion)</li>
<li><strong>Apraxia</strong> — Inability to perform learned movements despite intact motor strength (parietal lobe lesions)</li>
<li><strong>Cerebellar ataxia</strong> — Impaired timing and coordination</li>
<li><strong>Developmental coordination disorder</strong> — Motor planning deficits in children</li>
<li><strong>Huntington's disease</strong> — Involuntary movements from striatal degeneration</li>
</ul>
