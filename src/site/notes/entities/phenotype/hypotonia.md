<h1>Hypotonia</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Hypotonia is an objective medical phenotype characterized by massively reduced resting muscle tone. While frequently conflated with sheer muscle weakness, tone specifically measures the passive resistance of a muscle to being passively stretched (e.g., when a doctor bends a patient's arm).</p>
<p>Pathological hypotonia implies a major breakdown anywhere along the motor pathway axis: starting from the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord), descending down the peripheral nerves, crossing the neuromuscular junction, or localized directly within the muscular tissue itself. It is uniquely pervasive in neonatal medicine, where it is colloquially referred to as &quot;Floppy Infant Syndrome.&quot;</p>
<p>To systematically diagnose hypotonia, clinicians strictly separate it into Central vs. Peripheral origins:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Central Hypotonia:</strong> Involves brain injury (Hypoxic-Ischemic Encephalopathy), genetic trisomies (Down Syndrome), or metabolic disorders. Reflexes are bizarrely preserved or hyperactive.</li>
<li><strong>Peripheral Hypotonia:</strong> Triggers include Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA), muscular dystrophies, or severe neuromuscular blocks (Botulism). Reflexes are usually completely absent or severely diminished.</li>
</ul>
