<h1>Norepinephrine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Norepinephrine (NE, also called noradrenaline; C8H11NO3, molecular weight 169.18) is a catecholamine with dual roles as a central neurotransmitter and peripheral hormone. It is the principal neurotransmitter of the sympathetic nervous system and the brain's primary arousal-promoting signal.</p>
<p>In the brain, norepinephrine is produced almost exclusively by neurons of the locus coeruleus (LC), a small nucleus in the pons containing approximately 15,000 neurons. Despite their tiny number (less than one-millionth of all brain neurons), LC neurons project to virtually every brain region — cortex, hippocampus, amygdala, thalamus, hypothalamus, cerebellum, and spinal cord — giving them outsized influence. LC firing rates are tightly state-dependent: maximal during wakefulness and stress, reduced during quiet rest, and virtually silent during REM sleep. This phasic-tonic firing pattern allows NE to shift the brain between states of focused attention (optimal phasic LC firing), hypervigilance/anxiety (excessive tonic firing), and inattention (insufficient firing).</p>
<p>Peripherally, norepinephrine is the primary neurotransmitter of the sympathetic nervous system. Released from postganglionic sympathetic neurons and the adrenal medulla, it mediates the fight-or-flight response: increasing heart rate and blood pressure (alpha-1 vasoconstriction, beta-1 cardiac stimulation), mobilizing glucose from liver glycogen, dilating pupils, inhibiting gastrointestinal motility, and increasing bronchodilation (beta-2). Clinically, intravenous norepinephrine (Levophed) is a first-line vasopressor for septic shock.</p>

<p><strong>Key pharmacological targets:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Alpha-2 agonists (clonidine, guanfacine) — sedation, antihypertensive, ADHD treatment</li>
<li>Beta blockers (propranolol, metoprolol) — hypertension, anxiety, cardiac protection</li>
<li>SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) — antidepressants that increase synaptic NE and serotonin</li>
<li>Tricyclic antidepressants — block NE and serotonin reuptake</li>
<li>Alpha-1 blockers (prazosin) — PTSD nightmares, hypertension</li>
</ul>
<h2>Synthesis and Metabolism</h2>
<h3>Synthesis Pathway</h3>
<pre><code>Phenylalanine → Tyrosine (phenylalanine hydroxylase)
  → L-DOPA (tyrosine hydroxylase — rate-limiting step)
  → Dopamine (aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase, requires B6)
  → Norepinephrine (dopamine beta-hydroxylase, requires ascorbic acid + O2)
  → Epinephrine (PNMT, requires SAM)
</code></pre>
<p>The conversion of dopamine to norepinephrine by dopamine beta-hydroxylase (DBH) occurs inside synaptic vesicles, not in the cytosol.</p>
<h3>Degradation Pathways</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>MAO-A pathway (primary in CNS):</strong> NE → DHPG (via MAO-A) → MHPG (via COMT) — MHPG is the primary CNS metabolite measured in research</li>
<li><strong>COMT pathway (primary peripherally):</strong> NE → Normetanephrine (via COMT) → VMA (via MAO-A + aldehyde dehydrogenase) — VMA is measured clinically in pheochromocytoma screening</li>
</ol>
<h3>Reuptake</h3>
<p>Norepinephrine is cleared from the synaptic cleft primarily by the norepinephrine transporter (NET). NET is the target of:</p>
<ul>
<li>SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) — antidepressants</li>
<li>Tricyclic antidepressants (imipramine, amitriptyline)</li>
<li>Stimulants (methylphenidate, atomoxetine for ADHD)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<h3>Attention and ADHD</h3>
<p>The prefrontal cortex requires optimal NE levels (inverted-U relationship) for executive function. Too little NE produces inattention and impulsivity (ADHD); too much produces distractibility and anxiety. Atomoxetine (NET inhibitor) and guanfacine (alpha-2A agonist) are FDA-approved for ADHD, specifically targeting noradrenergic prefrontal circuits.</p>
<h3>Depression</h3>
<p>The monoamine hypothesis of depression includes NE deficiency as a core mechanism. SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) and tricyclic antidepressants (imipramine, amitriptyline) increase synaptic NE availability and are effective antidepressants. Alpha-2 antagonists (mirtazapine) enhance NE release by blocking the inhibitory autoreceptor.</p>
<h3>Stress and Anxiety</h3>
<p>The locus coeruleus-norepinephrine (LC-NE) system is the brain's primary stress response system. Chronic stress produces tonic LC hyperactivation, leading to hypervigilance, anxiety, and PTSD symptoms. Alpha-2 agonists (clonidine, prazosin) reduce LC firing and are used for PTSD nightmares and anxiety.</p>
<h3>Cardiovascular</h3>
<p>Peripherally, NE maintains blood pressure through alpha-1 mediated vasoconstriction. Orthostatic hypotension results from inadequate NE release on standing. Conversely, excessive sympathetic NE drive contributes to essential hypertension. Beta blockers are first-line antihypertensives.</p>
