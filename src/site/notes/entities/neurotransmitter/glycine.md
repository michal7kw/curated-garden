<h1>Glycine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Glycine (C2H5NO2, molecular weight 75.03) is the simplest amino acid and one of the most versatile molecules in human physiology. It is the only proteinogenic amino acid that is not chiral, lacking a side chain beyond a single hydrogen atom. This structural simplicity underlies its functional diversity.</p>
<p>In the central nervous system, glycine has a unique dual signaling role. Through strychnine-sensitive glycine receptors (GlyR), it acts as the major inhibitory neurotransmitter in the spinal cord, brainstem, and retina, producing hyperpolarization via chloride influx. Simultaneously, glycine is an obligatory co-agonist at NMDA glutamate receptors throughout the brain — both glycine and glutamate must bind for the NMDA channel to open, making glycine essential for excitatory synaptic plasticity, learning, and memory. This dual inhibitory/excitatory nature makes glycine a critical modulator of the excitation-inhibition balance.</p>
<p>Beyond neurotransmission, glycine is a key metabolic precursor. It constitutes approximately 35% of collagen amino acids, is a rate-limiting substrate for glutathione (the brain's primary antioxidant), and serves as a precursor for creatine, porphyrins (heme), and purines (DNA/RNA). Glycine also conjugates with bile acids and benzoate in hepatic detoxification. Emerging evidence suggests glycine supplementation may improve sleep quality, modulate metabolic syndrome, and augment NMDA receptor function in schizophrenia.</p>

<p>GlyR is a pentameric ligand-gated chloride channel. Activation increases Cl- conductance, hyperpolarizing the postsynaptic neuron. The embryonic alpha2 homopentamer produces excitatory (depolarizing) responses due to high intracellular chloride from NKCC1 expression; this switches to inhibitory as KCC2 expression matures postnatally.</p>
<p><strong>Key pharmacology:</strong> Strychnine is a high-affinity competitive GlyR antagonist (causes convulsions). Taurine and beta-alanine are endogenous GlyR agonists. Zinc is a positive allosteric modulator. Caffeine is a competitive GlyR antagonist.</p>
<h3>NMDA Receptor (Glycine Site) — Excitatory</h3>
<p>Glycine binds to the NR1 subunit of the NMDA receptor at a site distinct from the glutamate binding site on NR2 subunits. Both glycine and glutamate must be bound simultaneously for channel opening — glycine is therefore an obligatory co-agonist, not merely a modulator. The glycine site is a pharmacological target for enhancing NMDA receptor function in schizophrenia (glycine, D-serine, GlyT1 inhibitors like bitopertin).</p>
<h2>Synthesis and Metabolism</h2>
<h3>Synthesis Pathways</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Primary route:</strong> Serine → Glycine (via serine hydroxymethyltransferase, SHMT; requires tetrahydrofolate/folate as cofactor)</li>
<li><strong>Threonine route:</strong> Threonine → Glycine + Acetaldehyde (via threonine dehydrogenase)</li>
<li><strong>Choline route:</strong> Choline → Betaine → Glycine (via inter-organ liver-kidney metabolism)</li>
<li><strong>Glyoxylate route:</strong> Glyoxylate → Glycine (via transamination)</li>
</ol>
<h3>Degradation</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Glycine cleavage system (GCS):</strong> Primary route in mitochondria; glycine + tetrahydrofolate + NAD+ → CO2 + NH4+ + methylene-THF + NADH</li>
<li><strong>SHMT reverse:</strong> Glycine → Serine (via SHMT); serine then → pyruvate (via serine dehydratase)</li>
<li><strong>D-amino acid oxidase route:</strong> Glycine → Glyoxylate → Oxalate</li>
</ol>
<h3>Reuptake</h3>
<p>Glycine is cleared from the synaptic cleft by two sodium-dependent transporters:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>GlyT1</strong> — primarily on astrocytes and glutamatergic neurons; regulates NMDA receptor glycine site occupancy. GlyT1 inhibition enhances NMDA receptor function.</li>
<li><strong>GlyT2</strong> — primarily on glycinergic neurons (presynaptic); regulates inhibitory glycine signaling. GlyT2 inhibition may enhance glycinergic inhibition.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<h3>Neurological Disorders</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Hyperekplexia (startle disease):</strong> Mutations in GLRA1 (GlyR alpha1) or GLRB cause exaggerated startle responses and neonatal hypertonia due to impaired glycinergic inhibition.</li>
<li><strong>Tetanus:</strong> Tetanus toxin cleaves synaptobrevin in glycinergic interneurons, blocking glycine release and causing spastic paralysis.</li>
<li><strong>Strychnine poisoning:</strong> Competitive GlyR blockade produces hyperexcitability, convulsions, and death.</li>
</ul>
<h3>NMDA Receptor Dysfunction</h3>
<p>Glycine's role as an obligatory NMDA co-agonist links it to conditions involving NMDA hypofunction:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Schizophrenia:</strong> NMDA hypofunction hypothesis; glycine and D-serine supplementation, and GlyT1 inhibitors (bitopertin), have been investigated as augmentation therapy for negative symptoms.</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive function:</strong> Glycine site occupancy modulates NMDA-dependent LTP and memory formation.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Metabolic and Systemic Roles</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sleep quality:</strong> Oral glycine (3g before bed) has been shown to improve sleep quality and reduce daytime sleepiness, likely via enhanced glycinergic inhibition and core body temperature modulation.</li>
<li><strong>Collagen synthesis:</strong> Glycine is required at every third position of the collagen triple helix; deficiency impairs wound healing and connective tissue integrity.</li>
<li><strong>Glutathione synthesis:</strong> Glycine is one of three amino acids in glutathione (GSH) and can be rate-limiting under conditions of oxidative stress or high GSH demand.</li>
<li><strong>Creatine synthesis:</strong> Glycine + Arginine → Guanidinoacetate (via AGAT) → Creatine; important for muscle and brain energy metabolism.</li>
</ul>
