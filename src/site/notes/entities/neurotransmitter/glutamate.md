<h1>Glutamate</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Glutamate is the principal excitatory neurotransmitter in the vertebrate central nervous system, mediating an estimated 90% of all synaptic connections in the human brain. It is a non-essential amino acid (C5H9NO4, molecular weight 147.13) that cannot cross the blood-brain barrier unassisted and must be synthesized locally within the CNS.</p>
<p>As the primary driver of excitatory signaling, glutamate is indispensable for normal brain function. It underpins synaptic plasticity through long-term potentiation (LTP) and long-term depression (LTD), the cellular mechanisms of learning and memory. Glutamatergic transmission at hippocampal and neocortical synapses is fundamental to cognitive processes including spatial navigation, declarative memory formation, and executive function.</p>
<p>The clinical significance of glutamate lies in the dual nature of excitatory signaling. When glutamate homeostasis is disrupted through impaired reuptake by EAAT transporters, excessive release, or reverse transporter operation, extracellular glutamate accumulates and overactivates NMDA and AMPA receptors. This leads to pathological calcium influx (excitotoxicity), triggering mitochondrial dysfunction, reactive oxygen species generation, protease activation, and ultimately neuronal apoptosis. Excitotoxicity is a central mechanism in stroke, traumatic brain injury, ALS, Alzheimer's disease, Huntington's disease, epilepsy, and multiple sclerosis.</p>

<p><strong>Key receptor property:</strong> NMDA receptors require simultaneous glutamate binding and membrane depolarization (voltage-dependent Mg2+ block removal) for activation, making them coincidence detectors essential for associative learning.</p>
<h2>Synthesis and Metabolism</h2>
<h3>Synthesis Pathway</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Primary route:</strong> Glutamine → Glutamate (via mitochondrial glutaminase in the glutamate-glutamine cycle)</li>
<li><strong>TCA cycle route:</strong> Alpha-ketoglutarate → Glutamate (via transamination or glutamate dehydrogenase)</li>
</ol>
<h3>Metabolic Fates</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>GABA synthesis:</strong> Glutamate → GABA (via glutamic acid decarboxylase, GAD; requires Vitamin B6/pyridoxal phosphate)</li>
<li><strong>Glutamine recycling:</strong> Glutamate → Glutamine (via glutamine synthetase in astrocytes)</li>
<li><strong>Glutathione synthesis:</strong> Glutamate + Cysteine → Gamma-glutamylcysteine (via glutamate-cysteine ligase, rate-limiting step)</li>
<li><strong>TCA cycle entry:</strong> Glutamate → Alpha-ketoglutarate (via glutamate dehydrogenase)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Clearance Mechanisms</h3>
<p>Extracellular glutamate is rapidly cleared by excitatory amino acid transporters (EAAT1-5), primarily located on astrocytes. EAAT2 (GLT-1) handles approximately 90% of total brain glutamate uptake. Impaired EAAT function is a key contributor to excitotoxicity in neurodegenerative disease.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<h3>Excitotoxicity</h3>
<p>Excitotoxicity is the process by which excessive glutamate release or impaired reuptake leads to overactivation of glutamate receptors, pathological calcium influx, and neuronal death. The cascade involves:</p>
<ol>
<li>Excessive glutamate in the synaptic cleft</li>
<li>Overactivation of NMDA/AMPA receptors</li>
<li>Massive Ca2+ influx into neurons</li>
<li>Mitochondrial calcium overload and dysfunction</li>
<li>Reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation</li>
<li>Activation of calpains, phospholipases, and endonucleases</li>
<li>Apoptotic and necrotic cell death</li>
</ol>
<h3>Neurodegenerative Diseases</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Alzheimer's Disease:</strong> NMDA receptor dysfunction contributes to amyloid-beta toxicity and tau hyperphosphorylation. Memantine (NMDA antagonist) is an approved treatment.</li>
<li><strong>ALS:</strong> Impaired EAAT2 glutamate transport in motor neuron astrocytes leads to excitotoxic motor neuron death. Riluzole (glutamate release inhibitor) modestly extends survival.</li>
<li><strong>Huntington's Disease:</strong> Mutant huntingtin sensitizes NMDA receptors, making striatal neurons vulnerable to physiological glutamate levels.</li>
<li><strong>Parkinson's Disease:</strong> Glutamatergic hyperactivity in the subthalamic nucleus contributes to motor symptoms; NMDA antagonism may provide benefit.</li>
<li><strong>Epilepsy:</strong> Excessive glutamate release causes paroxysmal depolarizing shifts, driving seizure activity.</li>
<li><strong>Stroke:</strong> Ischemic energy failure causes reverse glutamate transporter operation, massive extracellular glutamate accumulation, and widespread excitotoxic death in the ischemic penumbra.</li>
</ul>
