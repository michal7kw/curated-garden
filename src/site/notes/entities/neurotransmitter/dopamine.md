<h1>Dopamine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Dopamine is a catecholamine neurotransmitter and the central chemical messenger for reward processing, motivation, motor control, and executive function. First identified as a distinct neurotransmitter by Arvid Carlsson in 1957 (Nobel Prize, 2000), dopamine is synthesized from the amino acid tyrosine via the intermediate L-DOPA. Although it constitutes only about 1% of brain neurons, dopaminergic signaling has outsized influence over behavior, cognition, and movement (Bjorklund &amp; Dunnett, 2007, PMID: 17408758).</p>
<p>Dopamine operates through four major pathways: the <strong>mesolimbic</strong> (VTA → nucleus accumbens, reward and reinforcement), <strong>mesocortical</strong> (VTA → prefrontal cortex, executive function), <strong>nigrostriatal</strong> (substantia nigra → striatum, motor control), and <strong>tuberoinfundibular</strong> (hypothalamus → pituitary, prolactin inhibition). A critical conceptual distinction: dopamine does not simply signal &quot;pleasure.&quot; Research by Berridge and Robinson established that dopamine encodes <strong>incentive salience</strong> — the motivational &quot;wanting&quot; of rewards — which is dissociable from hedonic &quot;liking&quot; (Berridge, 2007, PMID: 17072591). This distinction explains why addicts compulsively &quot;want&quot; drugs they no longer &quot;like.&quot;</p>
<p>Beyond the CNS, dopamine serves as a precursor to norepinephrine and epinephrine in the adrenal medulla and sympathetic nerves, acts as a renal vasodilator at low doses (D1 receptor activation), and modulates gastrointestinal motility and immune cell function. Peripheral dopamine cannot cross the blood-brain barrier.</p>
<p>Evidence Level: 5/5</p>
<p><strong>Chemical Formula:</strong> C8H11NO2<br />
<strong>Molecular Weight:</strong> 153.18 g/mol</p>

<h3>Receptor Distribution and Clinical Significance</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Striatal D1/D2 balance</strong>: The direct motor pathway (D1, facilitatory) and indirect motor pathway (D2, inhibitory) must be balanced for smooth movement; D2 blockade by antipsychotics causes extrapyramidal side effects (EPS)</li>
<li><strong>Presynaptic D2 autoreceptors</strong>: Inhibit dopamine synthesis and release when activated; provide negative feedback regulation</li>
<li><strong>D2 receptor occupancy</strong>: Antipsychotic efficacy requires ~65% D2 occupancy; &gt;80% occupancy increases extrapyramidal side effect risk</li>
</ul>
<h2>Synthesis Pathway</h2>
<pre><code>Phenylalanine → Tyrosine → L-DOPA → Dopamine → Norepinephrine → Epinephrine
   [PAH]       [TH]⭐       [DDC]      [DBH]          [PNMT]

⭐ = Rate-limiting step (tyrosine hydroxylase)
</code></pre>
<h3>Step 1 — Tyrosine Hydroxylation (Rate-Limiting)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Enzyme:</strong> Tyrosine hydroxylase (TH)</li>
<li><strong>Reaction:</strong> L-Tyrosine → L-DOPA</li>
<li><strong>Cofactors:</strong> Tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4), Fe2+, molecular oxygen</li>
<li><strong>Regulation:</strong> Phosphorylation by PKA, CaMKII increases activity; end-product inhibition by dopamine; this is the rate-limiting step for all catecholamine synthesis</li>
<li>TH is the most tightly regulated enzyme in the pathway and the primary control point for dopamine production</li>
</ul>
<h3>Step 2 — DOPA Decarboxylation</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Enzyme:</strong> Aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase (AADC / DOPA decarboxylase)</li>
<li><strong>Reaction:</strong> L-DOPA → Dopamine + CO2</li>
<li><strong>Cofactor:</strong> Pyridoxal phosphate (PLP, vitamin B6)</li>
<li>This enzyme is abundant and not rate-limiting; L-DOPA is therefore rapidly converted to dopamine when administered as a drug (Levodopa therapy for Parkinson's)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Cofactor Requirements</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>BH4 (tetrahydrobiopterin)</strong>: Required for TH; synthesized de novo from GTP via GTP cyclohydrolase I; BH4 deficiency causes dopa-responsive dystonia</li>
<li><strong>Vitamin B6 (pyridoxal phosphate)</strong>: Required for AADC; B6 deficiency can impair dopamine synthesis</li>
<li><strong>Iron (Fe2+)</strong>: Required for TH; iron deficiency may impair dopamine production</li>
</ul>
<h3>Further Metabolism (Catecholamine Pathway)</h3>
<p>Dopamine is the precursor for norepinephrine (via dopamine beta-hydroxylase, DBH) and epinephrine (via phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase, PNMT). In dopaminergic neurons, dopamine synthesis stops here. In noradrenergic neurons and adrenal chromaffin cells, dopamine is converted to norepinephrine and stored in synaptic vesicles via the vesicular monoamine transporter (VMAT2).</p>
<h2>Degradation Pathway</h2>
<pre><code>                    ┌─── MAO-A/B ───→ DOPAC ─── COMT ───→ HVA
Dopamine ───┤                                              (measured in CSF/urine)
                    └─── COMT ───→ 3-MT ─── MAO ───→ HVA
</code></pre>
<ul>
<li><strong>MAO-A</strong> (monoamine oxidase A) — mitochondrial enzyme; primary dopamine catabolism in catecholaminergic neurons; also degrades serotonin and norepinephrine; inhibited by moclobemide (reversible MAO-A inhibitor)</li>
<li><strong>MAO-B</strong> (monoamine oxidase B) — mitochondrial enzyme; primary dopamine catabolism in astrocytes and serotonergic neurons; selective inhibitor selegiline and rasagiline used in Parkinson's disease to increase dopamine availability</li>
<li><strong>COMT</strong> (catechol-O-methyltransferase) — cytosolic enzyme; particularly important for dopamine catabolism in the prefrontal cortex (where DAT expression is low and COMT is the primary clearance mechanism); COMT Val158Met polymorphism affects enzyme activity and is associated with executive function and pain sensitivity</li>
<li><strong>HVA (homovanillic acid)</strong> — the final metabolite of dopamine; measured in CSF and urine as a clinical marker of dopaminergic activity; low CSF HVA is found in Parkinson's disease and some depressive states</li>
<li><strong>3-MT (3-methoxytyramine)</strong> — O-methylated metabolite of dopamine; serves as a marker of dopamine release (reflects extracellular DA that has been metabolized)</li>
</ul>
