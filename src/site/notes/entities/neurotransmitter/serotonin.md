<h1>Serotonin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine, 5-HT) is an indolamine monoamine neurotransmitter first isolated from blood serum by Rapport, Green, and Page in 1948. It is one of the most widely distributed signaling molecules in the body, with functions spanning central neurotransmission, gastrointestinal motility, hemostasis, cardiovascular regulation, and bone metabolism. Chemically, serotonin has the formula C₁₀H₁₂N₂O and a molecular weight of 176.21 g/mol.</p>
<p>Approximately 90-95% of the body's total serotonin is produced in enterochromaffin cells of the gastrointestinal tract by the enzyme tryptophan hydroxylase 1 (TPH1). A separate, critically important pool (~1-2%) is synthesized in the central nervous system by TPH2, expressed predominantly in the raphe nuclei of the brainstem. These two pools are separated by the blood-brain barrier — peripheral serotonin does not cross into the CNS. Platelets transport serotonin via the serotonin transporter (SERT/SLC6A4) and release it during clotting to promote vasoconstriction and platelet aggregation.</p>
<p>Clinically, serotonin is the primary target of SSRIs, the most widely prescribed class of psychiatric medications. Serotonergic dysfunction is implicated in major depressive disorder, anxiety disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, migraine, and irritable bowel syndrome. However, the relationship between serotonin and mood is more nuanced than the historical &quot;serotonin deficiency&quot; hypothesis suggested — a 2022 umbrella review by Moncrieff et al. (PMID: 35854107) found no consistent evidence for lower serotonin concentrations in depression, though serotonergic drugs remain therapeutically effective, likely through downstream neuroplastic changes.</p>
<p>Evidence Level: 5/5</p>
<h2>Primary Functions</h2>
<h3>Central Nervous System</h3>
<ul>
<li>Mood and emotional regulation</li>
<li>Sleep-wake cycle (precursor to melatonin in pineal gland)</li>
<li>Appetite and satiety (5-HT2C promotes satiety)</li>
<li>Cognitive functions: learning, memory, attention</li>
<li>Pain modulation (descending inhibitory pathways from raphe nuclei)</li>
<li>Thermoregulation (5-HT7, 5-HT1A)</li>
<li>Sexual behavior (generally inhibitory; some pro-sexual effects via 5-HT2A)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Gastrointestinal System</h3>
<ul>
<li>Gut motility (5-HT4 agonism promotes peristalsis)</li>
<li>Secretion regulation</li>
<li>Nausea and vomiting (5-HT3 receptors on vagal afferents)</li>
<li>Gut-brain axis signaling</li>
</ul>
<h3>Cardiovascular System</h3>
<ul>
<li>Vasoconstriction (5-HT2A on vascular smooth muscle)</li>
<li>Vasodilation (5-HT7; endothelial NO release)</li>
<li>Platelet aggregation (amplified via 5-HT2A)</li>
<li>Cardiac valve development (5-HT2B agonism causes valvulopathy — fenfluramine mechanism)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Other Systems</h3>
<ul>
<li>Bone metabolism (peripheral serotonin via TPH1 inhibits osteoblast proliferation)</li>
<li>Hemostasis (platelet serotonin release during clotting)</li>
<li>Immune modulation (context-dependent pro- or anti-inflammatory)</li>
<li>Bladder control (modulation of micturition reflex)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Receptor Types</h2>
<p>Serotonin acts through <strong>seven distinct receptor families</strong> (5-HT1 through 5-HT7), comprising <strong>14 recognized receptor subtypes</strong> in humans. All are G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) except 5-HT3, which is a ligand-gated ion channel.</p>

<p>Key receptor notes:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>5-HT1A</strong> serves as both somatodendritic autoreceptor (raphe nuclei, inhibiting serotonin release) and postsynaptic receptor (limbic/cortical regions, mediating anxiolysis).</li>
<li><strong>5-HT2A</strong> is the primary target of classical psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, DMT) and is antagonized by atypical antipsychotics.</li>
<li><strong>5-HT3</strong> is the only ionotropic serotonin receptor — antagonized by antiemetics like ondansetron.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Synthesis Pathway</h2>
<p><strong>Two-step enzymatic pathway:</strong></p>
<pre><code>L-Tryptophan → 5-Hydroxytryptophan (5-HTP) → Serotonin (5-HT)
     [TPH1/TPH2]           [AADC/DDC]
</code></pre>
<h3>Step 1 — Tryptophan Hydroxylation (Rate-Limiting)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Enzyme:</strong> Tryptophan hydroxylase (TPH)</li>
<li><strong>Reaction:</strong> L-Tryptophan → 5-HTP</li>
<li><strong>Cofactor:</strong> Tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4), requires O₂ and Fe²⁺</li>
<li><strong>Two isoforms:</strong>
<ul>
<li><strong>TPH1</strong> — expressed in enterochromaffin cells (peripheral serotonin), pineal gland</li>
<li><strong>TPH2</strong> — brain-specific, expressed in raphe nuclei neurons (identified by Walther et al., 2003, PMID: 12511643)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Regulation:</strong> Substrate availability (tryptophan competes with other large neutral amino acids for LAT1 transporter at the blood-brain barrier), neuronal firing rate, and feedback inhibition via 5-HT1A autoreceptors</li>
</ul>
<h3>Step 2 — Decarboxylation</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Enzyme:</strong> Aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase (AADC / DOPA decarboxylase)</li>
<li><strong>Reaction:</strong> 5-HTP → Serotonin</li>
<li><strong>Cofactor:</strong> Pyridoxal phosphate (PLP), the active form of vitamin B6</li>
<li>Not rate-limiting; shared with dopamine synthesis (converts L-DOPA to dopamine)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Further Metabolism (Pineal Gland)</h3>
<p>Serotonin → N-acetylserotonin (via AANAT) → Melatonin (via ASMT/HIOMT). This occurs exclusively in the pineal gland under circadian control.</p>
<h2>Degradation Pathway</h2>
<p><strong>Two-step oxidative deamination:</strong></p>
<pre><code>Serotonin → 5-Hydroxyindoleacetaldehyde → 5-Hydroxyindoleacetic acid (5-HIAA)
     [MAO-A]            [ALDH]
</code></pre>
<ul>
<li><strong>MAO-A</strong> (outer mitochondrial membrane) has the highest affinity for serotonin among monoamine oxidases. MAO-A inhibition is the mechanism of MAOI antidepressants.</li>
<li><strong>ALDH</strong> (primarily ALDH2 mitochondrial) converts the aldehyde intermediate to 5-HIAA, the primary urinary metabolite measured clinically (24-hour urinary 5-HIAA) as a marker for carcinoid tumors.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reuptake termination:</strong> The primary mechanism for terminating serotonergic neurotransmission is reuptake via SERT (SLC6A4), which recycles serotonin into the presynaptic terminal. SERT is the target of SSRIs and TCAs.</p>
