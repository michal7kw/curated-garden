<h1>Endorphins</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Endorphins are a family of endogenous opioid neuropeptides — the brain's own painkilling and reward-signaling molecules. The three major classes are <strong>beta-endorphin</strong> (the most potent, with analgesic activity approximately 18-33 times that of morphine), <strong>enkephalins</strong> (Met-enkephalin and Leu-enkephalin, widely distributed throughout the CNS), and <strong>dynorphins</strong> (dynorphin A, dynorphin B, primarily in the striatum and hypothalamus). Despite popular usage, &quot;endorphin&quot; technically refers specifically to beta-endorphin and its fragments; the broader family is more accurately termed &quot;endogenous opioid peptides&quot; (Akil et al., 1984, PMID: 6143532).</p>
<p>Endorphins are produced primarily in the hypothalamus, pituitary gland (anterior and intermediate lobes), and brainstem periaqueductal gray (PAG). They are also synthesized by immune cells (lymphocytes, macrophages), creating a neuroimmune axis for pain and inflammation modulation. Release is triggered by physical stress, intense exercise, pain, acupuncture, certain foods (spicy foods via capsaicin receptors, dark chocolate), sexual activity, and social bonding behaviors. The widely known &quot;runner's high&quot; — a state of euphoria and reduced pain perception during prolonged aerobic exercise — is mediated partly by beta-endorphin release, though recent evidence also implicates endocannabinoids as co-contributors.</p>
<p>Endorphins act through three classical opioid receptor families — mu (MOR), delta (DOR), and kappa (KOR) — all of which are Gi/Go-coupled GPCRs that inhibit adenylyl cyclase, open potassium channels, and close voltage-gated calcium channels, ultimately reducing neuronal excitability and neurotransmitter release. The mu receptor is the primary target of exogenous opioids (morphine, fentanyl) and mediates both the therapeutic analgesic effects and the dangerous side effects of opioid drugs (respiratory depression, dependence, euphoria).</p>
<p>Evidence Level: 5/5</p>

<p>Beta-endorphin is cleaved into shorter active fragments: <strong>alpha-endorphin</strong> (residues 1-16, behavioral effects) and <strong>gamma-endorphin</strong> (residues 1-17, neuroleptic-like activity). Met-enkephalin (also known as opioid growth factor, OGF) has additional roles in cell proliferation and immune regulation beyond neurotransmission.</p>

<p>All three receptors couple to Gi/Go proteins, producing: (1) inhibition of adenylyl cyclase (reducing cAMP), (2) opening of inwardly-rectifying potassium channels (hyperpolarizing neurons), and (3) closing of voltage-gated N-type calcium channels (reducing neurotransmitter release). This combined effect produces neuronal inhibition and reduced pain signal transmission.</p>

<h3>Key Enzymes</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Prohormone convertase 1/3 (PC1/3)</strong> and <strong>prohormone convertase 2 (PC2)</strong> — cleave POMC into beta-endorphin, ACTH, and MSH in a tissue-specific manner; PC1/3 predominates in the anterior pituitary (producing ACTH), PC2 in the intermediate lobe and hypothalamus (producing beta-endorphin)</li>
<li><strong>Carboxypeptidase E (CPE)</strong> — further processes peptide intermediates</li>
<li>The POMC gene is a remarkable example of tissue-specific processing: the same precursor produces ACTH (anterior pituitary), MSH (skin, intermediate lobe), and beta-endorphin (hypothalamus, PAG)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Degradation Pathway</h2>
<pre><code>Endorphins/Enkephalins → Inactive fragments → Amino acids (recycled)
    [Enkephalinase]       [Aminopeptidase]
    (Neprilysin/NEP)      (APN, DPP4)
</code></pre>
<ul>
<li><strong>Enkephalinase (neprilysin, NEP)</strong> — zinc metallopeptidase; the primary degradative enzyme for enkephalins; cleaves at the Gly-Phe bond; also degrades other signaling peptides (substance P, bradykinin, amyloid-beta)</li>
<li><strong>Aminopeptidase N (APN)</strong> — removes N-terminal amino acids from enkephalins</li>
<li><strong>Dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP4)</strong> — cleaves N-terminal dipeptides from several opioid peptides</li>
<li><strong>Pharmacological targeting</strong>: Enkephalinase inhibitors (e.g., racecadotril, used as an antidiarrheal) increase endogenous enkephalin levels by blocking degradation, providing analgesic and antisecretory effects without the dependence liability of exogenous opioids</li>
</ul>
<h2>Primary Functions</h2>
<h3>Pain Modulation</h3>
<ul>
<li>Endorphins are the cornerstone of the <strong>descending pain inhibitory system</strong>: PAG neurons release beta-endorphin onto the rostral ventromedial medulla (RVM), which in turn inhibits nociceptive transmission in the spinal dorsal horn</li>
<li>This is the same circuit activated by exogenous opioids and by <strong>placebo analgesia</strong> (naloxone-reversible, confirming endorphin involvement)</li>
<li><strong>Stress-induced analgesia</strong> — intense stress (combat, injury) triggers beta-endorphin release, producing temporary pain insensitivity critical for survival</li>
</ul>
<h3>Reward and Reinforcement</h3>
<ul>
<li>Endorphins in the nucleus accumbens and ventral tegmental area (VTA) modulate dopamine release, creating the rewarding and reinforcing properties of natural stimuli (food, social bonding, exercise) and drugs (alcohol, opioids)</li>
<li>Mu receptor activation in the VTA disinhibits dopaminergic neurons (by inhibiting GABAergic interneurons), indirectly increasing dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens — the universal reward pathway</li>
<li>Alcohol stimulates beta-endorphin release, which contributes to its rewarding effects; naltrexone (MOR antagonist) blocks this and is used to treat alcohol dependence</li>
</ul>
<h3>Mood and Emotional Regulation</h3>
<ul>
<li>Beta-endorphin has potent mood-elevating and anxiolytic properties</li>
<li>Kappa receptor activation by dynorphins produces the opposite effect — dysphoria and aversion — creating a hedonic counterbalance system</li>
<li>The mu/kappa balance is disrupted in mood disorders and addiction</li>
</ul>
<h3>Immune Function</h3>
<ul>
<li>Immune cells (T-lymphocytes, B-lymphocytes, macrophages, natural killer cells) produce and respond to endorphins</li>
<li>Beta-endorphin enhances natural killer (NK) cell cytotoxicity and modulates cytokine production</li>
<li>This neuroimmune opioid axis provides a mechanistic link between stress, mood, and immune function</li>
</ul>
<h3>Exercise Physiology</h3>
<ul>
<li>Prolonged aerobic exercise (typically &gt;20-30 minutes at moderate-to-high intensity) increases plasma beta-endorphin levels by 2-10x above baseline</li>
<li>This endorphin release contributes to the &quot;runner's high,&quot; post-exercise mood elevation, and exercise-induced analgesia</li>
<li>HIIT training produces more rapid and pronounced endorphin release compared to steady-state exercise</li>
<li>The endorphin response to exercise is blunted in overtrained athletes, potentially contributing to overtraining syndrome</li>
</ul>
<h3>Appetite and Feeding</h3>
<ul>
<li>Endorphins stimulate appetite, particularly for palatable (sweet, high-fat) foods</li>
<li>Naloxone reduces food intake in humans, confirming endorphin involvement in hedonic feeding (Bodnar, 2004, PMID: 15193180)</li>
<li>The endorphin system may contribute to compulsive overeating and food addiction</li>
</ul>
