<h1>Acetylcholine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Acetylcholine (ACh) holds a singular place in neuroscience history as the first neurotransmitter ever identified. In 1921, Otto Loewi demonstrated that a chemical substance (&quot;Vagusstoff&quot;) slowed the heart when transferred between frog hearts — this substance was acetylcholine, earning him the Nobel Prize in 1936 (shared with Henry Dale). Today, ACh is recognized as a master regulator of cognition, movement, and autonomic function, acting through two fundamentally different receptor families: nicotinic receptors (fast, ionotropic) and muscarinic receptors (slower, metabotropic) (Mesulam, 2004, PMID: 14653733).</p>
<p>In the CNS, ACh is the primary neurotransmitter of the <strong>basal forebrain cholinergic system</strong> — neurons in the nucleus basalis of Meynert, medial septum, and diagonal band of Broca project widely to the hippocampus and cerebral cortex, providing the cholinergic &quot;enabling signal&quot; for attention, learning, and memory encoding. This system is devastated in Alzheimer's disease, where loss of basal forebrain cholinergic neurons correlates closely with cognitive decline (Whitehouse et al., 1982, PMID: 7058341). In the periphery, ACh is the neurotransmitter at every skeletal neuromuscular junction, all autonomic ganglia, all parasympathetic postganglionic synapses, and sympathetic sweat gland innervation — making it arguably the most widespread neurotransmitter in the body.</p>
<p>A critical feature of cholinergic signaling is its <strong>extremely rapid termination</strong>: acetylcholinesterase (AChE) in the synaptic cleft hydrolyzes ACh within milliseconds (turnover rate ~10,000 molecules/second), making AChE one of the fastest enzymes known. This ensures precise temporal control of ACh signaling, particularly at the neuromuscular junction where millisecond precision is essential for coordinated movement.</p>
<p>Evidence Level: 5/5</p>
<p><strong>Chemical Formula:</strong> C7H16NO2+<br />

<p>Nicotinic receptors are pentameric ligand-gated ion channels (pLGICs). Binding of two ACh molecules opens the channel, allowing Na+ and Ca2+ influx, producing fast depolarization (milliseconds). They rapidly desensitize with sustained ACh exposure — this is why nicotine produces initial stimulation followed by receptor desensitization.</p>

<h2>Synthesis Pathway</h2>
<pre><code>Glucose → Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA ──────────────────────┐
                     [mitochondria]    [mitochondrial]  │
                                                       ├──→ Acetylcholine
Dietary Choline ──→ Choline (via CHT1 transporter) ────┘     [ChAT]
(mg/kg eggs, liver,                          ↑
 soy, supplements)                    Rate-limiting step:
                                     Choline availability
</code></pre>
<h3>Step 1 — Choline Uptake (Rate-Limiting)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>High-affinity choline transporter (CHT1)</strong> — sodium-dependent transporter on cholinergic nerve terminals; recycles choline from ACh hydrolysis; the rate-limiting step for ACh synthesis (not ChAT itself, but choline availability)</li>
<li>Choline is an essential nutrient obtained from diet (eggs, liver, soybeans, cruciferous vegetables) and supplements (alpha-GPC, citicoline/CDP-choline)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Step 2 — Acetylation</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Choline acetyltransferase (ChAT)</strong> — cytoplasmic enzyme in cholinergic nerve terminals; transfers acetyl group from Acetyl-CoA to choline</li>
<li><strong>Acetyl-CoA</strong> — supplied by mitochondria via the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex; links ACh synthesis to cellular energy metabolism</li>
<li>ChAT is the specific marker enzyme for identifying cholinergic neurons (ChAT immunohistochemistry)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Step 3 — Vesicular Storage</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Vesicular ACh transporter (VAChT)</strong> — loads ACh into synaptic vesicles using a proton gradient (antiport); VAChT gene is located within the ChAT gene locus, ensuring coordinated expression</li>
</ul>
<h2>Degradation Pathway</h2>
<pre><code>Acetylcholine ──→ Choline + Acetate
     [AChE]            ↓           ↓
                 [CHT1 reuptake]  [metabolized]
                 (recycled)       (enters TCA cycle
                                   via acetyl-CoA)
</code></pre>
<ul>
<li><strong>Acetylcholinesterase (AChE)</strong> — the primary ACh-degrading enzyme; located in the synaptic cleft (anchored by collagen Q and perlecan); one of the most efficient enzymes known (turnover number ~10,000/s); ensures ACh signaling is terminated within ~1 millisecond</li>
<li><strong>Butyrylcholinesterase (BChE)</strong> — plasma and glial enzyme;次要的 ACh hydrolysis; becomes increasingly important when AChE is pharmacologically inhibited; genetic BChE deficiency causes prolonged paralysis after succinylcholine administration</li>
<li><strong>Organophosphate poisoning</strong> (nerve agents, pesticides) irreversibly inhibits AChE, causing massive ACh accumulation, continuous muscle stimulation, seizures, and respiratory failure; treated with atropine (muscarinic antagonist) and pralidoxime (AChE reactivator)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Pharmacological Targeting of AChE</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Donepezil</strong> (Aricept) — reversible AChE inhibitor; first-line for Alzheimer's disease</li>
<li><strong>Rivastigmine</strong> (Exelon) — pseudo-irreversible AChE and BChE inhibitor; used in Alzheimer's and Parkinson's dementia</li>
<li><strong>Galantamine</strong> (Razadyne) — reversible AChE inhibitor + allosteric nicotinic receptor potentiator; dual mechanism</li>
<li><strong>Physostigmine</strong> — reversible AChE inhibitor; crosses BBB (unlike neostigmine); used for glaucoma and anticholinergic toxicity</li>
</ul>
<h2>Primary Functions</h2>
<h3>Learning and Memory</h3>
<p>ACh is the molecular substrate of the &quot;novelty signal&quot; in the brain (Hasselmo, 2006, PMID: 17011182):</p>
<ul>
<li>In the <strong>hippocampus</strong>, ACh (via M1 receptors) switches the network from &quot;retrieval mode&quot; to &quot;encoding mode&quot; by suppressing recurrent excitatory connections while enhancing afferent input — this allows new memories to be formed without interference from old ones</li>
<li>In the <strong>cortex</strong>, ACh enhances signal-to-noise ratio, improving attentional filtering and cognitive flexibility</li>
<li>Muscarinic M1 receptors enhance NMDA receptor function, facilitating long-term potentiation (LTP), the cellular basis of memory</li>
<li>Anticholinergic drugs (diphenhydramine, atropine, scopolamine) impair memory consolidation — this is why antihistamines cause drowsiness and forgetfulness</li>
</ul>
<h3>Attention and Arousal</h3>
<ul>
<li>Basal forebrain cholinergic neurons provide tonic activating input to the cerebral cortex</li>
<li>ACh release increases during sustained attention tasks, detecting salient stimuli, and orienting to novel events</li>
<li>Lesions of the nucleus basalis produce profound attentional deficits resembling ADHD</li>
<li>Nicotine enhances attention via alpha4beta2 and alpha7 nAChRs in PFC — this is why smokers often report improved focus</li>
</ul>
<h3>Neuromuscular Transmission</h3>
<ul>
<li>ACh is the sole neurotransmitter at the skeletal neuromuscular junction</li>
<li>Motor neuron terminal releases ACh → binds nAChRs on the motor end plate → endplate potential → muscle contraction</li>
<li><strong>Myasthenia gravis</strong>: autoantibodies against muscle nAChRs destroy receptors, causing fatigable muscle weakness</li>
<li><strong>Botulinum toxin (Botox)</strong>: cleaves SNARE proteins (SNAP-25), preventing ACh vesicle release → muscle paralysis (used therapeutically for dystonia, spasticity, migraines, cosmetics)</li>
<li><strong>Succinylcholine</strong>: depolarizing muscle relaxant (nAChR agonist that causes sustained depolarization and desensitization)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Parasympathetic Nervous System</h3>
<p>ACh is the neurotransmitter at all parasympathetic postganglionic synapses, mediating &quot;rest and digest&quot;:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Heart</strong>: M2 receptor activation slows SA node firing rate and AV node conduction → bradycardia</li>
<li><strong>GI tract</strong>: M3 activation increases motility and secretions (saliva, gastric acid)</li>
<li><strong>Lungs</strong>: M3 activation causes bronchoconstriction and bronchial secretion</li>
<li><strong>Bladder</strong>: M3 activation contracts detrusor muscle → urination</li>
<li><strong>Eyes</strong>: M3 activation constricts pupil (miosis) and contracts ciliary muscle (accommodation for near vision)</li>
<li><strong>Atropine</strong> (M receptor antagonist) blocks these effects: dilates pupils, dries secretions, increases heart rate, reduces GI motility</li>
</ul>
<h3>REM Sleep</h3>
<ul>
<li>Cholinergic neurons in the pedunculopontine nucleus (PPN) and laterodorsal tegmental nucleus (LDT) are the &quot;REM-on&quot; neurons</li>
<li>During REM sleep, these neurons activate the thalamus and cortex, producing the characteristic EEG desynchronization (activated, dreaming brain)</li>
<li>Anticholinergic drugs suppress REM sleep; cholinergic agonists can increase REM sleep</li>
</ul>
<h3>Autonomic Ganglia</h3>
<ul>
<li>ALL autonomic ganglia (both sympathetic and parasympathetic) use ACh as the preganglionic neurotransmitter, acting on neuronal nAChRs</li>
<li>This is why nicotinic receptor agonists affect both sympathetic and parasympathetic systems simultaneously</li>
</ul>

<tr>
<td><strong>Alpha-GPC</strong></td>
<td>Direct choline donor; crosses BBB; increases brain ACh synthesis</td>
<td>Clinical trials show cognitive improvement in Alzheimer's and vascular dementia (PMID: 12856759)</td>

<tr>

<td>Provides both choline and cytidine (→ uridine for phospholipid synthesis); enhances ACh synthesis and membrane repair</td>

<tr>
<td><strong>Lion's Mane</strong></td>
<td>Hericenones and erinacines stimulate NGF production, which supports cholinergic neuron survival and function</td>
<td>Animal studies show enhanced cholinergic function; human pilot studies for cognitive improvement</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Omega-3 Fatty Acids</strong></td>
<td>DHA maintains cholinergic neuron membrane integrity; may enhance choline availability for ACh synthesis</td>
<td>Epidemiological studies associate DHA intake with preserved cholinergic function</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Caffeine</strong></td>
<td>Adenosine A1 receptor antagonism disinhibits basal forebrain cholinergic neurons, increasing cortical ACh release</td>
<td>PET studies show caffeine increases cortical ACh release (PMID: 20149320)</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Nicotine</strong></td>
<td>Direct nAChR agonist; enhances attention, working memory, and cortical arousal</td>
<td>Robust evidence for cognitive enhancement; limited therapeutic use due to addiction risk</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Exercise</strong></td>
<td>Upregulates ChAT expression and neurotrophic factors (BDNF, NGF) that support cholinergic neurons</td>
<td>Animal studies consistently show exercise-induced cholinergic enhancement</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Magnesium</strong></td>
<td>Blocks NMDA receptors (modulating ACh-glutamate interactions); essential for proper neuromuscular function</td>
<td>Magnesium deficiency impairs neuromuscular transmission and cognitive function</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Vitamin B12</strong></td>
<td>Required for myelin maintenance of cholinergic neurons; deficiency causes cholinergic neuropathy</td>
<td>B12 deficiency is a reversible cause of cognitive impairment</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
