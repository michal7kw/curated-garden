<h1>Adenosine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p><strong>Adenosine</strong> is a neuromodulator that functions as the brain's &quot;fatigue signal.&quot; It is a byproduct of ATP breakdown: as neurons work and consume energy (ATP → ADP → AMP), adenosine accumulates in the extracellular space.</p>
<p>This accumulation creates <strong>Sleep Pressure</strong> (homeostatic sleep drive). When we sleep, adenosine is cleared or re-phosphorylated back to ATP, resetting the pressure.</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Receptors:</strong> Binds primarily to <strong>A1 receptors</strong> (inhibitory) on wake-promoting neurons, slowing down nerve activity.</li>
<li><strong>Caffeine:</strong> Structurally similar to adenosine. It acts as a <strong>competitive antagonist</strong>, blocking adenosine from binding to its receptors without activating them. This masks the feeling of fatigue but does not remove the sleep need.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Recent Research (2025)</h2>
<p>A breakthrough 2025 study linked adenosine accumulation directly to <strong>mitochondrial electrical stress</strong> (electron leak) in sleep-regulating neurons, unifying the theories of energy metabolism and sleep function.</p>
