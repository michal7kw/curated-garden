<h1>Histamine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Histamine (C5H9N3, molecular weight 111.15) is a biogenic amine with a uniquely dual identity: it is both a central neurotransmitter and a peripheral immune/inflammatory mediator. Discovered in 1910, it is now recognized as one of the most versatile signaling molecules in human physiology, involved in at least 23 distinct physiological functions.</p>
<p>In the brain, histamine is the primary wakefulness-promoting neurotransmitter. It is produced exclusively by neurons of the tuberomammillary nucleus (TMN) in the posterior hypothalamus, which project widely to nearly every brain region including the cortex, hippocampus, basal ganglia, amygdala, and thalamus. TMN neuron firing rates are tightly correlated with behavioral state: they fire rapidly during wakefulness, slow during quiet rest, and cease firing entirely during both REM and NREM sleep. This makes histamine the brain's arousal signal — first-generation H1 antihistamines cause sedation precisely because they cross the blood-brain barrier and block histamine's wake-promoting action.</p>
<p>Peripherally, histamine is stored in mast cells and basophils and released during allergic and inflammatory responses. It mediates vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, bronchoconstriction, and itch sensation via H1 receptors. In the gastrointestinal system, enterochromaffin-like (ECL) cells release histamine to stimulate gastric acid secretion from parietal cells via H2 receptors — the basis for H2 receptor antagonist drugs (famotidine, ranitidine) in treating peptic ulcers and reflux.</p>

<p><strong>Key pharmacological distinction:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>First-generation H1 antihistamines (diphenhydramine, chlorpheniramine) cross BBB → sedation</li>
<li>Second-generation H1 antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine) do not readily cross BBB → non-sedating</li>
<li>H2 receptor antagonists (famotidine) reduce gastric acid</li>
<li>H3 receptor antagonists/inverse agonists (pitolisant) promote wakefulness — approved for narcolepsy</li>
</ul>
<h2>Synthesis and Metabolism</h2>
<h3>Synthesis</h3>
<p>Histidine → Histamine (via L-histidine decarboxylase, HDC; requires pyridoxal phosphate/Vitamin B6 as cofactor)</p>
<h3>Storage and Release</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>CNS:</strong> Histaminergic neurons in the TMN synthesize and release histamine as a neurotransmitter</li>
<li><strong>Peripheral:</strong> Mast cells and basophils store histamine in granules; release triggered by IgE-mediated degranulation, complement, or direct ligand effects (morphine, curare)</li>
<li><strong>GI tract:</strong> ECL cells release histamine to stimulate parietal cell acid secretion</li>
</ul>
<h3>Degradation</h3>
<p>Two primary pathways:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>HNMT pathway (dominant in CNS):</strong> Histamine → N-methylhistamine (via HNMT) → N-methylimidazole acetaldehyde (via MAO-B) → N-methylimidazole acetic acid (via ALDH2)</li>
<li><strong>DAO pathway (dominant in periphery/gut):</strong> Histamine → Imidazole acetaldehyde (via DAO) → Imidazole acetic acid (via ALDH2)</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Clinical note:</strong> DAO deficiency (genetic or acquired) causes histamine intolerance — characterized by headache, flushing, GI symptoms, and urticaria after consuming histamine-rich foods (aged cheese, fermented foods, wine, cured meats).</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<h3>Sleep-Wake Regulation</h3>
<p>Histamine is the brain's primary arousal neurotransmitter. The TMN histamine system works in concert with orexin/hypocretin, norepinephrine (locus coeruleus), serotonin (raphe nuclei), and acetylcholine (basal forebrain) to maintain wakefulness. H3 receptor antagonists (pitolisant) enhance histamine release and are approved for narcolepsy with cataplexy. Conversely, loss of histaminergic neurons contributes to excessive daytime sleepiness in conditions like narcolepsy and Parkinson's disease.</p>
<h3>Allergic and Immune Function</h3>
<p>Histamine is the primary mediator of Type I hypersensitivity (immediate allergic) reactions. Mast cell degranulation releases histamine, causing vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, smooth muscle contraction, and mucus secretion. H1 antihistamines are the primary treatment for allergic rhinitis, urticaria, and conjunctivitis. The H4 receptor plays a key role in mast cell chemotaxis and is a target for inflammatory and autoimmune conditions.</p>
<h3>Gastrointestinal Function</h3>
<p>Histamine from ECL cells is the final common stimulator of gastric acid secretion — more important than direct vagal or gastrin stimulation of parietal cells. H2 receptor antagonists (famotidine) block this pathway and are mainstays of peptic ulcer and GERD treatment.</p>
<h3>Neuroinflammation and Cognition</h3>
<p>Brain histamine modulates neuroinflammation and has neuroprotective properties. H3 receptor antagonists are being investigated for cognitive enhancement in Alzheimer's disease and ADHD. Altered brain histamine levels have been reported in schizophrenia, with some genetic studies implicating HNMT polymorphisms.</p>
