<h1>NADH</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>NADH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide, reduced form) is the primary biological electron carrier, functioning as the central link between catabolic energy-harvesting pathways and ATP synthesis. It consists of two nucleotides (adenine and nicotinamide) joined by a pyrophosphate bridge. During metabolic oxidation of glucose, fatty acids, and amino acids, NAD+ accepts a hydride ion (H-) to become NADH, which then delivers those electrons to Complex I of the mitochondrial electron transport chain to drive oxidative phosphorylation.</p>
<p>The NAD+/NADH ratio is a fundamental indicator of cellular redox state. In healthy mammalian cytoplasm, the free NAD+/NADH ratio is approximately 700:1, strongly favoring oxidative reactions. This ratio controls the activity of key metabolic enzymes including glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate dehydrogenase, pyruvate dehydrogenase, and lactate dehydrogenase. Disruption of this ratio is associated with metabolic dysfunction, mitochondrial disease, and aging.</p>
<p>Beyond its redox role, the NAD+/NADH system intersects with critical signaling pathways. NAD+ is consumed by sirtuins (NAD+-dependent deacetylases regulating aging and metabolism), PARPs (DNA repair enzymes), and CD38 (immune signaling). The resulting decline in NAD+ availability with aging has made NAD+ restoration a major focus of aging and longevity research.</p>
<h2>Redox Chemistry</h2>
<p>NADH carries electrons via the reversible reduction of the nicotinamide ring. The reaction involves transfer of a hydride ion (H-) to the C4 position of the nicotinamide moiety:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Oxidation (NADH → NAD+):</strong> NADH donates electrons to Complex I, reducing ubiquinone and pumping 4 protons across the inner mitochondrial membrane. Each NADH yields approximately 2.5 ATP via the full electron transport chain.</li>
<li><strong>Reduction (NAD+ → NADH):</strong> NAD+ accepts electrons at key metabolic steps: glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase and pyruvate dehydrogenase (glycolysis), isocitrate dehydrogenase, alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, and malate dehydrogenase (TCA cycle), and multiple steps in beta-oxidation.</li>
<li><strong>Midpoint potential:</strong> The NAD+/NADH couple has E°' = -0.32 V, making NADH a strong enough reducing agent to drive ATP synthesis but mild enough to be regenerated by common metabolic substrates.</li>
</ul>
<h2>NAD+ Biosynthesis and Salvage</h2>
<p>NAD+ (the oxidized precursor of NADH) is maintained through two pathways:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>De novo synthesis</strong> — From the amino acid tryptophan via the kynurenine pathway (primarily in the liver) or from aspartic acid (in some organisms).</li>
<li><strong>Salvage pathway</strong> — The major source in most tissues. NAMPT (nicotinamide phosphoribosyltransferase) recycles nicotinamide back to NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide), which is then converted to NAD+ by NMN adenylyltransferase. This is the rate-limiting step for NAD+ homeostasis.</li>
</ol>
<p>Dietary vitamin B3 precursors — niacin (nicotinic acid), nicotinamide, and nicotinamide riboside (NR) — all feed into NAD+ biosynthesis. NR has gained attention as an efficient oral NAD+ precursor due to its bioavailability.</p>
<h2>Cellular Distribution</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Mitochondria</strong> contain 40-70% of total cellular NAD+, with the highest concentrations. The mitochondrial NAD+/NADH ratio is approximately 7-8, reflecting the highly oxidative environment.</li>
<li><strong>Cytosol</strong> has a very high free NAD+/NADH ratio (~700:1), favoring glycolytic flux and oxidative reactions.</li>
<li><strong>Nucleus</strong> contains NAD+-consuming enzymes (PARPs, sirtuins) that are sensitive to NAD+ availability.</li>
<li>The inner mitochondrial membrane is impermeable to NADH, requiring shuttle systems (malate-aspartate shuttle, glycerol phosphate shuttle) to transfer reducing equivalents from cytosol into mitochondria.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<h3>Aging and Longevity</h3>
<p>NAD+ levels decline with age across multiple tissues, contributing to mitochondrial dysfunction, reduced sirtuin activity, and impaired DNA repair. This decline is one of the most studied hallmarks of aging. NAD+ precursor supplementation (NMN, nicotinamide riboside) has shown benefits in animal models for lifespan, metabolic health, and neuroprotection.</p>
<h3>Metabolic Disease</h3>
<p>The NAD+/NADH ratio is disrupted in diabetes, fatty liver disease, and obesity. High-fat diets can shift the redox balance toward NADH accumulation, inhibiting sirtuin activity and promoting lipogenesis.</p>
<h3>Neurodegeneration</h3>
<p>NAD+ depletion is implicated in Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and multiple sclerosis. Neurons are particularly dependent on mitochondrial NADH oxidation for energy, and the brain consumes 20% of the body's ATP despite being 2% of body weight.</p>
<h3>Mitochondrial Disease</h3>
<p>Defects in Complex I (NADH dehydrogenase) are the most common mitochondrial disorders, causing lactic acidosis, encephalopathy, and multi-organ dysfunction due to impaired NADH oxidation.</p>
