<h1>Nicotinamide Riboside</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Nicotinamide riboside (NR) is a pyridine-nucleoside form of vitamin B3 and one of three known direct precursors to nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+), alongside nicotinamide (NAM) and nicotinic acid (niacin). NR occurs naturally in trace amounts in cow's milk (approximately 3-6 micromol/L) and other foods, but dietary intake contributes minimally to systemic NAD+ pools. Interest in NR as a nutritional supplement stems from its ability to raise blood NAD+ levels by 40-90% in humans at oral doses of 250-1000 mg/day, as demonstrated in multiple clinical trials.</p>
<p>NAD+ is an essential coenzyme found in all living cells that participates in over 500 enzymatic reactions, including all major redox reactions in glycolysis, the TCA cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation. Beyond its coenzyme role, NAD+ serves as a substrate for three classes of signaling enzymes: sirtuins (SIRT1-7, deacylases regulating metabolism, stress responses, and epigenetics), poly(ADP-ribose) polymerases (PARPs, DNA repair and genomic maintenance), and CD38/CD157 (calcium signaling and immune function). NAD+ levels decline with age — by an estimated 50% between ages 40 and 60 — contributing to mitochondrial dysfunction, reduced DNA repair capacity, and metabolic dysregulation.</p>
<p>NR is distinguished from other NAD+ precursors by its unique entry point into NAD+ metabolism. While nicotinamide requires the rate-limiting NAMPT enzyme (often impaired in obesity and aging), and niacin causes prostaglandin-mediated flushing at therapeutic doses, NR is phosphorylated directly by NRK1 and NRK2 kinases to form NMN, bypassing NAMPT entirely and avoiding flushing. This makes NR particularly attractive for populations with compromised NAMPT activity.</p>
<h2>Biosynthesis and NAD+ Metabolism</h2>
<h3>The NRK Pathway</h3>
<p>NR enters NAD+ metabolism through a dedicated two-step pathway:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>NRK1/NRK2 phosphorylation:</strong> Nicotinamide riboside kinases (NRK1, ubiquitous; NRK2, muscle-enriched and inducible) phosphorylate NR at the 5'-hydroxyl to produce nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN). This reaction requires ATP.</li>
<li><strong>NMNAT adenylation:</strong> Nicotinamide mononucleotide adenylyltransferases (NMNAT1 in nucleus, NMNAT2 in cytosol/Golgi, NMNAT3 in mitochondria) convert NMN to NAD+, using ATP as the adenylate donor.</li>
</ol>

<p>NR supplementation primarily raises the cytosolic and nuclear NAD+ pools. Mitochondrial NAD+ is maintained by the inner membrane NMNAT3 and is largely impermeable to NAD+ transport across the inner mitochondrial membrane. SIRT3 activation in mitochondria depends on the overall increase in the matrix NAD+/NADH ratio.</p>
<h3>NAD+ Consumption and the Salvage Cycle</h3>
<p>NAD+ is continuously consumed by signaling enzymes:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sirtuins:</strong> Each deacylation reaction consumes one NAD+ molecule, producing nicotinamide (NAM) and O-acyl-ADP-ribose. NAM acts as a feedback inhibitor of sirtuins.</li>
<li><strong>PARPs:</strong> DNA damage activates PARP1/2, which can consume 100-200 molecules of NAD+ per PARylation event. Excessive PARP activation depletes NAD+ and impairs mitochondrial function.</li>
<li><strong>CD38:</strong> A major NAD+ glycohydrolase whose expression increases with age, contributing to age-related NAD+ decline. CD38 knockout mice maintain higher NAD+ levels with age.</li>
</ul>
<p>The salvage pathway recycles NAM back to NAD+ via NAMPT and NMNAT. When this pathway is impaired (aging, obesity), NR provides an alternative salvage route.</p>
<h2>Clinical Evidence</h2>
<h3>NAD+ Elevation in Humans</h3>
<p>Multiple randomized controlled trials have demonstrated that oral NR supplementation (250-2000 mg/day) safely elevates blood NAD+ and its metabolites:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Trammell et al. (2016):</strong> Single doses of 100-1000 mg NR increased blood NAD+ by 2-3 fold within 4-8 hours, with sustained elevation at 1000 mg/day for 7 days.</li>
<li><strong>Martens et al. (2018):</strong> 500 mg twice daily for 6 weeks increased NAD+ in peripheral blood mononuclear cells and reduced systolic blood pressure by approximately 10 mmHg in adults with stage 1 hypertension.</li>
<li><strong>Dollerup et al. (2018):</strong> 1000 mg/day for 12 weeks increased whole-blood NAD+ without significant improvement in insulin sensitivity or body composition in obese men.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Cardiovascular and Metabolic Effects</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Blood pressure:</strong> The Martens (2018) trial demonstrated a clinically meaningful reduction in systolic blood pressure (-9.4 mmHg) in a predefined subgroup with elevated baseline blood pressure.</li>
<li><strong>Arterial stiffness:</strong> NR improved arterial stiffness (reduced augmentation index) in healthy middle-aged and older adults (Martens et al., 2018).</li>
<li><strong>Insulin sensitivity:</strong> Results have been mixed. Some trials show improvement in insulin sensitivity markers; others show no effect. A 2020 meta-analysis found no significant effect on HbA1c, HOMA-IR, or fasting glucose.</li>
<li><strong>Lipid metabolism:</strong> NR supplementation increased HDL cholesterol and decreased LDL cholesterol in some trials, but results are not consistent.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Neurodegeneration and Brain Health</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Parkinson's disease:</strong> An open-label trial (Brakedal et al., 2022) found that 1000 mg NR twice daily for 30 days increased brain NAD+ levels (measured by 31P-MRS) and improved mitochondrial function markers.</li>
<li><strong>Alzheimer's disease:</strong> Preclinical data show NR improves cognitive function and reduces neuroinflammation in AD mouse models, but human trials are ongoing.</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive function:</strong> A 2023 RCT found no cognitive improvement in healthy older adults after 12 weeks of NR supplementation.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Exercise and Muscle Function</h3>
<ul>
<li>NR supplementation increased NAD+ in muscle tissue and improved mitochondrial biogenesis markers (PGC-1alpha, TFAM) in some trials.</li>
<li>However, a meta-analysis of exercise-related outcomes found no consistent improvement in VO2max, exercise capacity, or muscle strength.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Aging and Longevity</h3>
<p>NR activates multiple longevity-associated pathways (SIRT1, SIRT3, SIRT6) and has shown lifespan extension in mouse models. The ongoing NIA Interventions Testing Program is evaluating NR for longevity effects. The NIH-funded &quot;TAME&quot; (Targeting Aging with Metformin) trial has inspired similar NR-based aging trials.</p>
<h2>Safety and Administration</h2>
<h3>Safety Profile</h3>
<p>NR has an excellent safety profile in clinical trials:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>No flushing</strong> (unlike niacin/nicotinic acid)</li>
<li><strong>No hepatotoxicity</strong> reported at any dose tested (up to 2000 mg/day)</li>
<li><strong>Mild side effects</strong> (uncommon): nausea, headache, fatigue — typically transient</li>
<li><strong>No serious adverse events</strong> attributed to NR in any published trial (through 2025)</li>
<li>FDA has granted NR &quot;Generally Recognized as Safe&quot; (GRAS) status for use in foods and supplements</li>
</ul>

<h3>Drug Interactions</h3>
<ul>
<li>May affect NAD+-dependent drug metabolism (cytochrome P450 enzymes require NADPH)</li>
<li>Theoretical interaction with chemotherapy (NAD+ supports DNA repair, potentially reducing efficacy of DNA-damaging agents)</li>
<li>No clinically significant drug interactions reported in trials to date</li>
</ul>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Limited data in pregnancy and lactation</li>
<li>Caution with immunosuppressants (NAD+ modulates immune function)</li>
</ul>
