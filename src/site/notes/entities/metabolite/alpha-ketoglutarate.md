<h1>Alpha-Ketoglutarate</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Alpha-ketoglutarate (AKG, also known as 2-oxoglutarate or 2-OG) is a five-carbon dicarboxylic acid that occupies a pivotal position in cellular metabolism. As an intermediate in the citric acid (TCA) cycle, AKG is produced by isocitrate dehydrogenase from isocitrate and subsequently oxidized by the alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase complex (KGDHC) to succinyl-CoA, generating NADH and CO2 in the process. This reaction is one of three rate-limiting steps in the TCA cycle and a major site of metabolic regulation.</p>
<p>Beyond its energetic role, AKG is the primary carbon skeleton acceptor for amino groups in transamination reactions. Through glutamate dehydrogenase and various aminotransferases, AKG interconverts with glutamate, linking TCA cycle activity to amino acid metabolism, ammonia detoxification, and nitrogen balance. This nitrogen-shuttling function makes AKG essential for the urea cycle and for maintaining the glutamate/glutamine pool in the brain.</p>
<p>AKG is also a required cosubstrate for the large family of 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases (2-OGDDs), which includes prolyl and lysyl hydroxylases (collagen biosynthesis, HIF-1alpha regulation), TET DNA demethylases (epigenetic gene regulation), and JmjC-domain histone demethylases. These enzymes couple the oxidative decarboxylation of AKG to succinate with substrate hydroxylation or demethylation, directly linking cellular metabolic state to gene expression and extracellular matrix integrity.</p>
<h2>Metabolic Roles</h2>
<h3>TCA Cycle Intermediate</h3>
<p>AKG is formed in the TCA cycle by isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH), which catalyzes the oxidative decarboxylation of isocitrate. AKG is then oxidized by the alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase complex (KGDHC) — a large multienzyme complex homologous to the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex, requiring thiamine (B1), lipoic acid, CoA, FAD, and NAD+ as cofactors.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Produced by:</strong> Isocitrate + NAD+ → Alpha-ketoglutarate + CO2 + NADH (via IDH)</li>
<li><strong>Consumed by:</strong> Alpha-ketoglutarate + NAD+ + CoA → Succinyl-CoA + CO2 + NADH (via KGDHC)</li>
<li><strong>Regulation:</strong> KGDHC is inhibited by its products (succinyl-CoA, NADH) and by calcium activation. IDH is activated by ADP (energy demand signal) and inhibited by ATP and NADH.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Nitrogen Metabolism Hub</h3>
<p>AKG is the central carbon acceptor for amino groups:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Glutamate dehydrogenase:</strong> AKG + NH4+ + NAD(P)H ⇌ Glutamate + NAD(P)+ (reversible, links to urea cycle)</li>
<li><strong>Aminotransferases (ALT, AST):</strong> AKG + Amino acid ⇌ Glutamate + Alpha-keto acid (tissue-specific transamination)</li>
<li><strong>Clinical relevance:</strong> Elevated AKG or altered AKG/succinate ratios can indicate impaired KGDHC activity, seen in thiamine deficiency and neurodegenerative diseases.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Supplementation and Longevity</h2>
<h3>Animal Evidence</h3>
<p>Calcium alpha-ketoglutarate (CaAKG) supplementation extended lifespan and healthspan in C. elegans and mice. In a landmark 2020 study, AKG extended median lifespan by approximately 10% in mice, reducing frailty and improving inflammatory profiles. The mechanism appears to involve:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Inhibition of ATP synthase and TOR signaling:</strong> AKG extends lifespan by conserved mechanisms overlapping with caloric restriction</li>
<li><strong>Epigenetic rejuvenation:</strong> AKG supports TET-mediated DNA demethylation, potentially reversing age-related epigenetic drift</li>
<li><strong>Reduced inflammation:</strong> AKG supplementation decreases inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha) in aged animals</li>
<li><strong>Collagen support:</strong> As a prolyl/lysyl hydroxylase cosubstrate, AKG supports collagen synthesis and connective tissue integrity</li>
</ul>
<h3>Human Evidence</h3>
<p>Human data on AKG supplementation remains limited but promising:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Post-surgical recovery:</strong> Ornithine alpha-ketoglutarate (OKG) improves nitrogen balance and wound healing after surgery</li>
<li><strong>Athletic performance:</strong> Some evidence for reduced ammonia accumulation during exercise</li>
<li><strong>Bone health:</strong> AKG stimulates osteoblast differentiation and collagen production</li>
<li><strong>Gut health:</strong> AKG serves as an energy source for intestinal enterocytes</li>
</ul>
<h3>Dosing</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Longevity/healthspan (based on animal data extrapolation):</strong> 1-3 g/day of calcium alpha-ketoglutarate</li>
<li><strong>Athletic support:</strong> 2-5 g/day</li>
<li><strong>Post-surgical (OKG form):</strong> 10-15 g/day (split doses)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Thiamine (B1) deficiency:</strong> Impairs KGDHC (a B1-dependent enzyme), reducing AKG oxidation and causing accumulation of upstream metabolites. Contributes to Wernicke-Korsakoff encephalopathy.</li>
<li><strong>Neurodegeneration:</strong> Reduced KGDHC activity is one of the most consistent findings in Alzheimer's disease brain tissue, impairing AKG utilization and TCA cycle flux.</li>
<li><strong>Cancer metabolism:</strong> IDH mutations (IDH1 R132H, IDH2 R140Q) convert AKG to the oncometabolite D-2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG), which inhibits 2-OGDDs including TET enzymes and JmjC demethylases, driving epigenetic dysregulation.</li>
<li><strong>Surgical catabolism:</strong> OKG (ornithine alpha-ketoglutarate) is used clinically to preserve nitrogen balance in post-surgical and critically ill patients.</li>
</ul>
