<h1>Phosphocreatine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Phosphocreatine (PCr), also known as creatine phosphate, is a phosphorylated form of creatine that serves as the most rapidly mobilizable reserve of high-energy phosphates in vertebrate cells. It is concentrated predominantly in tissues with high and fluctuating energy demands — skeletal muscle (approximately 95% of the body's total creatine pool), myocardium, and brain. A 70 kg adult contains roughly 120 g of total creatine, with approximately 60% stored as phosphocreatine and 40% as free creatine.</p>
<p>The phosphocreatine-creatine kinase (PCr-CK) system functions as a spatial and temporal energy buffer. During rest, excess ATP phosphorylates creatine to form phosphocreatine. During intense activity, creatine kinase catalyzes the transfer of the high-energy phosphate from PCr to ADP, regenerating ATP within milliseconds — far faster than either glycolysis or oxidative phosphorylation. This system alone sustains maximal muscular effort for approximately 5-8 seconds and is critical for activities requiring explosive power output.</p>
<p>Beyond its role as an immediate energy buffer, phosphocreatine participates in intracellular energy transport via the creatine kinase phosphotransfer network, shuttling high-energy phosphates from mitochondria (sites of ATP production) to sites of ATP utilization such as myofibrils, ion pumps, and synaptic terminals. The human body recycles its entire body weight in ATP each day through this phosphocreatine-mediated system.</p>
<h2>Biosynthesis and Metabolism</h2>
<p>Phosphocreatine is formed intracellularly when creatine kinase phosphorylates creatine using ATP. Creatine itself is synthesized endogenously in a two-step process: first, arginine and glycine are converted to guanidinoacetate (GAA) by AGAT (arginine:glycine amidinotransferase) primarily in the kidneys; second, GAA is methylated by GAMT (guanidinoacetate N-methyltransferase) in the liver using S-adenosylmethionine as the methyl donor. The resulting creatine is transported via blood to muscle, brain, and heart, where it is phosphorylated to phosphocreatine.</p>
<p>Approximately 1-2% of the total creatine pool is broken down non-enzymatically to creatinine each day and excreted in urine, necessitating continuous replenishment through diet (red meat, fish) or de novo synthesis. This turnover rate is the basis for using serum creatinine as a marker of renal function and muscle mass.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<h3>Cardiovascular</h3>
<p>Elevated creatine kinase (CK-MB isoenzyme) in blood plasma is a diagnostic marker of myocardial infarction, reflecting release from damaged cardiac muscle cells where the phosphocreatine system is essential for maintaining cardiac contractility. Intravenous phosphocreatine (marketed as Neoton in some countries) has been used clinically for cardiovascular protection during cardiac surgery and in the management of heart failure, though this use is not universal.</p>
<h3>Muscular</h3>
<p>Phosphocreatine depletion is a primary factor limiting performance during short-duration, high-intensity exercise. The rate of PCr resynthesis during recovery correlates with athletic recovery capacity and is used in exercise physiology as a measure of mitochondrial function via 31P-MRS (phosphorus magnetic resonance spectroscopy).</p>
<h3>Neurological</h3>
<p>The brain, which consumes approximately 20% of the body's ATP despite representing only 2% of body weight, relies heavily on the phosphocreatine system for maintaining neuronal ion gradients, neurotransmitter cycling, and synaptic transmission. Impairments in brain phosphocreatine metabolism have been associated with neurodegenerative conditions, traumatic brain injury, and cognitive decline.</p>
<h2>Measurement</h2>
<p>Phosphocreatine levels are assessed non-invasively using 31P-MRS (phosphorus magnetic resonance spectroscopy), which can quantify PCr, ATP, and inorganic phosphate in real time in muscle, heart, and brain tissue. Resting skeletal muscle PCr concentrations are typically 20-30 mmol/kg dry weight. Serum creatine kinase (CK) activity serves as an indirect biomarker of tissue PCr-CK system integrity and muscle damage.</p>
