<h1>C-Peptide</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>C-Peptide (connecting peptide) is a 31-amino acid polypeptide formed when proinsulin is enzymatically cleaved into insulin and C-peptide in the secretory granules of pancreatic beta cells. Both molecules are released into the portal circulation in equimolar amounts, but their subsequent fates differ substantially. Insulin undergoes significant first-pass extraction by the liver (40-60%), while C-peptide passes through the liver unchanged and is cleared primarily by the kidneys with a half-life of approximately 30 minutes versus insulin's 3-5 minutes.</p>
<p>These pharmacokinetic differences make C-peptide a more accurate and stable measure of endogenous insulin secretion than measuring insulin itself. In clinical practice, C-peptide is essential for differentiating type 1 from type 2 diabetes, assessing residual beta-cell function, evaluating hypoglycemia etiology, and monitoring pancreatic function after islet cell transplantation. It is also used to distinguish endogenous from exogenous hyperinsulinemia, as patients receiving exogenous insulin therapy will have low C-peptide despite high insulin levels.</p>
<p>C-peptide was historically considered biologically inert, but emerging evidence suggests it has physiological roles including improvement of microvascular blood flow, reduction of glomerular hyperfiltration, and potential neuroprotective effects. C-peptide replacement in type 1 diabetes patients has shown modest improvements in microvascular complications in some studies, though this remains an active area of research.</p>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase C-Peptide:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome (compensatory hyperinsulinemia)</li>
<li>Type 2 diabetes (early to mid-stage with active beta-cell compensation)</li>
<li>Insulinoma (autonomous beta-cell tumor producing excess insulin and C-peptide)</li>
<li>Renal impairment (reduced renal clearance of C-peptide)</li>
<li>Obesity (increased insulin demand drives higher beta-cell output)</li>
<li>Pregnancy (physiological insulin resistance increases beta-cell secretion)</li>
<li>Sulfonylurea medications (stimulate insulin and C-peptide secretion)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease C-Peptide:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Type 1 diabetes (autoimmune beta-cell destruction)</li>
<li>Late-stage type 2 diabetes (beta-cell exhaustion after years of insulin resistance)</li>
<li>Exogenous insulin therapy (suppression of endogenous secretion via feedback)</li>
<li>Pancreatic surgery or pancreatectomy (reduced beta-cell mass)</li>
<li>Pancreatitis and pancreatic insufficiency (damage to islet cells)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Type 2 Diabetes</strong> -- fasting C-peptide &gt; 2.0 ng/mL indicates residual beta-cell function and insulin resistance; levels help guide treatment decisions between insulin-sensitizing agents and insulin therapy (PMID: 18442638)</li>
<li><strong>Insulin Resistance</strong> -- elevated C-peptide with concurrent hyperglycemia indicates compensatory hyperinsulinemia; correlates with HOMA-IR and predicts progression to type 2 diabetes</li>
<li><strong>Insulinoma</strong> -- inappropriately elevated C-peptide (&gt; 2.5 ng/mL) during hypoglycemia is diagnostic; distinguishes from exogenous insulin administration (low C-peptide)</li>
<li><strong>Renal Disease</strong> -- C-peptide accumulates in CKD; levels must be interpreted with eGFR</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Type 1 Diabetes</strong> -- C-peptide &lt; 0.3 ng/mL (fasting) or &lt; 0.6 ng/mL (stimulated) confirms beta-cell failure; partial remission (&quot;honeymoon phase&quot;) may show transiently higher values</li>
<li><strong>Late-Stage Type 2 Diabetes</strong> -- declining C-peptide indicates beta-cell exhaustion; signals need for insulin therapy initiation</li>
<li><strong>Pancreatic Insufficiency</strong> -- reduced beta-cell mass from chronic pancreatitis or cystic fibrosis</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Differentiating type 1 from type 2 diabetes at diagnosis</li>
<li>Assessing residual beta-cell function for insulin therapy decisions</li>
<li>Evaluating cause of hypoglycemia (endogenous vs. exogenous insulin)</li>
<li>Monitoring islet cell transplant function</li>
<li>Predicting diabetes complications risk (higher C-peptide in type 1 associated with fewer microvascular complications)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Preserve beta-cell function</strong> -- Early intensive glycemic control reduces glucotoxicity, the primary driver of beta-cell apoptosis. Target HbA1c &lt; 7% within the first year of diagnosis to preserve C-peptide production.</li>
<li><strong>Berberine supplementation</strong> -- 500 mg 2-3 times daily has been shown to increase C-peptide levels in type 2 diabetes, suggesting improved beta-cell function via AMPK activation and insulin sensitization (PMID: 18442638).</li>
<li><strong>Chromium supplementation</strong> -- 200-1000 mcg/day of chromium picolinate may enhance insulin sensitivity and modestly improve beta-cell function in type 2 diabetes (PMID: 17109600).</li>
<li><strong>Vitamin D optimization</strong> -- Maintaining serum 25(OH)D &gt; 40 ng/mL supports beta-cell function through vitamin D receptors on pancreatic islets; deficiency is associated with reduced insulin secretion (PMID: 23328785).</li>
<li><strong>Weight management and exercise</strong> -- 5-10% body weight reduction and regular moderate exercise (150 minutes/week) improve insulin sensitivity, reducing demand on beta cells and preserving C-peptide production over time.</li>
<li><strong>Low glycemic index diet</strong> -- Minimizing postprandial glucose spikes reduces glucotoxic stress on beta cells, preserving their capacity for endogenous insulin secretion.</li>
</ol>
