<h1>Blood NfL</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Neurofilament light chain (NfL) is a 68 kDa cytoskeletal protein abundantly expressed in large-caliber myelinated axons of the central and peripheral nervous system. When axons are damaged by injury, inflammation, or degeneration, NfL is released into the interstitial fluid, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), and ultimately the blood. The development of ultrasensitive single molecule array (Simoa) technology by Quanterix enabled reliable detection of NfL in serum and plasma at sub-picogram concentrations, transforming it from a CSF-only research marker into a practical blood-based biomarker.</p>
<p>Blood NfL serves as a disease-agnostic indicator of neuroaxonal injury. It is elevated across a remarkably broad range of neurological conditions, including multiple sclerosis (MS), amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), Alzheimer's disease (AD), frontotemporal dementia (FTD), Parkinson's disease (PD), Huntington's disease (HD), stroke, and traumatic brain injury (TBI). In MS, blood NfL levels correlate with relapse activity, gadolinium-enhancing lesions on MRI, brain atrophy rate, and disability progression, making it a powerful tool for monitoring treatment response.</p>
<p>Blood NfL has limitations: levels increase with normal aging (approximately 2-3% per year after age 40), are influenced by body mass index and renal function, and are not specific to any single disease. It is most useful as a prognostic and monitoring biomarker rather than a diagnostic one, and should be interpreted alongside clinical context and neuroimaging findings.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Reference ranges are assay-dependent. Values above assume Simoa (Quanterix) platform.</li>
<li>BMI and renal function affect blood NfL levels — higher BMI is associated with slightly lower values; impaired renal clearance increases values.</li>
<li>No universally standardized clinical cutoff exists; percentile-based reference values and disease-specific thresholds are evolving.</li>
<li>Intra-individual biological variation is approximately 10-15%, making serial monitoring feasible with appropriate intervals (3-6 months).</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase Blood NfL:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Normal aging (progressive increase from age 40, approximately 2-3% per year)</li>
<li>Multiple sclerosis relapses and active lesion formation (acute neuroaxonal injury)</li>
<li>ALS and other motor neuron diseases (severe, progressive axonal degeneration)</li>
<li>Alzheimer's disease and other dementias (chronic neurodegeneration)</li>
<li>Traumatic brain injury (mechanical axonal shearing)</li>
<li>Stroke (ischemic and hemorrhagic neuronal damage)</li>
<li>Spinal cord injury (axonal disruption)</li>
<li>Cerebral small vessel disease (chronic white matter damage)</li>
<li>High-intensity exercise (transient mild increase lasting hours)</li>
<li>Renal impairment (reduced clearance of NfL from circulation)</li>
<li>Low body mass index (less dilutional effect)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease Blood NfL:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Effective disease-modifying treatment in MS (e.g., natalizumab, ocrelizumab)</li>
<li>Disease stability or remission in neurodegenerative conditions</li>
<li>Higher body mass index (dilutional effect)</li>
<li>Regular moderate exercise (neuroprotective effect over time)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Multiple Sclerosis</strong> -- blood NfL predicts relapse activity, new MRI lesions, brain atrophy, and disability progression; levels drop within 3-6 months of effective DMT initiation (PMID: 30242207)</li>
<li><strong>Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis</strong> -- among the strongest biomarkers for ALS; levels correlate with disease progression rate and survival; baseline NfL &gt; 50 pg/mL suggests rapidly progressive disease (PMID: 31015300)</li>
<li><strong>Alzheimer's Disease</strong> -- elevated even in preclinical stages; predicts cognitive decline rate and progression from mild cognitive impairment to dementia (PMID: 31061527)</li>
<li><strong>Frontotemporal Dementia</strong> -- among the highest NfL levels of any neurodegenerative disease; useful for differentiating FTD from psychiatric mimics (PMID: 29070667)</li>
<li><strong>Parkinson's Disease</strong> -- elevated in PD with cognitive decline and dementia; helps identify patients at risk for progression (PMID: 31118248)</li>
<li><strong>Huntington's Disease</strong> -- levels correlate with disease stage, CAG repeat length, and brain atrophy; useful as a progression biomarker in clinical trials (PMID: 30578220)</li>
<li><strong>Stroke</strong> -- acutely elevated within hours; peak levels predict infarct volume and functional outcome (PMID: 30355010)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Neurological stability and effective treatment response</li>
<li>Favorable prognosis in MS and ALS</li>
<li>Lower rates of brain atrophy on MRI</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Tracking disease-modifying treatment response in MS</li>
<li>Stratifying progression risk in ALS and FTD</li>
<li>Screening for preclinical neurodegeneration in at-risk populations</li>
<li>Monitoring recovery after traumatic brain injury</li>
<li>Serving as an outcome measure in neurodegenerative disease clinical trials</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Disease-modifying treatment</strong> -- In MS, initiating high-efficacy DMTs (e.g., natalizumab, ocrelizumab, ofatumumab) reduces blood NfL by 40-60% within 3-6 months. Timely treatment initiation is the most impactful intervention.</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation</strong> -- 1-2 g/day of EPA/DHA has been associated with reduced neuroaxonal injury markers in observational studies, potentially through anti-inflammatory and membrane-stabilizing effects (PMID: 29771296).</li>
<li><strong>Vitamin D optimization</strong> -- Maintaining serum 25(OH)D levels above 40 ng/mL may reduce NfL in MS patients with vitamin D deficiency, likely via immunomodulatory mechanisms (PMID: 31542602).</li>
<li><strong>Regular exercise</strong> -- Moderate aerobic exercise (150 minutes/week) has neuroprotective effects, reduces neuroinflammation, and may lower baseline NfL over time through BDNF-mediated axonal resilience.</li>
<li><strong>Curcumin supplementation</strong> -- 500-1000 mg/day of bioavailable curcumin formulations may reduce neuroinflammation and oxidative damage to axons (PMID: 30513703).</li>
<li><strong>Sleep optimization</strong> -- Adequate sleep (7-9 hours) supports glymphatic clearance of neurotoxic waste products and reduces neuroinflammatory burden.</li>
</ol>
