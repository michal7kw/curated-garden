<h1>Blood Urea Nitrogen</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) quantifies the nitrogen component of urea in circulating blood. Urea is synthesized in the liver through the urea cycle as the primary end product of amino acid catabolism, then transported via the bloodstream to the kidneys where it is freely filtered at the glomerulus. Approximately 40-60% of filtered urea is reabsorbed in the renal tubules, with the remainder excreted in urine. Because this process depends on both hepatic synthesis and renal clearance, BUN reflects the interplay between protein metabolism, liver function, and kidney function.</p>
<p>BUN is most commonly interpreted alongside serum creatinine to calculate the BUN-to-creatinine ratio (BCR), which helps distinguish prerenal from intrinsic renal causes of elevated values. A BCR greater than 20:1 typically suggests a prerenal cause (dehydration, heart failure, GI bleeding), while a ratio near 10-15:1 points toward intrinsic kidney disease. BUN is routinely included in both the comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP) and basic metabolic panel (BMP), making it one of the most frequently ordered laboratory tests.</p>
<p>BUN has important limitations: it is influenced by non-renal factors including dietary protein intake, hydration status, corticosteroid use, and gastrointestinal bleeding. It is less specific for kidney injury than creatinine or cystatin C and should always be interpreted in clinical context with these complementary markers.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Normal ranges may vary slightly by laboratory (typically 6-24 mg/dL or 7-20 mg/dL).</li>
<li>Age-related increases are common; elderly patients may have mildly elevated BUN due to reduced renal blood flow.</li>
<li>BUN is measured in mg/dL in the United States; in many countries, serum urea is reported in mmol/L (multiply BUN by 0.357 to convert).</li>
<li>The BUN-to-creatinine ratio (normal 10:1 to 20:1) is critical for differential diagnosis.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase BUN:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>High dietary protein intake (increased amino acid catabolism and urea production)</li>
<li>Dehydration (reduced renal perfusion and increased tubular reabsorption of urea)</li>
<li>Congestive heart failure (reduced renal blood flow, prerenal azotemia)</li>
<li>Gastrointestinal bleeding (digested blood proteins increase urea production)</li>
<li>Corticosteroid and tetracycline use (increased protein catabolism)</li>
<li>Urinary tract obstruction (impaired urea excretion)</li>
<li>Advanced age (natural decline in glomerular filtration rate)</li>
<li>Creatine supplementation (increased nitrogen load from creatine metabolism)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease BUN:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Liver disease or liver failure (impaired urea cycle function)</li>
<li>Malnutrition or low-protein diet (reduced substrate for urea synthesis)</li>
<li>Overhydration (dilutional effect)</li>
<li>Pregnancy (increased glomerular filtration rate and plasma volume)</li>
<li>Syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion (SIADH)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Chronic Kidney Disease</strong> -- progressive decline in GFR leads to urea accumulation; BUN rises in stages 3-5 CKD alongside creatinine (PMID: 10694992)</li>
<li><strong>Acute Kidney Injury</strong> -- rapid rise in BUN within hours to days; serial monitoring helps track injury progression and recovery (PMID: 19168795)</li>
<li><strong>Heart Failure</strong> -- reduced cardiac output decreases renal perfusion, causing prerenal azotemia with BCR &gt; 20:1 (PMID: 15522038)</li>
<li><strong>Diabetic Kidney Disease</strong> -- chronic hyperglycemia damages glomeruli, progressively impairing urea clearance (PMID: 14506253)</li>
<li><strong>Liver Disease</strong> -- paradoxically, severe liver disease can lower BUN due to impaired urea cycle function</li>
<li><strong>Gastrointestinal Bleeding</strong> -- digested blood acts as a protein load, elevating BUN disproportionately to creatinine (BCR &gt; 30:1)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Liver failure or severe hepatic insufficiency (impaired urea synthesis)</li>
<li>Malnutrition, starvation, or very low protein diets</li>
<li>Overhydration and SIADH (dilutional effect)</li>
<li>Normal pregnancy (physiological increase in GFR)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Routine kidney function screening in annual physicals</li>
<li>Monitoring disease progression in CKD (stages 1-5)</li>
<li>Assessing hydration status in hospitalized patients</li>
<li>Evaluating dialysis adequacy (BUN reduction ratio)</li>
<li>Monitoring patients on nephrotoxic medications</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Adequate hydration</strong> -- Maintaining proper fluid intake (2-3 L/day for most adults) supports renal perfusion and urea clearance. Dehydration is the most common reversible cause of elevated BUN.</li>
<li><strong>Moderate protein intake</strong> -- For patients with CKD, a moderate protein diet (0.8-1.0 g/kg/day) reduces nitrogen load without causing malnutrition. Very high protein diets (&gt;2.0 g/kg/day) can elevate BUN in healthy individuals.</li>
<li><strong>Blood pressure and glycemic control</strong> -- Managing hypertension and diabetes protects remaining renal function, preventing progressive BUN elevation. Target BP &lt; 130/80 mmHg and HbA1c &lt; 7% in diabetic kidney disease.</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation</strong> -- 1-2 g/day of EPA/DHA may reduce inflammation and slow CKD progression, modestly improving urea clearance (PMID: 18541578).</li>
<li><strong>NAC supplementation</strong> -- N-acetylcysteine (600-1200 mg/day) has shown renoprotective effects in some studies, potentially supporting tubular function (PMID: 16825567).</li>
<li><strong>Avoid nephrotoxic agents</strong> -- Minimize use of NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, and contrast media when possible; monitor BUN closely when these agents are necessary.</li>
</ol>
