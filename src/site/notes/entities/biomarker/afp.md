<h1>AFP</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) is a 70 kDa glycoprotein produced primarily by the fetal liver and yolk sac during embryonic development. It is the dominant serum protein in early fetal life (analogous to albumin in adults) and normally declines to very low levels after birth. In healthy non-pregnant adults, serum AFP is typically below 10 ng/mL.</p>
<p>Elevated AFP in non-pregnant adults is clinically significant as a tumor marker. It is most strongly associated with hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) and non-seminomatous germ cell tumors (particularly yolk sac tumors and embryonal carcinoma). AFP, when combined with hepatic imaging, is the cornerstone of HCC surveillance in at-risk populations (cirrhosis, chronic hepatitis B/C). Levels above 400 ng/mL in the appropriate clinical context are considered diagnostic for HCC.</p>
<p>In prenatal medicine, maternal serum AFP is measured as part of the triple/quad screen at 15-20 weeks gestation. Elevated levels may indicate neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly), while low levels are associated with chromosomal abnormalities (Down syndrome). However, this biomarker note focuses on AFP as an adult tumor marker rather than prenatal screening applications.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Normal levels do not exclude HCC; approximately 20% of HCCs are AFP-negative</li>
<li>Chronic hepatitis and cirrhosis can cause moderate elevations (10-200 ng/mL) without malignancy</li>
<li>AFP is less sensitive for early-stage HCC (40-65% sensitivity) than for advanced HCC (80-90%)</li>
<li>Combined AFP + imaging (ultrasound every 6 months) is the recommended surveillance strategy</li>
<li>In germ cell tumors, AFP is elevated in yolk sac tumors and embryonal carcinoma but not pure seminoma</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase AFP:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Hepatocellular carcinoma (most clinically significant cause in non-pregnant adults)</li>
<li>Non-seminomatous germ cell tumors (yolk sac tumor, embryonal carcinoma)</li>
<li>Chronic hepatitis B and C (acute flares can elevate AFP to 100-400 ng/mL)</li>
<li>Cirrhosis (regenerating liver nodules produce AFP)</li>
<li>Hepatic adenoma</li>
<li>Ataxia-telangiectasia (genetically elevated AFP)</li>
<li>Pregnancy (physiological elevation; measured as part of prenatal screening)</li>
<li>Hereditary persistence of AFP (rare genetic condition)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease AFP:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Effective HCC treatment (surgical resection, transplant, locoregional therapy)</li>
<li>Successful germ cell tumor treatment</li>
<li>Resolution of acute hepatitis</li>
<li>Age (AFP declines from high fetal levels to adult baseline within first year of life)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated AFP indicates:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Hepatocellular carcinoma</strong> -- AFP is elevated in 60-80% of HCC patients; serial rise is more diagnostic than a single elevation. Used with ultrasound for HCC surveillance per AASLD and EASL guidelines</li>
<li><strong>Germ cell tumors</strong> -- AFP is a staging and monitoring marker for non-seminomatous germ cell tumors; serial measurements track treatment response. Combined with beta-hCG and LDH for IGCCCG prognostic classification</li>
<li><strong>Chronic liver disease</strong> -- Moderate elevations (10-200 ng/mL) occur in 20-40% of cirrhosis patients due to regenerative activity; must be distinguished from malignant elevation through imaging</li>
<li><strong>Prenatal screening</strong> -- Elevated maternal serum AFP suggests neural tube defects; low AFP suggests chromosomal abnormalities (measured as part of quad screen)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Limitations:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Not sufficiently sensitive or specific for population-level cancer screening</li>
<li>False positives from benign liver disease are common</li>
<li>20-30% of early HCCs are AFP-negative (necessitating imaging-based surveillance)</li>
<li>New biomarkers like AFP-L3% and DCP (PIVKA-II) improve specificity when combined with total AFP</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>HCC surveillance in cirrhotic patients (every 6 months with ultrasound)</li>
<li>Germ cell tumor staging and treatment response monitoring</li>
<li>Post-treatment recurrence surveillance for HCC and germ cell tumors</li>
<li>Chronic hepatitis activity assessment</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Manage Elevated AFP</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>HCC surveillance</strong> -- Patients with cirrhosis or chronic hepatitis B should undergo AFP + ultrasound every 6 months per AASLD guidelines</li>
<li><strong>Imaging workup</strong> -- Any persistently elevated AFP (&gt;10 ng/mL) in a cirrhotic patient warrants contrast-enhanced CT or MRI to evaluate for HCC</li>
<li><strong>AFP-L3% and DCP (PIVKA-II)</strong> -- Second-line biomarkers that improve HCC detection specificity when total AFP is borderline</li>
<li><strong>Treat underlying liver disease</strong> -- Antiviral therapy for hepatitis B/C reduces HCC risk and may normalize AFP if elevation is from inflammatory activity</li>
<li><strong>Germ cell tumor protocol</strong> -- AFP + beta-hCG + LDH for staging; serial monitoring every 2-4 weeks during chemotherapy to assess treatment response</li>
<li><strong>Avoid alcohol and hepatotoxins</strong> -- Reduces chronic liver injury and regenerative AFP production</li>
</ol>
