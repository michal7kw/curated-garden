<h1>BNP</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>B-type Natriuretic Peptide (BNP) is a 32-amino-acid peptide hormone produced primarily by ventricular cardiomyocytes. It is synthesized as a 108-amino-acid prohormone (proBNP) that is cleaved into the biologically active BNP and the inactive N-terminal fragment NT-proBNP. Release is triggered by myocardial wall stretch from volume or pressure overload, and the peptide acts to promote natriuresis, vasodilation, and inhibition of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), serving as a natural counter-regulatory response to cardiac stress.</p>
<p>BNP and NT-proBNP are the gold-standard blood biomarkers for the diagnosis and exclusion of heart failure. In the emergency setting, a BNP below 100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP below age-stratified cutoffs effectively rules out heart failure as the cause of acute dyspnea (negative predictive value &gt; 95%). Beyond diagnosis, serial BNP measurements guide therapy optimization, predict hospitalization risk, and correlate with mortality in both heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) and preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF).</p>
<p>Important limitations exist: BNP is physiologically elevated by age, female sex, and renal impairment, and is paradoxically lowered by obesity (due to increased clearance via expanded adipose tissue natriuretic peptide receptor C expression). NT-proBNP is primarily cleared renally and is more affected by kidney dysfunction than BNP. Both assays are included in current ESC and ACC/AHA heart failure guidelines as Class I recommendations for acute dyspnea evaluation.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>BNP has a half-life of approximately 20 minutes; NT-proBNP has a half-life of 60-120 minutes, making it more stable analytically.</li>
<li>Obesity reduces BNP/NT-proBNP by approximately 20-40% due to enhanced clearance. Use higher clinical suspicion in obese patients.</li>
<li>Renal impairment (eGFR &lt; 60) elevates both markers, especially NT-proBNP.</li>
<li>Atrial fibrillation independently elevates BNP by approximately 30-50% regardless of ventricular function.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase BNP:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Heart failure (ventricular wall stretch from volume/pressure overload)</li>
<li>Acute coronary syndromes (myocardial ischemia and necrosis)</li>
<li>Pulmonary hypertension (right ventricular pressure overload)</li>
<li>Atrial fibrillation (atrial stretch and neurohormonal activation)</li>
<li>Renal impairment (reduced clearance, especially for NT-proBNP)</li>
<li>Advanced age (progressive myocardial stiffness and reduced clearance)</li>
<li>Female sex (higher baseline natriuretic peptide levels)</li>
<li>Pulmonary embolism (acute right ventricular strain)</li>
<li>Cirrhosis with ascites (central volume overload)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease BNP:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Obesity (enhanced clearance via NPR-C receptors on adipocytes)</li>
<li>Effective heart failure treatment (ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, SGLT2 inhibitors, ARNI)</li>
<li>Flash pulmonary edema treated acutely (rapid reduction after diuresis)</li>
<li>Low body weight and cachexia</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Heart Failure</strong> -- the primary diagnostic application; BNP &gt; 400 pg/mL has &gt;90% positive predictive value for heart failure in patients with dyspnea; serial monitoring predicts hospitalization and mortality (PMID: 12074125)</li>
<li><strong>Acute Coronary Syndrome</strong> -- BNP rises within hours of myocardial infarction and predicts adverse outcomes, left ventricular dysfunction, and mortality at 30 days and 1 year (PMID: 11524443)</li>
<li><strong>Pulmonary Hypertension</strong> -- reflects right ventricular strain and correlates with disease severity and survival (PMID: 16601037)</li>
<li><strong>Atrial Fibrillation</strong> -- elevated BNP in AF predicts progression to permanent AF, stroke risk, and response to cardioversion (PMID: 17045829)</li>
<li><strong>Coronary Artery Disease</strong> -- elevated BNP in stable CAD identifies patients at higher risk for future cardiovascular events (PMID: 14656941)</li>
<li><strong>Chronic Kidney Disease</strong> -- BNP is elevated in CKD due to reduced clearance and comorbid cardiac disease; interpretation requires renal function context (PMID: 15522038)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Effective heart failure therapy (target: BNP reduction &gt; 30% from baseline)</li>
<li>Low cardiovascular risk</li>
<li>Obesity (falsely low — do not interpret as reassurance in obese patients)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Guiding heart failure treatment optimization (titration of GDMT)</li>
<li>Predicting heart failure hospitalization and cardiovascular mortality</li>
<li>Evaluating response to cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT)</li>
<li>Screening for early cardiac dysfunction in chemotherapy patients</li>
<li>Risk stratification prior to non-cardiac surgery</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT)</strong> -- The most effective way to reduce elevated BNP is treating the underlying heart failure. Initiation of ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan), SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin), beta-blockers, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists reduces BNP by 30-60% and improves outcomes.</li>
<li><strong>Coenzyme Q10 supplementation</strong> -- 100-300 mg/day of ubiquinol has been shown in the Q-SYMBIO trial to reduce BNP levels and improve ejection fraction in heart failure patients (PMID: 16887893).</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation</strong> -- 1 g/day of EPA/DHA may modestly reduce BNP and inflammatory markers in heart failure patients through anti-inflammatory and membrane-stabilizing effects (PMID: 18633135).</li>
<li><strong>Thiamine supplementation</strong> -- 200-300 mg/day is recommended for heart failure patients on chronic loop diuretics, who are at risk for thiamine depletion; repletion has been shown to reduce BNP and improve cardiac function (PMID: 16887893).</li>
<li><strong>Sodium and fluid restriction</strong> -- Limiting sodium to &lt; 2 g/day and fluid to 1.5-2 L/day reduces volume overload, ventricular wall stretch, and BNP levels in decompensated heart failure.</li>
<li><strong>Regular exercise</strong> -- Moderate aerobic exercise (150 minutes/week) improves functional capacity and has been shown to modestly reduce BNP in stable heart failure patients through improved cardiac efficiency.</li>
</ol>
