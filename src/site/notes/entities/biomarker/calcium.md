<h1>Calcium</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the human body, with approximately 99% stored in bones and teeth and the remaining 1% distributed in extracellular fluid, cells, and blood. Serum calcium exists in three forms: ionized (free, ~50%, biologically active), protein-bound (~40%, primarily to albumin), and complexed to anions (~10%, bicarbonate, phosphate, citrate). Total serum calcium reflects all three fractions and is the most commonly measured form, though ionized calcium is more accurate in settings of acid-base disturbance or hypoalbuminemia.</p>
<p>Calcium homeostasis is maintained by the integrated actions of parathyroid hormone (PTH), 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol), and calcitonin. PTH raises serum calcium by stimulating osteoclastic bone resorption, enhancing renal tubular calcium reabsorption, and promoting renal activation of vitamin D. Calcitriol increases intestinal calcium absorption. Calcitonin provides a counter-regulatory brake by inhibiting osteoclasts. Disruption of any of these axes produces clinically significant hypo- or hypercalcemia.</p>
<p>Serum calcium is a standard component of the comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP) and is routinely assessed in the evaluation of bone disease, kidney stones, parathyroid disorders, malignancy, and critically ill patients. Because total calcium is albumin-dependent, corrected calcium (adjusted for albumin) or ionized calcium should be used when albumin is abnormal, using the formula: Corrected Ca = Measured Ca + 0.8 x (4.0 - Albumin).</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Ionized calcium reference range: 4.6-5.3 mg/dL (1.15-1.33 mmol/L) — more accurate in hypoalbuminemia.</li>
<li>Corrected calcium formula: Corrected Ca = Measured Ca + 0.8 x (4.0 - serum albumin in g/dL).</li>
<li>Slight age-related increase; postmenopausal women may have marginally higher values due to increased bone resorption.</li>
<li>Values should be interpreted with concurrent albumin, PTH, phosphorus, and creatinine.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase Calcium:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Primary hyperparathyroidism (most common cause of outpatient hypercalcemia)</li>
<li>Malignancy — PTHrP secretion, osteolytic bone metastases, humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy</li>
<li>Thiazide diuretics (enhanced renal tubular calcium reabsorption)</li>
<li>Vitamin D excess / intoxication (increased intestinal absorption and bone resorption)</li>
<li>Granulomatous diseases — sarcoidosis, tuberculosis (macrophage 1-alpha-hydroxylase activation)</li>
<li>Prolonged immobilization (disuse osteoporosis and bone resorption)</li>
<li>Milk-alkali syndrome (excessive calcium and absorbable antacid intake)</li>
<li>Dehydration (hemoconcentration)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease Calcium:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Hypoalbuminemia (reduces total but not ionized calcium; use corrected calcium)</li>
<li>Vitamin D deficiency (reduced intestinal absorption and bone mobilization)</li>
<li>Chronic kidney disease (reduced calcitriol production, secondary hyperparathyroidism)</li>
<li>Hypoparathyroidism (post-surgical, autoimmune, genetic)</li>
<li>Acute pancreatitis (saponification of calcium in peripancreatic fat)</li>
<li>Hyperphosphatemia (precipitates calcium-phosphate complexes)</li>
<li>Magnesium deficiency (impairs PTH secretion and action)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Primary Hyperparathyroidism</strong> -- the most common cause of hypercalcemia in ambulatory settings; typically mild elevation (10.5-12.0 mg/dL) with concurrent low phosphorus and elevated PTH (PMID: 28536364)</li>
<li><strong>Malignancy</strong> -- the most common cause of inpatient hypercalcemia; mediated by PTHrP secretion or osteolytic metastases; levels &gt; 13 mg/dL suggest malignancy (PMID: 21149359)</li>
<li><strong>Chronic Kidney Disease</strong> -- secondary hyperparathyroidism from hypocalcemia drives elevated PTH; calcium may be low, normal, or elevated depending on stage and treatment</li>
<li><strong>Osteoporosis</strong> -- chronic elevated calcium from bone resorption may contribute to bone density loss</li>
<li><strong>Kidney Stones</strong> -- hypercalciuria from hypercalcemia increases calcium oxalate and calcium phosphate stone risk</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Vitamin D Deficiency</strong> -- insufficient calcitriol reduces intestinal calcium absorption, leading to secondary hyperparathyroidism and hypocalcemia in severe cases</li>
<li><strong>Hypoparathyroidism</strong> -- post-surgical (thyroidectomy, parathyroidectomy) or autoimmune; characterized by low calcium, high phosphorus, and low PTH</li>
<li><strong>Acute Pancreatitis</strong> -- peripancreatic fat necrosis saponifies calcium, producing transient hypocalcemia</li>
<li><strong>CKD-MBD (Chronic Kidney Disease-Mineral and Bone Disorder)</strong> -- reduced calcitriol synthesis, phosphate retention, and secondary hyperparathyroidism</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Routine metabolic screening (via CMP) in annual physicals</li>
<li>Monitoring hyperparathyroidism (pre- and post-surgical)</li>
<li>Assessing bone health in osteoporosis management</li>
<li>Evaluating malignancy-associated hypercalcemia</li>
<li>Monitoring calcium in CKD patients on vitamin D analogs or phosphate binders</li>
<li>Pre- and post-thyroidectomy/parathyroidectomy surveillance</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Vitamin D optimization</strong> -- Maintaining serum 25(OH)D levels of 40-60 ng/mL ensures adequate intestinal calcium absorption (10-15% without vitamin D vs. 30-40% with sufficient vitamin D). Supplement with 2000-5000 IU/day as needed (PMID: 28536364).</li>
<li><strong>Adequate dietary calcium</strong> -- 1000-1200 mg/day from food sources (dairy, leafy greens, fortified foods) is preferred over supplements. Excessive supplementation (&gt; 1500 mg/day) is associated with increased cardiovascular risk without additional bone benefit.</li>
<li><strong>Magnesium supplementation</strong> -- 200-400 mg/day of elemental magnesium supports PTH secretion and action; magnesium deficiency causes functional hypoparathyroidism and impairs vitamin D activation (PMID: 26444592).</li>
<li><strong>Vitamin K2 supplementation</strong> -- 100-200 mcg/day of MK-7 activates osteocalcin (directs calcium to bone) and matrix Gla protein (prevents vascular calcification), improving calcium utilization (PMID: 21149359).</li>
<li><strong>Weight-bearing exercise</strong> -- 150 minutes/week of weight-bearing and resistance training stimulates osteoblast activity, improves bone mineral density, and supports healthy calcium deposition in bone.</li>
<li><strong>Address underlying causes</strong> -- Hypercalcemia requires treating the cause (parathyroidectomy for primary hyperparathyroidism, hydration and bisphosphonates for malignancy). Hypocalcemia requires addressing vitamin D deficiency, magnesium deficiency, or hypoparathyroidism.</li>
</ol>
