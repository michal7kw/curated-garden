<h1>Alanine Transaminase</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Alanine Transaminase (ALT), also known as serum glutamic-pyruvic transaminase (SGPT), is a pyridoxal phosphate-dependent enzyme concentrated in hepatocyte cytoplasm. It catalyzes the reversible transfer of an amino group from alanine to alpha-ketoglutarate, producing pyruvate and glutamate -- a key step linking amino acid metabolism to the citric acid cycle.</p>
<p>ALT is the most liver-specific serum enzyme marker because it is found predominantly in the liver (with much smaller amounts in kidney, heart, and skeletal muscle). When hepatocyte membranes are damaged or become more permeable, ALT leaks into the sinusoidal blood and systemic circulation, producing measurable serum elevations within 6-12 hours of injury. This makes ALT more specific for liver injury than AST (aspartate aminotransferase), which is also abundant in cardiac and skeletal muscle.</p>
<p>The AST/ALT ratio (De Ritis ratio) provides additional diagnostic information: ratios &gt;2.0 suggest alcoholic liver disease or cirrhosis, while ratios &lt;1.0 are typical of viral hepatitis and NAFLD. Persistent mild ALT elevation (56-150 U/L) is the most common abnormal laboratory finding in clinical practice and frequently reflects non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), which affects 25-30% of adults in Western populations.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Some guidelines recommend lower upper limits (M: &lt;30 U/L, F: &lt;19 U/L) for detecting early NAFLD</li>
<li>Levels fluctuate with meals, exercise, and menstrual cycle</li>
<li>Reference ranges vary slightly between laboratories</li>
<li>Always interpret alongside AST, GGT, bilirubin, and alkaline phosphatase</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase ALT:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD/NASH) -- most common cause of mild chronic elevation</li>
<li>Viral hepatitis (A, B, C, E) -- acute infections cause marked elevation</li>
<li>Alcoholic liver disease (AST typically elevated more than ALT)</li>
<li>Drug-induced liver injury (acetaminophen, statins, antibiotics, anticonvulsants, NSAIDs)</li>
<li>Autoimmune hepatitis</li>
<li>Hemochromatosis and Wilson disease</li>
<li>Muscle injury or strenuous exercise (mild, transient elevation)</li>
<li>Celiac disease (mild elevation resolves with gluten-free diet)</li>
<li>Hepatic ischemia (shock liver)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease ALT:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) deficiency -- ALT requires B6 as cofactor</li>
<li>End-stage liver disease (burned-out cirrhosis with insufficient hepatocyte mass)</li>
<li>Uremia (dialysis patients)</li>
<li>Regular coffee consumption (associated with lower ALT levels)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated ALT indicates:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>NAFLD/NASH</strong> -- The most common cause of persistent mild ALT elevation (56-150 U/L) in developed countries. ALT &gt;30 U/L with fatty liver on imaging is suggestive. Progressive NASH with fibrosis is a leading indication for liver transplant</li>
<li><strong>Viral hepatitis</strong> -- Acute hepatitis A/B/C produces ALT levels &gt;1000 U/L with AST elevation. Chronic hepatitis C often presents with fluctuating mild-moderate elevations</li>
<li><strong>Drug-induced liver injury (DILI)</strong> -- Statins, acetaminophen, isoniazid, amoxicillin-clavulanate, and &gt;1000 other drugs. Statin-related ALT elevation is usually mild (&lt;3x ULN) and self-limited</li>
<li><strong>Alcoholic liver disease</strong> -- Characterized by AST &gt; ALT (De Ritis ratio &gt;2.0), with GGT elevation. AST rarely exceeds 300 U/L in pure alcoholic hepatitis</li>
<li><strong>Autoimmune hepatitis</strong> -- Moderate to severe elevation with positive ANA and anti-smooth muscle antibodies</li>
<li><strong>Hemochromatosis</strong> -- Mild-moderate elevation from iron deposition; ferritin and transferrin saturation are key diagnostic markers</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Clinical utility:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Most sensitive routine marker for hepatocellular injury</li>
<li>AST/ALT ratio helps differentiate liver disease etiology</li>
<li>Serial monitoring guides treatment response in chronic liver disease</li>
<li>Part of standard liver panel and comprehensive metabolic panel</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Annual screening via CMP in adults</li>
<li>Monitoring hepatotoxic medications (statins, methotrexate, isoniazid)</li>
<li>Tracking NAFLD progression and lifestyle intervention response</li>
<li>Post-treatment surveillance for viral hepatitis</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Weight loss</strong> -- 5-10% body weight reduction is the most effective intervention for NAFLD-related ALT elevation; target gradual loss of 0.5-1 kg/week</li>
<li><strong>Reduce alcohol intake</strong> -- Limit to &lt;2 drinks/day for men, &lt;1 for women; abstinence recommended if ALT &gt;2x ULN</li>
<li><strong>NAC supplementation</strong> -- 600-1200 mg/day supports glutathione production and hepatocyte protection (evidence level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Curcumin supplementation</strong> -- 500-1000 mg/day with piperine has shown ALT reduction in NAFLD trials (evidence level 3)</li>
<li><strong>Review medications</strong> -- Evaluate statins, supplements (vitamin A, kava, green tea extract at high doses), and OTC drugs for hepatotoxicity</li>
<li><strong>Exercise regularly</strong> -- 150+ minutes/week of aerobic activity reduces hepatic fat and ALT independent of weight loss</li>
<li><strong>Coffee consumption</strong> -- 2-3 cups/day is associated with lower ALT and reduced liver fibrosis risk</li>
</ol>
