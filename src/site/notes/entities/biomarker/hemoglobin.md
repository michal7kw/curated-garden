<h1>Hemoglobin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Hemoglobin (Hb) is the iron-containing oxygen-transport metalloprotein in red blood cells. Each hemoglobin molecule can carry up to four oxygen molecules, making it essential for tissue oxygenation. Hemoglobin concentration is the most widely used screening test for anemia and polycythemia worldwide.</p>
<p>Low hemoglobin (anemia) affects an estimated 1.8 billion people globally, with iron deficiency being the most common cause. Symptoms include fatigue, weakness, pallor, shortness of breath, and reduced exercise capacity. High hemoglobin (polycythemia) increases blood viscosity and risk of thrombosis.</p>
<p>Hemoglobin is measured as part of a complete blood count (CBC) and reflects the total oxygen-carrying capacity of blood. It is distinct from hematocrit (the proportion of red blood cells by volume), though the two are closely correlated (Hb ≈ Hct ÷ 3).</p>

<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated Hemoglobin (Polycythemia):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Polycythemia vera (myeloproliferative neoplasm)</li>
<li>Chronic hypoxia (COPD, sleep apnea, high altitude)</li>
<li>Dehydration (relative increase)</li>
<li>Erythropoietin-secreting tumors</li>
<li>Testosterone therapy</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced Hemoglobin (Anemia):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Iron deficiency (most common cause worldwide)</li>
<li>Vitamin B12 or folate deficiency (megaloblastic anemia)</li>
<li>Chronic disease/inflammation (anemia of chronic disease)</li>
<li>Acute or chronic blood loss</li>
<li>Bone marrow disorders (aplastic anemia, MDS)</li>
<li>Hemolytic anemias (sickle cell, thalassemia, autoimmune)</li>
</ul>
