<h1>AA/EPA Ratio</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The AA/EPA ratio quantifies the balance between arachidonic acid (AA), a pro-inflammatory omega-6 fatty acid derived from linoleic acid, and eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA), an anti-inflammatory omega-3 fatty acid. This ratio is one of the most clinically validated biomarkers for assessing cellular inflammation status and predicting cardiovascular risk.</p>
<p>Arachidonic acid serves as the primary precursor for pro-inflammatory eicosanoids (prostaglandins, leukotrienes, thromboxanes) via the COX and LOX enzymatic pathways. EPA competes with AA for these same enzymes but produces less inflammatory (or actively anti-inflammatory) eicosanoids, as well as specialized pro-resolving mediators (resolvins, protectins). A high AA/EPA ratio indicates that the cellular membrane fatty acid pool is skewed toward pro-inflammatory signaling.</p>
<p>The typical Western diet produces ratios of 10:1 to 20:1 due to excessive omega-6 intake from vegetable oils and insufficient omega-3 from fatty fish. Japanese populations consuming traditional diets typically have ratios of 2-4:1 and correspondingly lower cardiovascular disease rates. Serial measurement is valuable for monitoring omega-3 supplementation efficacy and dietary intervention success.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Values are for whole blood or serum phospholipid fraction (RBC membrane levels are more stable but less commonly reported)</li>
<li>The ratio is more clinically informative than absolute AA or EPA levels alone</li>
<li>Japanese guidelines consider &lt;3:1 as the target for cardiovascular prevention</li>
<li>Ratios &gt;15:1 are associated with 2-3x increased cardiovascular event risk in prospective studies</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase AA/EPA Ratio:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>High omega-6 intake from vegetable oils (soybean, corn, sunflower, safflower)</li>
<li>Low fatty fish consumption (dietary omega-3 deficiency)</li>
<li>High intake of grain-fed animal products (higher AA content than grass-fed)</li>
<li>Processed food consumption (often cooked in omega-6-rich oils)</li>
<li>Aging (reduced delta-6 desaturase activity impairs EPA conversion)</li>
<li>Insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome (alter fatty acid metabolism)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease AA/EPA Ratio:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Omega-3 supplementation (EPA and DHA from fish oil, krill oil, algal oil)</li>
<li>Regular fatty fish consumption (salmon, sardines, mackerel, anchovies)</li>
<li>Mediterranean or traditional Japanese dietary patterns</li>
<li>Reducing vegetable oil and processed food intake</li>
<li>Grass-fed meat and pasture-raised eggs (higher omega-3, lower omega-6)</li>
<li>Walnuts and flaxseed (ALA, though conversion to EPA is limited)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated ratio is associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular disease</strong> -- High AA/EPA ratios independently predict cardiovascular events; AA-derived eicosanoids promote platelet aggregation, vasoconstriction, and vascular inflammation</li>
<li><strong>Cancer risk</strong> -- Elevated ratios correlate with increased risk of colorectal, breast, and prostate cancers; pro-inflammatory eicosanoids promote cell proliferation and angiogenesis</li>
<li><strong>Depression and mood disorders</strong> -- Neuroinflammation driven by AA-derived eicosanoids; low omega-3 status is a replicable risk factor for major depression</li>
<li><strong>Autoimmune disease</strong> -- Rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, and other autoimmune conditions show elevated AA/EPA ratios; pro-inflammatory eicosanoids amplify immune activation</li>
<li><strong>Asthma and allergic disease</strong> -- AA-derived leukotrienes are potent bronchoconstrictors; elevated ratios correlate with asthma severity</li>
<li><strong>Metabolic dysfunction</strong> -- Insulin resistance, obesity, and type 2 diabetes are associated with higher ratios reflecting chronic low-grade inflammation</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced ratio (optimal &lt;3:1) is associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Lower systemic inflammation and CRP levels</li>
<li>Reduced cardiovascular event rates</li>
<li>Improved insulin sensitivity</li>
<li>Better cognitive function and mood</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Assessing omega-3 supplement bioavailability and dosing adequacy</li>
<li>Cardiovascular risk stratification beyond lipid panels</li>
<li>Monitoring dietary intervention success</li>
<li>Guiding anti-inflammatory supplementation protocols</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Omega-3 supplementation</strong> -- EPA-rich fish oil (1-4 g/day combined EPA+DHA); higher EPA content specifically targets the ratio. Prescription omega-3 (Vascepa, Lovaza) provides standardized dosing</li>
<li><strong>Increase fatty fish intake</strong> -- 2-3 servings per week of salmon, sardines, mackerel, or anchovies provides ~1-2 g/day EPA+DHA</li>
<li><strong>Reduce omega-6 intake</strong> -- Replace vegetable oils (soybean, corn, sunflower) with olive oil or avocado oil; minimize processed and fried foods</li>
<li><strong>Choose grass-fed and pasture-raised products</strong> -- Higher omega-3 and lower omega-6 content compared to grain-fed alternatives</li>
<li><strong>Consider krill oil or algal oil</strong> -- Krill oil may have superior bioavailability due to phospholipid delivery; algal oil is the vegan EPA+DHA source</li>
<li><strong>Reduce processed food consumption</strong> -- Industrial foods are the primary source of excess omega-6 in Western diets</li>
</ol>
