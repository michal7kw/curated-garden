<h1>BDNF</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) is a 27 kDa dimeric protein belonging to the neurotrophin family. It is the most abundant neurotrophin in the mammalian brain and is essential for neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, long-term potentiation (LTP -- the cellular basis of learning and memory), and adult neurogenesis in the hippocampal dentate gyrus.</p>
<p>BDNF acts primarily through the TrkB (tropomyosin receptor kinase B) receptor, activating three major signaling cascades: MAPK/ERK (neuronal differentiation), PI3K/Akt (cell survival), and PLC-gamma (synaptic plasticity). Serum BDNF crosses the blood-brain barrier and reflects approximately 70-80% of brain BDNF levels, making peripheral measurement a valid proxy for central BDNF status.</p>
<p>The BDNF gene contains a functional Val66Met polymorphism (rs6265) present in ~30% of Caucasians and ~50% of Asians. Met carriers have reduced activity-dependent BDNF secretion, smaller hippocampal volumes, and modestly increased risk for anxiety, depression, and cognitive impairment. However, the polymorphism accounts for only a small fraction of BDNF variability -- lifestyle factors (exercise, sleep, diet, stress) exert much stronger influence on circulating BDNF levels.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Serum BDNF varies throughout the day (diurnal variation, highest in morning)</li>
<li>Exercise acutely increases BDNF 2-3x within 30 minutes, returning to baseline over hours</li>
<li>Platelets store large amounts of BDNF; serum levels are higher than plasma due to platelet release during clotting</li>
<li>Assay method matters: ELISA is standard, but inter-assay variability is significant</li>
<li>No universally standardized clinical reference range exists; used primarily in research</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase BDNF:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Aerobic exercise (most potent natural stimulus -- 30+ minutes of moderate-intensity)</li>
<li>Learning and cognitive engagement (new skill acquisition, language learning)</li>
<li>Adequate sleep (7-9 hours; slow-wave sleep promotes BDNF expression)</li>
<li>Meditation and mindfulness practice</li>
<li>Omega-3 DHA supplementation</li>
<li>Sunlight exposure and vitamin D adequacy</li>
<li>Social interaction and positive relationships</li>
<li>Intermittent fasting and caloric restriction</li>
<li>Certain supplements (curcumin, lion's mane, bacopa, magnesium L-threonate)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease BDNF:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Chronic stress and elevated cortisol (glucocorticoids suppress BDNF gene expression)</li>
<li>Depression and anxiety disorders</li>
<li>Alzheimer's disease and other neurodegenerative conditions</li>
<li>Sedentary lifestyle</li>
<li>Sleep deprivation</li>
<li>Social isolation</li>
<li>High-sugar and high-fat diets</li>
<li>Aging (hippocampal BDNF declines ~15% per decade after age 50)</li>
<li>Traumatic brain injury (acute decrease, may remain chronically low)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Low BDNF is associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Major depressive disorder</strong> -- BDNF levels are consistently reduced in depressed patients (effect size d = 0.8-1.0) and normalize with successful antidepressant treatment. The &quot;neurogenic hypothesis of depression&quot; proposes that reduced hippocampal BDNF and neurogenesis are core pathophysiological features</li>
<li><strong>Alzheimer's disease</strong> -- Hippocampal BDNF is reduced 40-50% in AD; serum BDNF correlates with disease severity and cognitive decline rate. Low BDNF may be both a risk factor and consequence of neurodegeneration</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive decline and MCI</strong> -- Lower serum BDNF predicts faster conversion from MCI to dementia</li>
<li><strong>PTSD and anxiety</strong> -- Chronic stress reduces BDNF through cortisol-mediated suppression</li>
<li><strong>Obesity and metabolic syndrome</strong> -- Adipose tissue inflammation and insulin resistance reduce circulating BDNF</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>High BDNF is associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Better cognitive performance (memory, executive function, processing speed)</li>
<li>Resilience to stress and lower depression vulnerability</li>
<li>Greater hippocampal volume on MRI</li>
<li>Slower cognitive aging</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Research endpoint in exercise, nutrition, and cognitive intervention studies</li>
<li>Tracking antidepressant treatment response</li>
<li>Evaluating neuroprotective supplement efficacy</li>
<li>Biomarker for neuroplasticity in cognitive rehabilitation</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Regular aerobic exercise</strong> -- The single most effective intervention; 150+ minutes/week of moderate-intensity exercise (running, cycling, swimming) increases BDNF 2-3x. Both acute and chronic exercise elevate levels</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 DHA supplementation</strong> -- 1000-2000 mg/day DHA supports BDNF expression through TrkB receptor signaling (evidence level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Lion's Mane mushroom</strong> -- 500-3000 mg/day of Hericium erinaceus extract stimulates NGF and BDNF production (evidence level 3)</li>
<li><strong>Curcumin supplementation</strong> -- 500-1000 mg/day enhances BDNF through CREB pathway activation (evidence level 3)</li>
<li><strong>Meditation and mindfulness</strong> -- 20+ minutes daily practice increases serum BDNF and hippocampal gray matter density</li>
<li><strong>Sleep optimization</strong> -- 7-9 hours of quality sleep; slow-wave sleep is the primary period of BDNF-mediated memory consolidation</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive challenge</strong> -- Learning new skills (musical instruments, languages, complex motor tasks) upregulates BDNF through activity-dependent mechanisms</li>
</ol>
