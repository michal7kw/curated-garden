<h1>Adiponectin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Adiponectin is an anti-inflammatory adipokine hormone produced exclusively by adipose tissue that enhances insulin sensitivity, inhibits hepatic gluconeogenesis, and protects against atherosclerosis. It is the most abundant circulating adipokine, accounting for approximately 0.01% of total plasma protein, yet is paradoxically reduced in obesity -- a phenomenon termed the &quot;adiponectin paradox.&quot;</p>
<p>Adiponectin exists in three oligomeric forms in circulation: low-molecular-weight trimers, medium-molecular-weight hexamers, and high-molecular-weight (HMW) multimers. The HMW form is considered the most biologically active and is the strongest predictor of insulin sensitivity. Adiponectin signals through two receptors, AdipoR1 (predominantly in skeletal muscle) and AdipoR2 (predominantly in liver), activating AMPK and PPAR-alpha pathways that increase fatty acid oxidation and glucose uptake.</p>
<p>Despite being produced by fat cells, adiponectin levels decrease as adiposity increases because pro-inflammatory cytokines from visceral fat (TNF-alpha, IL-6) suppress adiponectin gene expression. This inverse relationship makes adiponectin a unique biomarker where higher levels are protective and lower levels signal metabolic dysfunction. Measurement is primarily used in research and integrative medicine settings for insulin resistance assessment.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Women typically have 30-40% higher adiponectin levels than men, partly due to androgen suppression of adiponectin in men</li>
<li>HMW adiponectin is more clinically informative than total adiponectin; some labs report both</li>
<li>Levels decrease with age after peaking in early adulthood</li>
<li>No universally standardized reference range; values vary by assay method (ELISA, immunoturbidimetric)</li>
<li>Interpret relative to BMI, waist circumference, and other metabolic markers</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase Adiponectin:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Weight loss (particularly visceral fat reduction)</li>
<li>Regular aerobic exercise (independent of weight loss)</li>
<li>Mediterranean and plant-based dietary patterns</li>
<li>Thiazolidinedione medications (pioglitazone, rosiglitazone -- PPAR-gamma agonists)</li>
<li>Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation (modest effect)</li>
<li>Moderate alcohol consumption (transient increase)</li>
<li>Female sex hormones (estrogen increases adiponectin)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease Adiponectin:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Obesity, particularly visceral adiposity (pro-inflammatory cytokines suppress production)</li>
<li>Insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes</li>
<li>Metabolic syndrome</li>
<li>High-saturated-fat and high-sugar diets</li>
<li>Sedentary lifestyle</li>
<li>Chronic stress and elevated cortisol</li>
<li>Androgens (testosterone reduces adiponectin)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Low levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes</strong> -- Adiponectin is one of the strongest predictors of insulin resistance; low levels precede the development of hyperglycemia by years and independently predict type 2 diabetes onset</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular disease</strong> -- Low adiponectin is an independent risk factor for coronary artery disease, myocardial infarction, and stroke; it promotes endothelial dysfunction and vascular inflammation when deficient</li>
<li><strong>Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD)</strong> -- Reduced adiponectin contributes to hepatic steatosis by failing to suppress hepatic lipogenesis; HMW adiponectin inversely correlates with liver fat content</li>
<li><strong>Metabolic syndrome</strong> -- Adiponectin is inversely correlated with all five metabolic syndrome criteria (waist circumference, triglycerides, HDL, blood pressure, fasting glucose)</li>
<li><strong>Hypertension</strong> -- Low adiponectin correlates with endothelial dysfunction and impaired nitric oxide production</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>High levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Lean body habitus and favorable metabolic profile</li>
<li>Reduced cardiovascular event rates</li>
<li>Protection against hepatic steatosis</li>
<li>Improved insulin sensitivity</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Assessing insulin resistance beyond HOMA-IR</li>
<li>Monitoring response to weight loss interventions</li>
<li>Evaluating cardiometabolic risk in metabolically unhealthy normal-weight individuals</li>
<li>Research endpoint in clinical trials of metabolic interventions</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Weight loss</strong> -- Reducing visceral adiposity is the single most effective intervention; even 5-10% weight loss can significantly raise adiponectin levels</li>
<li><strong>Regular aerobic exercise</strong> -- 150+ minutes per week of moderate-intensity exercise increases adiponectin independent of weight loss</li>
<li><strong>Mediterranean dietary pattern</strong> -- Rich in monounsaturated fats, fiber, and polyphenols; consistently associated with higher adiponectin levels</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 supplementation</strong> -- 2-4 g/day EPA+DHA may modestly increase adiponectin (evidence level 3)</li>
<li><strong>Berberine supplementation</strong> -- 500 mg 2-3x/day has shown adiponectin-elevating effects in metabolic syndrome trials</li>
<li><strong>Reduce refined carbohydrates and saturated fat</strong> -- High-glycemic and high-saturated-fat diets suppress adiponectin production</li>
</ol>
