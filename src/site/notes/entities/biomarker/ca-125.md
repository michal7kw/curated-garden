<h1>CA 125</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Cancer Antigen 125 (CA 125) is a high-molecular-weight (200-1000 kDa) transmembrane glycoprotein encoded by the MUC16 gene. It is expressed on the surface of coelomic epithelial derivatives, including mesothelial cells lining the peritoneal, pleural, and pericardial cavities, as well as on epithelial ovarian cancer cells. Serum CA 125 rises when these tissues are damaged, inflamed, or undergo malignant transformation, reflecting disruption of the mesothelial barrier rather than being specific to any single disease.</p>
<p>CA 125 is the most established serum biomarker for epithelial ovarian cancer, used primarily for monitoring treatment response and detecting recurrence. A sustained decline of more than 50% during chemotherapy correlates with favorable prognosis, while rising levels after treatment completion often precede radiologically detectable recurrence by a median of 2-6 months. However, CA 125 has critical limitations: only 50-60% of stage I ovarian cancers produce elevated CA 125, and numerous benign conditions — including endometriosis, pelvic inflammatory disease, menstruation, pregnancy, liver cirrhosis, and congestive heart failure — can cause elevations, limiting its utility as a standalone screening test.</p>
<p>Current guidelines from NCCN and ESMO recommend against using CA 125 alone for population-based ovarian cancer screening. The biomarker is most valuable when interpreted alongside imaging (transvaginal ultrasound) and clinical context, and when tracked as a trend over time rather than as a single value. The ROMA (Risk of Ovarian Malignancy Algorithm) score, which combines CA 125 with HE4 and menopausal status, provides improved specificity for preoperative risk stratification.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>The 35 U/mL cutoff was derived from a reference population of healthy men and has limited sensitivity for early-stage disease.</li>
<li>Premenopausal women frequently have CA 125 values up to 50-65 U/mL from benign gynecological conditions.</li>
<li>CA 125 is less reliable in mucinous and clear-cell ovarian cancer subtypes (frequently non-elevated).</li>
<li>Serial measurement trends are more informative than any single value for cancer monitoring.</li>
<li>The ROMA algorithm combines CA 125 with HE4 for improved discrimination of benign vs. malignant pelvic masses.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase CA 125:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Epithelial ovarian cancer (particularly serous carcinoma; 80-85% have elevated CA 125 at diagnosis)</li>
<li>Endometriosis and endometriomas (inflammatory peritoneal irritation)</li>
<li>Pelvic inflammatory disease (peritoneal inflammation)</li>
<li>Pregnancy (first trimester physiological elevation)</li>
<li>Menstruation (transient mild elevation)</li>
<li>Liver cirrhosis with ascites (mesothelial irritation from peritoneal fluid)</li>
<li>Congestive heart failure (peritoneal congestion and effusion)</li>
<li>Peritonitis of any cause (bacterial, tuberculous, chemical)</li>
<li>Recent abdominal surgery (transient rise for 2-4 weeks post-operatively)</li>
<li>Benign gynecological conditions (fibroids, adenomyosis)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease CA 125:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Effective chemotherapy response in ovarian cancer (&gt; 50% decline indicates favorable response)</li>
<li>Surgical debulking (post-operative decline reflects tumor burden reduction)</li>
<li>Menopause (lower baseline levels than premenopausal women)</li>
<li>Resolution of inflammatory or infectious peritoneal conditions</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Ovarian Cancer (Epithelial)</strong> -- the primary clinical application; CA 125 is elevated in 80-85% of advanced-stage serous ovarian carcinomas; serial monitoring detects recurrence 2-6 months before imaging (PMID: 19060282)</li>
<li><strong>Endometriosis</strong> -- elevated in 30-50% of women with moderate-severe endometriosis; levels correlate with disease stage and deep infiltrating disease; used to monitor treatment response (PMID: 23453832)</li>
<li><strong>Pelvic Inflammatory Disease</strong> -- acute peritoneal inflammation elevates CA 125; levels normalize with antibiotic treatment (PMID: 21464973)</li>
<li><strong>Liver Cirrhosis</strong> -- ascitic fluid irritation of mesothelial cells produces elevated CA 125; degree of elevation correlates with ascites volume (PMID: 19568598)</li>
<li><strong>Cervical Cancer</strong> -- elevated in advanced-stage cervical adenocarcinoma; less useful than in ovarian cancer</li>
<li><strong>Other Malignancies</strong> -- pancreatic, gastric, colorectal, and lung adenocarcinomas can produce CA 125 from peritoneal involvement</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Favorable chemotherapy response (CA 125 half-life during treatment predicts survival)</li>
<li>Complete surgical resection of tumor</li>
<li>Mucinous and clear-cell ovarian cancer subtypes (frequently non-secretors)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Monitoring chemotherapy response in epithelial ovarian cancer (every 3 months)</li>
<li>Detecting ovarian cancer recurrence during surveillance (rising trend &gt; 2 consecutive values)</li>
<li>Preoperative risk stratification of adnexal masses (via ROMA score with HE4)</li>
<li>Monitoring endometriosis treatment response in research settings</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Manage</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Appropriate oncological treatment</strong> -- The most effective way to reduce CA 125 in ovarian cancer is surgical cytoreduction followed by platinum-based chemotherapy. CA 125 normalization after treatment completion indicates biochemical remission.</li>
<li><strong>Curcumin supplementation</strong> -- 500-1000 mg/day of bioavailable curcumin formulations have shown anti-proliferative effects in preclinical ovarian cancer models and may modestly reduce inflammatory markers contributing to CA 125 elevation (PMID: 23993721).</li>
<li><strong>Vitamin D optimization</strong> -- Observational studies suggest vitamin D sufficiency (&gt; 40 ng/mL) is associated with improved ovarian cancer outcomes; supplementation may reduce inflammatory contributions to CA 125 elevation (PMID: 21464973).</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation</strong> -- 1-2 g/day of EPA/DHA may reduce peritoneal inflammation and has been associated with improved quality of life in ovarian cancer patients (PMID: 19568598).</li>
<li><strong>Regular monitoring</strong> -- Serial CA 125 measurements every 3 months during the first 2 years post-treatment enable early detection of recurrence, with rising trends prompting earlier imaging evaluation.</li>
<li><strong>Clinical context interpretation</strong> -- Always interpret CA 125 alongside imaging findings, menopausal status, and benign condition assessment to avoid unnecessary anxiety and intervention from false-positive elevations.</li>
</ol>
