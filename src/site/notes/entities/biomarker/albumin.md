<h1>Albumin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Albumin is the most abundant protein in human blood plasma, accounting for approximately 50-60% of total plasma protein content. Produced exclusively by the liver at a rate of roughly 10-15 grams per day, it has a half-life of about 20 days.</p>
<p>Albumin serves three primary physiological functions:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Colloidal Osmotic Pressure (COP):</strong> It provides about 80% of the oncotic pressure of plasma, which keeps fluid within the vascular compartment and prevents it from leaking into interstitial tissues.</li>
<li><strong>Molecular Transport:</strong> It acts as a non-specific carrier for a wide range of endogenous and exogenous substances, including hormones (thyroid hormones, steroids), minerals (calcium, magnesium, zinc), fatty acids, bilirubin, and many pharmacological drugs.</li>
<li><strong>Antioxidant and Buffering:</strong> It possesses significant antioxidant capacity by binding to transition metals and scavenging reactive oxygen species, and contributes to the acid-base buffering capacity of blood.</li>
</ol>
<p>In clinical settings, albumin is a negative acute-phase reactant, meaning its levels decrease during systemic inflammation, infection, or trauma. It is also used as a marker of hepatic synthetic function and long-term nutritional status.</p>

<h2>Testing Information</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Measurement Unit</strong>: grams per deciliter (g/dL) or grams per liter (g/L)</li>
<li><strong>Sample Type</strong>: Blood (Serum)</li>
<li><strong>Fasting Required</strong>: False (though often part of a CMP)</li>
<li><strong>Recommended Test Frequency</strong>: Annually; more frequent if monitoring chronic liver or kidney disease.</li>
</ul>
<h2>💊 Supplements That Affect This Biomarker</h2>
<h3>Increasing Albumin</h3>
<ul>
<li><a class="internal-link is-unresolved" href="/404">Whey Protein</a> - Direct provision of amino acids supports hepatic protein synthesis, particularly in malnourished or elderly populations (Moderate magnitude, Evidence Level 5).</li>
<li><a href="/garden/michal.k7w@gmail.com/supplement/omega-3-fatty-acids/" class="internal-link">Omega-3 Fatty Acids</a> - May increase albumin levels by reducing systemic inflammation, particularly in chronic disease states like cancer or dialysis (Moderate magnitude, Evidence Level 4).</li>
<li><a href="/garden/michal.k7w@gmail.com/supplement/zinc/" class="internal-link">Zinc</a> - Essential cofactor for protein synthesis; deficiency can impair albumin production (Mild magnitude, Evidence Level 3).</li>
<li><a href="/garden/michal.k7w@gmail.com/supplement/vitamin-c/" class="internal-link">Vitamin C</a> - Supports antioxidant status and protein metabolism (Mild magnitude, Evidence Level 3).</li>
</ul>
<h3>Factors Decreasing Albumin</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Chronic Alcohol Consumption</strong> - Directly impairs hepatic synthetic function.</li>
<li><strong>Systemic Inflammation</strong> - Cytokines (like IL-6) shift liver protein synthesis away from albumin and toward acute-phase reactants (e.g., CRP).</li>
</ul>
