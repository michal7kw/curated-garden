<h1>Albumin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Albumin is the most abundant circulating protein in human plasma, accounting for approximately 50-60% of total plasma protein. Synthesized exclusively by hepatocytes at a rate of 12-15 g/day, albumin has a half-life of 18-20 days and is distributed between the intravascular (40%) and extravascular (60%) compartments.</p>
<p>Albumin serves three major physiological functions: (1) maintenance of colloid oncotic pressure, which prevents fluid transudation from capillaries into interstitial tissue; (2) transport of endogenous and exogenous substances including fatty acids, bilirubin, thyroid hormones, cortisol, calcium, drugs, and metal ions; and (3) antioxidant and free radical scavenging through its thiol groups (Cys-34 residue).</p>
<p>Because albumin synthesis requires adequate hepatocyte function, sufficient amino acid supply, and absence of chronic inflammatory cytokine suppression (IL-6, TNF-alpha downregulate albumin gene expression), serum albumin serves as a global integrative marker. Low albumin reflects the combined impact of hepatic dysfunction, nutritional deficiency, systemic inflammation, and protein loss -- making it a powerful prognostic indicator in acute and chronic disease.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Slight decrease with age (~0.1 g/dL per decade after age 50)</li>
<li>Levels may appear falsely low with IV fluid dilution, pregnancy, and recumbent position</li>
<li>Always interpret alongside total protein, globulin, and clinical context</li>
<li>Prealbumin (transthyretin, half-life 2 days) is better for acute nutritional monitoring</li>
<li>Albumin is a negative acute-phase reactant -- decreases during inflammation regardless of nutrition</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase Albumin:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Dehydration (hemoconcentration -- most common cause of elevated albumin)</li>
<li>High protein intake in previously malnourished individuals (repletion)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease Albumin:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Liver disease (reduced synthesis -- cirrhosis, hepatitis, hepatocellular carcinoma)</li>
<li>Nephrotic syndrome (renal protein loss &gt;3.5 g/day)</li>
<li>Protein-losing enteropathy (GI loss -- Crohn disease, celiac disease)</li>
<li>Malnutrition and protein-calorie deficiency</li>
<li>Chronic inflammation and infection (cytokines suppress synthesis)</li>
<li>Sepsis and critical illness (capillary leak increases extravascular distribution)</li>
<li>Burns (loss through damaged skin)</li>
<li>Heart failure (chronic venous congestion impairs lymphatic return)</li>
<li>Pregnancy (hemodilution)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Low albumin indicates:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Liver disease</strong> -- Albumin is one of the best markers of hepatic synthetic function; values &lt;3.0 g/dL in cirrhosis indicate decompensation and are part of the Child-Pugh scoring system</li>
<li><strong>Nephrotic syndrome</strong> -- Urinary albumin loss &gt;3.5 g/day causes hypoalbuminemia and edema; serum albumin often &lt;2.5 g/dL in severe cases</li>
<li><strong>Malnutrition</strong> -- Chronic protein-calorie malnutrition reduces hepatic synthesis; however, albumin is a late marker (half-life 18-20 days) and insensitive to acute nutritional changes</li>
<li><strong>Chronic inflammation</strong> -- IL-6 and TNF-alpha shift hepatic protein synthesis from albumin to acute-phase reactants (CRP, fibrinogen); albumin is a negative acute-phase reactant</li>
<li><strong>Critical illness prognosis</strong> -- Albumin &lt;2.5 g/dL in hospitalized patients is an independent predictor of mortality, prolonged ICU stay, and complications</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Clinical scoring systems using albumin:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Child-Pugh classification</strong> -- Cirrhosis severity scoring (albumin is one of 5 components)</li>
<li><strong>MELD-Na score</strong> -- Model for end-stage liver disease; albumin not directly included but correlates with prognosis</li>
<li><strong>Nutritional risk index</strong> -- (Albumin x 0.1) + (weight change x 0.5) for malnutrition screening</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Cirrhosis surveillance and transplant listing assessment</li>
<li>Nephrotic syndrome disease activity monitoring</li>
<li>Nutritional status in chronic illness and elderly patients</li>
<li>Critical illness prognosis and fluid management guidance</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Adequate protein intake</strong> -- 1.0-1.5 g/kg/day of high-quality protein; increase to 1.5-2.0 g/kg/day in cirrhosis to counteract catabolism</li>
<li><strong>Treat underlying liver disease</strong> -- Antiviral therapy for hepatitis B/C, alcohol cessation, weight loss for NAFLD</li>
<li><strong>Manage nephrotic syndrome</strong> -- ACE inhibitors/ARBs reduce proteinuria; corticosteroids for minimal change disease; treat underlying cause</li>
<li><strong>Address chronic inflammation</strong> -- Treat infections, manage autoimmune disease, optimize chronic disease control</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 supplementation</strong> -- 2-4 g/day may modestly support albumin synthesis in chronic liver disease (evidence level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Zinc supplementation</strong> -- 15-30 mg/day; zinc deficiency impairs albumin synthesis and is common in cirrhosis</li>
</ol>
