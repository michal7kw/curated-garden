<h1>Chloride</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Chloride is the most abundant anion (negatively charged ion) in the extracellular fluid. It is typically found in combination with sodium (as NaCl) or hydrogen (as HCl in the stomach). Chloride is essential for maintaining:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Electrical Neutrality:</strong> Balancing the positive charges of sodium and potassium.</li>
<li><strong>Osmotic Pressure:</strong> Regulating the distribution of water between compartments.</li>
<li><strong>Acid-Base Balance:</strong> Acting as a buffer; the kidneys exchange chloride for bicarbonate to help regulate blood pH.</li>
<li><strong>Digestion:</strong> Serving as a primary component of hydrochloric acid (gastric juice).</li>
</ul>
<p>Chloride levels are rarely evaluated in isolation. Clinicians interpret chloride in the context of other electrolytes—specifically the <strong>Anion Gap</strong>—to differentiate between various types of metabolic acidosis and alkalosis.</p>

<h2>Testing Information</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Measurement Unit</strong>: milliequivalents per liter (mEq/L) or millimoles per liter (mmol/L)</li>
<li><strong>Sample Type</strong>: Blood (Serum or Plasma)</li>
<li><strong>Fasting Required</strong>: False (though usually part of a fasting BMP/CMP)</li>
<li><strong>Recommended Test Frequency</strong>: Annually; more frequent if monitoring kidney disease, hypertension, or acid-base disorders.</li>
</ul>
<h2>⚖️ Factors That Affect This Biomarker</h2>
<h3>Causes of Low Chloride (Hypochloremia)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Fluid Loss:</strong> Excessive vomiting (loss of HCl), heavy sweating, or chronic diarrhea.</li>
<li><strong>Kidney Issues:</strong> <a class="internal-link is-unresolved" href="/404">Addison's Disease</a> (adrenal insufficiency) or overuse of loop diuretics.</li>
<li><strong>Respiratory Acidosis:</strong> The body compensates by excreting chloride to retain bicarbonate.</li>
<li><strong>Overhydration:</strong> Dilutional hyponatremia often leads to hypochloremia.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Causes of High Chloride (Hyperchloremia)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Dehydration:</strong> Concentration of electrolytes due to water loss.</li>
<li><strong>Kidney Dysfunction:</strong> Renal tubular acidosis (RTA) or chronic kidney disease.</li>
<li><strong>High Salt Intake:</strong> Excessive consumption of sodium chloride.</li>
<li><strong>Metabolic Acidosis:</strong> Particularly &quot;Normal Anion Gap&quot; acidosis (hyperchloremic acidosis).</li>
<li><strong>Medications:</strong> Certain diuretics (acetazolamide) or corticosteroids.</li>
</ul>
<h2>🧪 Lab Tests That Measure This Biomarker</h2>
<h3>Direct Tests</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Chloride, Serum</strong> - Part of standard electrolyte testing.</li>
<li><strong>Chloride, Urine</strong> - Used to evaluate the cause of metabolic alkalosis.</li>
</ul>

<ul>

<li><strong>Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)</strong> - Often interpreted alongside chloride for acid-base assessment.</li>
</ul>
