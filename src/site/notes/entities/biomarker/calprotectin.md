<h1>Calprotectin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Calprotectin is a 36 kDa heterodimeric protein complex composed of S100A8 and S100A9 subunits, belonging to the S100 family of calcium-binding proteins. It constitutes approximately 60% of the cytosolic protein content of neutrophils and is released in large quantities when these cells migrate into the gastrointestinal mucosa in response to inflammation, infection, or tissue damage. Because calprotectin is resistant to bacterial degradation and remains stable in stool for up to 7 days at room temperature, it is well-suited as a practical non-invasive biomarker.</p>
<p>Fecal calprotectin is the most validated non-invasive marker for detecting intestinal inflammation and is recommended by multiple gastroenterology guidelines (ECCO, BSG, AGA) as a first-line test in the diagnostic workup of patients with lower gastrointestinal symptoms. It has a sensitivity of over 90% and a specificity of approximately 80% for distinguishing organic intestinal inflammation (IBD) from functional disorders (IBS), significantly reducing the need for invasive colonoscopy in low-risk patients.</p>
<p>Beyond diagnosis, fecal calprotectin is used for monitoring disease activity in Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, predicting relapse, assessing mucosal healing in response to treatment, and evaluating response to biologic therapy. It correlates well with endoscopic disease activity scores (SES-CD for Crohn's, Mayo endoscopic subscore for UC) and provides a reliable surrogate when endoscopy is not feasible.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Cutoff values vary by assay manufacturer (Buhlmann, QuantOn Cal, EliA). Always reference the specific assay's validated cutoffs.</li>
<li>In children, a lower cutoff of &lt; 50 mcg/g is used; neonates and infants may have physiologically elevated levels.</li>
<li>False positives can occur from NSAID use, PPI use, GI infections, and recent dietary changes.</li>
<li>Serial measurements are more informative than a single value; a rising trend predicts relapse in IBD patients.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase Fecal Calprotectin:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Inflammatory bowel disease — Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis (neutrophil infiltration of intestinal mucosa)</li>
<li>Gastrointestinal infections — bacterial (Campylobacter, Salmonella, C. difficile), viral, parasitic</li>
<li>NSAID enteropathy (NSAID-induced small intestinal ulceration and inflammation)</li>
<li>Celiac disease (active, untreated mucosal inflammation)</li>
<li>Colorectal cancer (tumor-associated inflammation and mucosal disruption)</li>
<li>Diverticulitis (localized inflammatory process)</li>
<li>Proton pump inhibitor use (mild increase in some patients)</li>
<li>Recent colonoscopy or bowel preparation (transient elevation)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease Fecal Calprotectin:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Effective IBD treatment (biologics, immunomodulators, corticosteroids achieving mucosal healing)</li>
<li>Dietary modifications (anti-inflammatory diets in functional GI patients)</li>
<li>Resolution of acute GI infections</li>
<li>Probiotic supplementation (modest reduction in some IBD patients)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Inflammatory Bowel Disease</strong> -- fecal calprotectin &gt; 200 mcg/g has &gt; 90% sensitivity for IBD; levels correlate with endoscopic disease activity and predict relapse 2-3 months before symptom onset (PMID: 31299865)</li>
<li><strong>Celiac Disease</strong> -- elevated in newly diagnosed, untreated celiac disease due to villous atrophy and mucosal inflammation; levels normalize on gluten-free diet (PMID: 29532975)</li>
<li><strong>GI Infections</strong> -- bacterial gastroenteritis produces transient calprotectin elevation that resolves with treatment; useful for differentiating infectious from functional diarrhea</li>
<li><strong>Colorectal Cancer</strong> -- moderately elevated calprotectin in CRC from tumor-associated inflammation; not a screening test but may prompt investigation (PMID: 30065775)</li>
<li><strong>NSAID Enteropathy</strong> -- chronic NSAID use causes small intestinal inflammation detectable by elevated fecal calprotectin; prevalence of NSAID enteropathy is 50-70% in chronic users</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Irritable Bowel Syndrome (calprotectin &lt; 50 mcg/g supports IBS diagnosis)</li>
<li>Mucosal healing in treated IBD patients (calprotectin &lt; 150 mcg/g correlates with endoscopic remission)</li>
<li>Effective treatment response in celiac disease patients on gluten-free diet</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Differentiating IBD from IBS in patients with chronic lower GI symptoms</li>
<li>Monitoring disease activity and treatment response in established IBD</li>
<li>Predicting IBD relapse (rising calprotectin precedes clinical symptoms by 2-3 months)</li>
<li>Assessing mucosal healing as a treatment target</li>
<li>Evaluating GI side effects of chronic NSAID use</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>IBD-specific treatment</strong> -- The most effective way to normalize calprotectin is treating the underlying IBD. Anti-TNF biologics (infliximab, adalimumab), anti-integrin therapy (vedolizumab), and JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib) achieve mucosal healing and calprotectin normalization in 40-60% of patients.</li>
<li><strong>Curcumin supplementation</strong> -- 2-3 g/day of curcumin has been shown to reduce fecal calprotectin by 30-50% in mild-to-moderate ulcerative colitis, with efficacy comparable to mesalamine in some trials (PMID: 31299865).</li>
<li><strong>Probiotics</strong> -- Specific strains (VSL#3, E. coli Nissle 1917, Saccharomyces boulardii) have shown modest calprotectin reduction in pouchitis and mild UC through competitive exclusion and anti-inflammatory effects (PMID: 29532975).</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation</strong> -- 2-3 g/day of EPA/DHA may reduce intestinal inflammation through resolvins and protectins, with modest calprotectin reductions observed in some IBD studies (PMID: 30065775).</li>
<li><strong>Boswellia serrata</strong> -- 300-500 mg 3 times daily of boswellic acid extract has anti-inflammatory effects on the intestinal mucosa and may reduce calprotectin in mild IBD (PMID: 17879274).</li>
<li><strong>Avoid NSAIDs and dietary triggers</strong> -- Discontinuing NSAIDs eliminates NSAID enteropathy as a cause of elevated calprotectin. Identifying and removing personal dietary triggers through an elimination diet may reduce GI inflammation.</li>
</ol>
