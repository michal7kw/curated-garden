<h1>Apolipoprotein B</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Apolipoprotein B (ApoB) is the primary structural protein found on all atherogenic lipoprotein particles. There are two forms: ApoB-100 (synthesized in the liver, found on VLDL, IDL, LDL, and Lp(a)) and ApoB-48 (synthesized in the intestine, found on chylomicrons). When clinicians measure &quot;ApoB,&quot; they are measuring ApoB-100, which reflects the total number of circulating atherogenic particles.</p>
<p>The critical advantage of ApoB over LDL cholesterol (LDL-C) is conceptual: LDL-C measures the cholesterol content inside LDL particles, while ApoB counts the number of particles. Since each atherogenic lipoprotein carries exactly one ApoB molecule, serum ApoB concentration equals the total atherogenic particle count. This matters because cardiovascular risk is driven by particle number, not cholesterol content -- a person with many small, cholesterol-poor LDL particles may have &quot;normal&quot; LDL-C but elevated ApoB and high actual cardiovascular risk.</p>
<p>Landmark trials (AMORIS, INTERHEART, MESA, Copenhagen General Population Study) have consistently demonstrated that ApoB outperforms LDL-C and non-HDL-C for cardiovascular risk prediction. The 2022 European Atherosclerosis Society (EAS) consensus statement recommends ApoB as the preferred primary lipid target for risk assessment and treatment monitoring, particularly in patients with metabolic syndrome, diabetes, or triglycerides &gt;150 mg/dL where LDL-C underestimates risk.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Targets should be individualized based on overall cardiovascular risk (lower targets for diabetics, prior MI, or high ASCVD risk)</li>
<li>ApoB &gt;130 mg/dL corresponds roughly to LDL-C &gt;160 mg/dL but captures risk from all atherogenic particles</li>
<li>ApoB is more reliable than LDL-C in hypertriglyceridemia, metabolic syndrome, and diabetes</li>
<li>Levels are relatively stable day-to-day compared to triglycerides</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase ApoB:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome (increased VLDL production)</li>
<li>Diabetes mellitus (increased hepatic VLDL secretion)</li>
<li>Hypothyroidism (reduced LDL receptor activity)</li>
<li>Chronic kidney disease (impaired lipoprotein clearance)</li>
<li>Familial hypercholesterolemia (LDL receptor defects)</li>
<li>High saturated and trans fat intake</li>
<li>Obesity, particularly visceral adiposity</li>
<li>Sedentary lifestyle</li>
<li>Nephrotic syndrome (increased hepatic lipoprotein synthesis)</li>
<li>Alcohol excess (increases VLDL production)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease ApoB:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Statin therapy (increases LDL receptor expression, reduces particle number)</li>
<li>PCSK9 inhibitors (dramatically increase LDL receptor recycling)</li>
<li>Ezetimibe (reduces intestinal cholesterol absorption)</li>
<li>Weight loss and exercise</li>
<li>Mediterranean dietary pattern</li>
<li>Niacin supplementation (reduces hepatic VLDL synthesis)</li>
<li>Abetalipoproteinemia (rare genetic deficiency; ApoB near zero)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated ApoB indicates:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular disease risk</strong> -- ApoB is the single best lipid predictor of ASCVD events. In the INTERHEART study, the ApoB/ApoA1 ratio was the strongest lipid predictor of myocardial infarction across all populations</li>
<li><strong>Atherogenic dyslipidemia</strong> -- In metabolic syndrome and diabetes, patients often have &quot;normal&quot; LDL-C but elevated ApoB due to increased small dense LDL particles. ApoB captures this residual risk that LDL-C misses</li>
<li><strong>Familial hypercholesterolemia</strong> -- Markedly elevated ApoB (&gt;200 mg/dL) with family history suggests FH and warrants genetic testing and aggressive treatment</li>
<li><strong>Treatment adequacy</strong> -- On-treatment ApoB &lt;80 mg/dL indicates adequate atherogenic particle reduction, even if LDL-C appears discordantly higher</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>When ApoB adds value beyond LDL-C:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Metabolic syndrome and diabetes (LDL-C underestimates risk)</li>
<li>Hypertriglyceridemia &gt;150 mg/dL (LDL-C calculation becomes unreliable)</li>
<li>Discordant lipid profiles (high ApoB with &quot;normal&quot; LDL-C)</li>
<li>Monitoring response to lipid-lowering therapy</li>
<li>Familial combined hyperlipidemia diagnosis</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Primary cardiovascular risk assessment</li>
<li>Monitoring statin, PCSK9 inhibitor, and combination therapy response</li>
<li>Evaluating residual cardiovascular risk in treated patients</li>
<li>Guiding treatment intensity in high-risk patients</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Statin therapy</strong> -- First-line pharmacotherapy; reduces ApoB 30-50% by upregulating LDL receptors and reducing VLDL production</li>
<li><strong>Niacin supplementation</strong> -- 1000-2000 mg/day reduces hepatic VLDL synthesis and ApoB by 15-25% (evidence level 5; note: flushing side effect, use extended-release form)</li>
<li><strong>Berberine</strong> -- 500 mg 2-3x/day reduces ApoB and LDL-C via LDL receptor upregulation and AMPK activation (evidence level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 fatty acids</strong> -- 2-4 g/day EPA+DHA reduces VLDL production and ApoB, particularly effective in hypertriglyceridemia (evidence level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Mediterranean diet</strong> -- Reduces ApoB by replacing saturated fats with monounsaturated fats and increasing fiber intake</li>
<li><strong>Weight loss</strong> -- 5-10% body weight reduction significantly lowers ApoB by improving insulin sensitivity and reducing hepatic VLDL output</li>
<li><strong>Regular aerobic exercise</strong> -- 150+ minutes/week reduces ApoB independent of weight loss</li>
</ol>
