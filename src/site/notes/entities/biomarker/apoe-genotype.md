<h1>APOE Genotype</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The APOE gene encodes apolipoprotein E, a 299-amino acid protein that mediates lipid transport and metabolism in the brain and periphery. Three common alleles -- epsilon2 (e2), epsilon3 (e3), and epsilon4 (e4) -- arise from single nucleotide polymorphisms that alter two amino acids (positions 112 and 158), producing three protein isoforms with distinct structural and functional properties.</p>
<p>APOE genotype is the strongest known genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's disease (LOAD). One copy of e4 increases Alzheimer's risk 3-4 fold, while two copies (e4/e4) increase risk 8-12 fold compared to the e3/e3 reference genotype. The e4 allele accelerates amyloid-beta aggregation, impairs amyloid clearance across the blood-brain barrier, promotes tau pathology, and disrupts synaptic function. Approximately 25% of the population carries at least one e4 allele.</p>
<p>The e2 allele, in contrast, is associated with reduced Alzheimer's risk and longevity, but increased risk of type III hyperlipoproteinemia and atherosclerosis at very high lipid levels. The e3 allele (most common, ~78% allele frequency) is considered the neutral reference. Beyond neurodegeneration, APOE genotype influences cardiovascular disease risk through its effects on LDL cholesterol metabolism, response to dietary fat, and statin therapy efficacy.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>APOE genotype is a risk modifier, not a deterministic test -- many e4 carriers never develop Alzheimer's, and many non-carriers do</li>
<li>Risk estimates vary by ethnicity, sex, and environmental factors</li>
<li>Clinical guidelines do not recommend routine APOE testing for asymptomatic individuals</li>
<li>Testing is most useful in the context of cognitive symptoms or when evaluating patients for anti-amyloid therapies</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Risk Expression</h2>
<p><strong>Factors that amplify e4 risk:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Sedentary lifestyle and physical inactivity</li>
<li>Western diet high in saturated fat and processed foods</li>
<li>Poor sleep quality and untreated sleep apnea</li>
<li>Midlife hypertension and diabetes</li>
<li>Low cognitive reserve (limited education, social isolation)</li>
<li>History of traumatic brain injury</li>
<li>Female sex (e4 risk is stronger in women)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Factors that attenuate e4 risk:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Regular aerobic exercise (150+ minutes/week, strongest modifiable protective factor)</li>
<li>Mediterranean or MIND dietary pattern</li>
<li>Cognitive engagement and lifelong learning</li>
<li>Adequate sleep (7-8 hours, particularly slow-wave sleep for glymphatic clearance)</li>
<li>Social engagement and strong social networks</li>
<li>Omega-3 supplementation (e4 carriers show differential response)</li>
<li>Midlife cardiovascular risk factor management</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Alzheimer's disease:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>APOE4 is the strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset AD, accounting for ~25% of population-attributable risk</li>
<li>e4 carriers develop Alzheimer's pathology 5-10 years earlier than non-carriers</li>
<li>APOE4 modifies response to anti-amyloid therapies (lecanemab, donanemab) -- e4 carriers may respond differently</li>
<li>The e4 allele accelerates amyloid deposition and tau spread through impaired clearance mechanisms</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cardiovascular disease:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>e4 carriers have higher LDL cholesterol and slightly higher cardiovascular event rates</li>
<li>Differential response to dietary fat: e4 carriers are more sensitive to saturated fat intake</li>
<li>Statin response may vary: some evidence suggests e4 carriers have less LDL reduction with certain statins</li>
<li>e2 carriers have lower LDL but may develop type III hyperlipoproteinemia (rare)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Neurological conditions:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>APOE4 increases risk of cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA) and intracerebral hemorrhage</li>
<li>APOE4 is associated with worse outcomes after traumatic brain injury and stroke</li>
<li>APOE2 may be protective against Parkinson's disease progression</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Risk stratification in patients with cognitive symptoms</li>
<li>Treatment eligibility assessment for anti-amyloid therapies</li>
<li>Pharmacogenomic guidance for lipid-lowering therapy</li>
<li>Family counseling when a relative has been diagnosed with APOE-associated conditions</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Mitigate e4 Risk</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Regular aerobic exercise</strong> -- The single most evidence-based intervention for e4 carriers; 150+ minutes/week of moderate-intensity exercise may reduce amyloid accumulation and improve cognitive outcomes</li>
<li><strong>Mediterranean or MIND diet</strong> -- Plant-rich, low-saturated-fat diet; e4 carriers benefit more from dietary intervention than non-carriers</li>
<li><strong>Sleep optimization</strong> -- 7-8 hours of quality sleep; treat sleep apnea aggressively (e4 carriers have worse outcomes with untreated apnea)</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 fatty acids</strong> -- 1-2 g/day DHA; note that e4 carriers show differential (sometimes less favorable) response to DHA supplementation compared to non-carriers</li>
<li><strong>B-complex vitamins</strong> -- Especially B6, B12, and folate to maintain low homocysteine; high homocysteine amplifies e4 risk for cognitive decline</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive engagement</strong> -- Lifelong learning, bilingualism, social interaction, and mentally stimulating hobbies build cognitive reserve</li>
<li><strong>Aggressive cardiovascular risk management</strong> -- Control blood pressure, glucose, and lipids starting in midlife; e4 carriers derive particular benefit from statin therapy</li>
</ol>
