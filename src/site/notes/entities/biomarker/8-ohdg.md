<h1>8-OHdG</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>8-hydroxy-2'-deoxyguanosine (8-OHdG) is a modified nucleoside produced when hydroxyl radicals and other reactive oxygen species attack the C-8 position of the guanine base in DNA. Because guanine has the lowest oxidation potential of the four nucleobases, 8-OHdG formation is both the most frequent and most mutagenic oxidative DNA lesion, capable of mispairing with adenine and causing G-to-T transversions during replication.</p>
<p>Urinary 8-OHdG reflects the net balance between oxidative DNA damage and the base excision repair pathway (primarily via OGG1, the 8-oxoguanine glycosylase). It serves as a non-invasive, integrative biomarker of whole-body oxidative stress over preceding hours to days. Elevated levels have been documented in cardiovascular disease, diabetes, cancer, neurodegenerative disorders, and chronic kidney disease, as well as in individuals exposed to environmental pollutants, cigarette smoke, and radiation.</p>
<p>Clinically, 8-OHdG is used to assess the efficacy of antioxidant interventions, monitor occupational or environmental genotoxic exposure, and stratify risk in chronic inflammatory and metabolic conditions. It complements other oxidative stress biomarkers such as F2-isoprostanes (lipid peroxidation) and malondialdehyde.</p>

<ul>
<li>Ranges vary by assay method (ELISA, HPLC-ECD, LC-MS/MS). LC-MS/MS is considered the gold standard.</li>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase 8-OHdG:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Ionizing radiation and UV exposure (direct DNA oxidation via hydroxyl radical formation)</li>
<li>Cigarette smoking (contains free radicals and pro-oxidant compounds)</li>
<li>Environmental pollutants — particulate matter, benzene, heavy metals (induce ROS generation)</li>
<li>Intense or prolonged physical exercise (transient increase from elevated oxygen consumption)</li>
<li>Chronic inflammatory conditions (ROS production by activated immune cells)</li>
<li>Chemotherapy and radiation therapy (DNA-damaging treatments)</li>
<li>Poor diet low in antioxidants (reduced scavenging capacity)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease 8-OHdG:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Adequate dietary intake of antioxidants (vitamins C, E, polyphenols)</li>
<li>Regular moderate exercise (upregulates endogenous antioxidant enzymes)</li>
<li>Caloric restriction and intermittent fasting (reduced mitochondrial ROS production)</li>
<li>Supplementation with CoQ10, NAC, or alpha-lipoic acid (enhanced antioxidant defense)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular Disease</strong> -- oxidative damage to vascular endothelial DNA contributes to atherosclerosis progression; 8-OHdG correlates with plaque instability (PMID: 12665478)</li>
<li><strong>Cancer</strong> -- cumulative oxidative DNA damage drives mutagenesis; elevated 8-OHdG is documented in patients with lung, breast, colorectal, and prostate cancers (PMID: 15734201)</li>
<li><strong>Alzheimer's Disease</strong> -- increased 8-OHdG in brain tissue and body fluids reflects neuronal oxidative damage and mitochondrial dysfunction (PMID: 11880493)</li>
<li><strong>Diabetes</strong> -- hyperglycemia-induced ROS generation elevates 8-OHdG; associated with diabetic complications including nephropathy and retinopathy (PMID: 14506253)</li>
<li><strong>Chronic Kidney Disease</strong> -- impaired clearance and increased oxidative stress both contribute to elevated urinary and plasma 8-OHdG (PMID: 15721892)</li>
<li><strong>Non-alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease</strong> -- hepatic oxidative stress from lipid accumulation increases systemic 8-OHdG levels</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Evaluating the efficacy of antioxidant supplementation protocols</li>
<li>Assessing occupational exposure to genotoxic chemicals and heavy metals</li>
<li>Monitoring oxidative stress burden in chronic inflammatory diseases</li>
<li>Stratifying cancer risk in high-risk populations</li>
<li>Tracking DNA damage response during and after chemotherapy</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Antioxidant supplementation</strong> -- Coenzyme Q10 (100-300 mg/day) has been shown to reduce urinary 8-OHdG by 20-30% in clinical trials. Vitamin C (500-1000 mg/day) and Vitamin E (200-400 IU/day) provide additive benefit.</li>
<li><strong>NAC supplementation</strong> -- N-acetylcysteine (600-1200 mg/day) replenishes glutathione, the primary intracellular antioxidant, and has demonstrated reductions in 8-OHdG in oxidative stress conditions (PMID: 16380549).</li>
<li><strong>Alpha-lipoic acid</strong> -- 300-600 mg/day reduces mitochondrial ROS production and has shown benefit in diabetic patients with elevated 8-OHdG (PMID: 15669844).</li>
<li><strong>Dietary polyphenols</strong> -- Regular consumption of curcumin (500-1000 mg/day), green tea catechins, and berries rich in anthocyanins provides direct free radical scavenging and upregulates endogenous antioxidant enzymes via Nrf2 activation.</li>
<li><strong>Regular moderate exercise</strong> -- 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity upregulates superoxide dismutase, catalase, and glutathione peroxidase, lowering baseline 8-OHdG.</li>
<li><strong>Reduce environmental exposures</strong> -- Minimize cigarette smoke, air pollution exposure, and unnecessary radiation; use air filtration in high-pollution areas.</li>
</ol>
