<h1>CEA</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) is a 180-200 kDa cell surface glycoprotein belonging to the immunoglobulin superfamily, first identified in 1965 from human colon carcinoma extracts. It is normally produced during fetal development by epithelial cells of the gastrointestinal tract and pancreas, with minimal expression in healthy adults. In malignancy, CEA expression is reactivated, particularly in colorectal adenocarcinoma, where it serves as the primary serum biomarker for treatment monitoring and recurrence detection.</p>
<p>CEA is not recommended for population-based cancer screening due to its limited sensitivity (40-50% for early-stage colorectal cancer) and poor specificity — elevated levels occur in numerous non-malignant conditions including smoking, inflammatory bowel disease, liver disease, pancreatitis, and even benign respiratory infections. Its clinical value lies in serial monitoring: a rising CEA trend in a patient with known colorectal cancer has a positive predictive value of 65-85% for recurrence, often preceding clinical or radiographic detection by 2-6 months.</p>
<p>Current NCCN and ASCO guidelines recommend CEA testing every 3-6 months for 5 years after curative-intent colorectal cancer surgery, alongside periodic imaging. Pre-operative CEA levels also carry prognostic significance: a preoperative CEA &gt; 5 ng/mL is associated with worse disease-free and overall survival independent of tumor stage.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Approximately 15-20% of colorectal cancers never produce CEA; these are &quot;non-secretors.&quot;</li>
<li>CEA is less useful for mucinous and signet-ring cell colorectal cancer subtypes.</li>
<li>Benign elevations are common: 15-20% of smokers, patients with IBD flares, liver disease, and infections.</li>
<li>Serial trend interpretation is more valuable than any single value. A sustained rise &gt; 25% over 3 consecutive measurements suggests recurrence.</li>
<li>Post-operative CEA should normalize within 4-6 weeks if complete resection was achieved.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase CEA:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Colorectal cancer (most common malignant cause; 60-70% of cases produce elevated CEA)</li>
<li>Other GI malignancies — gastric, pancreatic, biliary, esophageal</li>
<li>Non-GI malignancies — breast, lung, thyroid medullary carcinoma</li>
<li>Cigarette smoking (chronic inflammation of bronchial epithelium; 15-20% of smokers have mildly elevated CEA)</li>
<li>Inflammatory bowel disease (mucosal inflammation and epithelial turnover)</li>
<li>Liver disease — cirrhosis, hepatitis (impaired hepatic clearance of CEA)</li>
<li>Acute and chronic pancreatitis</li>
<li>Benign respiratory conditions — pneumonia, bronchitis</li>
<li>Renal failure (reduced clearance)</li>
<li>Hypothyroidism</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease CEA:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Effective chemotherapy or radiation therapy response</li>
<li>Complete surgical resection of tumor (normalization within 4-6 weeks)</li>
<li>Smoking cessation (levels gradually decline over months)</li>
<li>Resolution of inflammatory conditions</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Colorectal Cancer</strong> -- the primary clinical application; preoperative CEA &gt; 5 ng/mL is an independent poor prognostic factor; rising post-operative CEA predicts recurrence 2-6 months before imaging (PMID: 20484222)</li>
<li><strong>Other Gastrointestinal Cancers</strong> -- gastric, pancreatic, and biliary tract malignancies produce CEA through reactivation of fetal gene expression</li>
<li><strong>Breast and Lung Cancer</strong> -- moderate CEA elevations occur in 30-50% of metastatic breast cancer and 40-60% of advanced non-small cell lung cancer</li>
<li><strong>Liver Disease</strong> -- cirrhosis and hepatitis impair CEA clearance from circulation, producing mild elevations (typically &lt; 10 ng/mL)</li>
<li><strong>Pancreatitis</strong> -- acute pancreatic inflammation causes transient CEA elevation from acinar cell damage (PMID: 19033598)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reduced levels are associated with:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Favorable treatment response (CEA normalization after surgery indicates complete resection)</li>
<li>Disease remission</li>
<li>Low tumor burden</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Post-operative surveillance for colorectal cancer recurrence (NCCN/ASCO recommended)</li>
<li>Assessing response to chemotherapy in metastatic colorectal cancer</li>
<li>Pre-operative prognostic stratification</li>
<li>Evaluating disease progression in other CEA-producing malignancies</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Manage</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Appropriate oncological treatment</strong> -- The most effective way to reduce CEA is treating the underlying malignancy. Surgical resection, chemotherapy (FOLFOX, FOLFIRI), and targeted therapies (bevacizumab, cetuximab) reduce tumor burden and CEA levels accordingly.</li>
<li><strong>Curcumin supplementation</strong> -- 2-3 g/day of standardized curcumin has shown chemopreventive properties in colorectal cancer models and may modestly reduce CEA in adjunctive settings (PMID: 20484222).</li>
<li><strong>Vitamin D optimization</strong> -- Maintaining serum 25(OH)D &gt; 40 ng/mL is associated with improved colorectal cancer outcomes in observational studies; vitamin D may reduce proliferation of malignant epithelial cells (PMID: 20040488).</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation</strong> -- 1-2 g/day of EPA/DHA has anti-inflammatory properties that may modulate tumor-associated inflammation (PMID: 19033598).</li>
<li><strong>Smoking cessation</strong> -- Cigarette smoking is a common cause of benign CEA elevation and a known risk factor for colorectal cancer. Cessation removes a confounding factor and reduces cancer risk.</li>
<li><strong>Regular surveillance</strong> -- Adhering to post-treatment CEA monitoring schedules (every 3 months for 2 years, then every 6 months) enables early detection of recurrence when treatment is most effective.</li>
</ol>
