<h1>Alkaline Phosphatase</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) is a zinc-dependent metalloenzyme that hydrolyzes phosphate esters at alkaline pH. It is located on the outer surface of cell membranes and is found in high concentrations in the liver (canalicular membrane of hepatocytes and biliary epithelium), bone (osteoblast cell membrane), intestinal mucosa, kidney, and placenta. The enzyme requires zinc and magnesium as cofactors.</p>
<p>Serum ALP reflects the combined activity of multiple tissue-specific isoenzymes. The two dominant contributors in healthy adults are the liver isoform (~50-60%) and the bone isoform (~40-50%). In clinical practice, an isolated ALP elevation raises the differential between hepatobiliary disease and bone disease, which is resolved by measuring GGT (elevated in liver but not bone disease) or ALP isoenzyme fractionation.</p>
<p>ALP is particularly valuable for detecting cholestatic liver disease and biliary obstruction. In these conditions, ALP rises before bilirubin because biliary pressure induces ALP synthesis (not just release from damaged cells), and the enzyme is partially regurgitated into sinusoidal blood. In bone, ALP reflects osteoblast activity and is elevated in conditions of high bone turnover.</p>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase ALP:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Biliary obstruction (choledocholithiasis, cholangiocarcinoma, pancreatic head mass)</li>
<li>Intrahepatic cholestasis (primary biliary cholangitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, drug-induced)</li>
<li>Bone disorders with high turnover (Paget disease, fractures, osteomalacia, bone metastases, hyperparathyroidism)</li>
<li>Pregnancy (3rd trimester -- placental isoenzyme)</li>
<li>Childhood and adolescence (physiological bone growth)</li>
<li>Medications (phenytoin, carbamazepine, rifampin, oral contraceptives)</li>
<li>Liver metastases (tumor compresses bile ducts)</li>
<li>Lymphoma and leukemia (hepatic infiltration)</li>
<li>Healing fractures (transient rise from osteoblast activity)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease ALP:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Zinc deficiency (ALP is a zinc metalloenzyme)</li>
<li>Magnesium deficiency (required cofactor)</li>
<li>Hypothyroidism (reduced bone turnover)</li>
<li>Malnutrition and protein deficiency</li>
<li>Pernicious anemia (vitamin B12 deficiency)</li>
<li>Celiac disease</li>
<li>Wilson disease (copper displaces zinc at active site)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated ALP indicates:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Biliary obstruction</strong> -- ALP rises 3-10x in extrahepatic biliary obstruction; the degree of elevation often correlates with the degree of obstruction. GGT rises in parallel, confirming hepatic origin</li>
<li><strong>Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)</strong> -- ALP is the hallmark enzyme, often elevated 2-10x with positive AMA (anti-mitochondrial antibodies). ALP normalization with UDCA treatment predicts favorable prognosis</li>
<li><strong>Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC)</strong> -- ALP elevation with cholangiographic bile duct strictures; associated with inflammatory bowel disease</li>
<li><strong>Paget disease of bone</strong> -- ALP elevated 3-25x normal with characteristic radiographic findings. Level correlates with disease activity and response to bisphosphonate therapy</li>
<li><strong>Bone metastases</strong> -- Prostate, breast, and lung cancer metastatic to bone cause ALP elevation from osteoblastic reaction</li>
<li><strong>Osteomalacia and rickets</strong> -- Vitamin D deficiency causes elevated ALP from increased osteoblast activity attempting to mineralize bone</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Diagnostic approach to isolated ALP elevation:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li>Check GGT -- if elevated, confirms liver origin; if normal, suggests bone origin</li>
<li>Check 5'-nucleotidase -- liver-specific alternative to GGT</li>
<li>ALP isoenzyme fractionation -- separates liver, bone, intestinal, and placental isoforms</li>
<li>Correlate with clinical context, other liver enzymes, calcium, vitamin D</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Detecting biliary obstruction and cholestasis</li>
<li>Monitoring treatment response in PBC and PSC</li>
<li>Assessing Paget disease activity and bisphosphonate response</li>
<li>Screening for bone metastases in known malignancy</li>
<li>Evaluating drug-induced liver injury (cholestatic pattern)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Manage Abnormal ALP</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Determine source</strong> -- Always check GGT alongside ALP to distinguish liver from bone origin</li>
<li><strong>Treat underlying biliary disease</strong> -- Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) for PBC; endoscopic intervention for biliary obstruction</li>
<li><strong>Address vitamin D deficiency</strong> -- Replete with vitamin D3 (2000-5000 IU/day) and calcium if osteomalacia is the cause</li>
<li><strong>Zinc and magnesium optimization</strong> -- Deficiency of either cofactor can lower ALP; supplement if levels are low (zinc 15-30 mg/day, magnesium 200-400 mg/day)</li>
<li><strong>Review medications</strong> -- Phenytoin, carbamazepine, and many drugs induce ALP; discuss alternatives with prescribing physician</li>
<li><strong>Paget disease management</strong> -- Bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid, alendronate) reduce bone turnover and normalize ALP</li>
<li><strong>Liver diet and lifestyle</strong> -- Alcohol reduction, weight management, and avoiding hepatotoxins support hepatic ALP normalization</li>
</ol>
