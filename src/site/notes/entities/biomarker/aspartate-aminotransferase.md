<h1>Aspartate Aminotransferase</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Aspartate Aminotransferase (AST), also known as serum glutamic-oxaloacetic transaminase (SGOT), is a pyridoxal phosphate-dependent enzyme that catalyzes the reversible transamination between aspartate and alpha-ketoglutarate to form oxaloacetate and glutamate. Unlike ALT, which is predominantly liver-specific, AST is distributed across multiple tissues: liver (highest concentration per gram), heart, skeletal muscle, kidney, brain, pancreas, and red blood cells.</p>
<p>Because of its multi-organ distribution, an isolated AST elevation is less specific than ALT for liver injury and requires clinical correlation to identify the source tissue. The AST/ALT ratio (De Ritis ratio) is a key diagnostic tool: a ratio &gt;2.0 suggests alcoholic liver disease or cirrhosis, a ratio &lt;1.0 suggests viral hepatitis or NAFLD, and very high ratios (&gt;5.0) with minimal ALT elevation suggest extrahepatic sources (myocardial infarction, rhabdomyolysis, or hemolysis).</p>
<p>AST exists as two isoenzymes: mitochondrial AST (mAST) and cytoplasmic AST (cAST). The mitochondrial isoform is released with more severe or deeper cellular injury (necrosis), while the cytoplasmic isoform is released with milder membrane damage. Although isoenzyme fractionation is rarely performed clinically, the principle explains why proportionally higher AST relative to ALT indicates more severe hepatocellular damage.</p>

<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Always interpret alongside ALT to calculate the De Ritis ratio</li>
<li>Mild isolated AST elevation with normal ALT suggests non-hepatic source (muscle, heart, hemolysis)</li>
<li>AST elevations from myocardial infarction peak at 24-48 hours and normalize by day 4-5</li>
<li>Hemolysis during blood draw can falsely elevate AST (RBCs contain AST)</li>
<li>Reference ranges may vary slightly by laboratory</li>
</ul>

<h2>Factors Affecting Levels</h2>
<p><strong>Increase AST:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis, hepatocellular carcinoma, ischemic hepatitis)</li>
<li>Myocardial infarction (peaks 24-48 hours post-MI)</li>
<li>Skeletal muscle injury (trauma, rhabdomyolysis, strenuous exercise, polymyositis)</li>
<li>Hemolytic anemia (RBCs contain AST)</li>
<li>Alcohol consumption (AST typically &gt; ALT; De Ritis ratio &gt;2.0)</li>
<li>Medications (statins, acetaminophen, NSAIDs, antibiotics, anticonvulsants)</li>
<li>Pancreatitis and renal infarction</li>
<li>Thyroid disorders (hyperthyroidism can cause mild elevation)</li>
<li>Supplements (ashwagandha, high-dose vitamin A, kava)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Decrease AST:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) deficiency -- AST requires B6 as cofactor</li>
<li>End-stage liver disease (insufficient hepatocyte mass)</li>
<li>Chronic renal dialysis (removes the enzyme)</li>
<li>Uremia</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated AST indicates:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Alcoholic liver disease</strong> -- AST is disproportionately elevated vs ALT (De Ritis ratio &gt;2.0) due to alcohol-induced mitochondrial damage releasing mAST and pyridoxal phosphate depletion preferentially affecting ALT. GGT is typically co-elevated</li>
<li><strong>Viral hepatitis</strong> -- Both AST and ALT markedly elevated (&gt;1000 U/L in acute infection), with ALT &gt; AST initially; the ratio may reverse as recovery progresses</li>
<li><strong>NAFLD/NASH</strong> -- Mild-moderate elevation with ALT &gt; AST; AST predominance suggests progression to advanced fibrosis or cirrhosis</li>
<li><strong>Myocardial infarction</strong> -- AST rises 6-12 hours post-MI, peaks at 24-48 hours. Largely replaced by troponin for MI diagnosis but still appears in standard panels</li>
<li><strong>Rhabdomyolysis</strong> -- Marked AST elevation with disproportionately higher CK (often &gt;10,000 U/L); AST:CK ratio &lt;1:10 suggests muscle origin</li>
<li><strong>Cirrhosis</strong> -- AST &gt; ALT (ratio &gt;1.0) reflects progression from hepatocellular injury to fibrosis; a rising De Ritis ratio over time signals worsening liver disease</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Monitoring use cases:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Liver disease activity and progression monitoring</li>
<li>Alcoholic liver disease screening (AST/ALT ratio + GGT)</li>
<li>Hepatotoxic medication surveillance</li>
<li>Post-MI enzyme tracking (historical; troponin preferred)</li>
<li>Rhabdomyolysis and muscle injury assessment</li>
</ul>
<h2>Strategies to Improve</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Reduce alcohol intake</strong> -- The most impactful intervention for alcoholic AST elevation; abstinence normalizes AST within weeks in uncomplicated alcoholic hepatitis</li>
<li><strong>Treat underlying liver disease</strong> -- Antiviral therapy for hepatitis B/C, weight loss for NAFLD, alcohol cessation for alcoholic liver disease</li>
<li><strong>NAC supplementation</strong> -- 600-1200 mg/day supports glutathione and hepatocyte protection (evidence level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Curcumin supplementation</strong> -- 500-1000 mg/day has shown transaminase reduction in NAFLD trials (evidence level 3)</li>
<li><strong>Review medications and supplements</strong> -- Evaluate statins, ashwagandha, kava, high-dose vitamin A for hepatotoxicity; check CK if statin-related</li>
<li><strong>Exercise modification</strong> -- Avoid extreme exercise that causes rhabdomyolysis; moderate exercise improves liver health</li>
<li><strong>Coffee consumption</strong> -- 2-3 cups/day associated with lower transaminases and reduced liver fibrosis risk</li>
</ol>
