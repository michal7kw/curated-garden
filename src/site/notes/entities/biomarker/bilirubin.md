<h1>Bilirubin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Bilirubin is a tetrapyrrole pigment and a breakdown product of heme catabolism. Most bilirubin (70-90%) is derived from the hemoglobin of senescent red blood cells, with the remainder coming from ineffective erythropoiesis and the turnover of other heme-containing proteins (such as cytochromes and myoglobin).</p>
<p>The metabolism of bilirubin involves several stages:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Production:</strong> Hemoglobin is broken down into heme, then biliverdin, and finally unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin.</li>
<li><strong>Transport:</strong> Unconjugated bilirubin is water-insoluble and travels in the blood bound to <a href="/garden/michal.k7w@gmail.com/labtest/albumin/" class="internal-link">Albumin</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Conjugation:</strong> In the liver, the enzyme UGT1A1 adds glucuronic acid to bilirubin, making it water-soluble (conjugated or direct bilirubin).</li>
<li><strong>Excretion:</strong> Conjugated bilirubin is secreted into bile, enters the intestines, and is eventually eliminated in the stool (as stercobilin) or urine (as urobilin).</li>
</ol>
<p>Clinically, bilirubin is used to diagnose and monitor liver diseases, biliary obstructions, and hemolytic anemias.</p>

<h2>Testing Information</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Measurement Unit</strong>: milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL) or micromoles per liter (µmol/L)</li>
<li><strong>Sample Type</strong>: Blood (Serum or Plasma)</li>
<li><strong>Fasting Required</strong>: False (though often part of a fasting CMP)</li>
<li><strong>Recommended Test Frequency</strong>: Annually as part of routine screening; more frequent if jaundice or liver/gallbladder symptoms are present.</li>
</ul>
<h2>💊 Supplements That Affect This Biomarker</h2>
<h3>Potentially Elevating Bilirubin (Hepatotoxic Risk)</h3>
<ul>
<li><a class="internal-link is-unresolved" href="/404">Anabolic Steroids</a> - Highly associated with cholestatic liver injury and jaundice (High magnitude, Evidence Level 4).</li>
<li><a class="internal-link is-unresolved" href="/404">Green Tea Extract</a> - High doses of EGCG can cause idiosyncratic liver injury leading to elevated bilirubin (Variable magnitude, Evidence Level 3).</li>
<li><a class="internal-link is-unresolved" href="/404">Kava</a> - Associated with rare but severe hepatotoxicity.</li>
<li><a class="internal-link is-unresolved" href="/404">Vitamin A</a> - Chronic high doses can lead to liver damage and impaired bilirubin clearance.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Factors Decreasing Bilirubin</h3>
<ul>
<li><a class="internal-link is-unresolved" href="/404">Caffeine</a> - High intake has been associated with slightly lower serum bilirubin levels (Mild magnitude, Evidence Level 2).</li>
<li><strong>Phenobarbital</strong> - A medication (not a supplement) that induces conjugation enzymes, often used to lower bilirubin in specific neonatal or genetic conditions.</li>
</ul>
<h2>🧪 Lab Tests That Measure This Biomarker</h2>
<h3>Direct Tests</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Bilirubin Total</strong> - Measures both conjugated and unconjugated forms.</li>
<li><strong>Bilirubin Direct</strong> - Specifically measures the conjugated, water-soluble form.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Panel Tests</h3>
<ul>
<li><a class="internal-link is-unresolved" href="/404">Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)</a> - Includes Total Bilirubin.</li>
<li><a href="/garden/michal.k7w@gmail.com/labtest/liver-function-panel/" class="internal-link">Liver Function Panel</a> - Typically includes Total and Direct Bilirubin.</li>
</ul>
