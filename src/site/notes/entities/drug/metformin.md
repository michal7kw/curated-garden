<h1>METFORMIN</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p><strong>Metformin</strong> (dimethylbiguanide) is the most widely prescribed antidiabetic drug in the world, with over 150 million prescriptions annually. A member of the biguanide class derived from the French lilac plant (<em>Galega officinalis</em>), metformin has been the <strong>first-line pharmacotherapy for type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2D)</strong> since its FDA approval in 1995 (available in Europe since 1957). Its enduring clinical dominance stems from a unique combination of efficacy, safety, weight neutrality, cardiovascular benefit, low cost, and an extraordinarily favorable risk-benefit profile supported by decades of clinical experience.</p>
<p>Beyond diabetes, metformin has emerged as one of the most intensely studied drugs in <strong>longevity and aging research</strong>. Epidemiological observations consistently show that metformin-treated diabetic patients have lower all-cause mortality than non-diabetic controls, lower cancer incidence, and reduced cardiovascular events. These observations led to the design of the <strong>TAME (Targeting Aging with Metformin) trial</strong>, the first FDA-approved clinical trial to test a drug specifically for its effects on aging-related outcomes in non-diabetic older adults.</p>
<p>Metformin is notable in pharmacogenomics for what it is <em>not</em>: it is <strong>not metabolized by cytochrome P450 enzymes</strong>. Instead, it is eliminated unchanged by the kidneys via organic cation transporters (OCT2 for renal uptake, MATE1/MATE2-K for tubular secretion). This means CYP genotyping is irrelevant for metformin, but polymorphisms in SLC22A2 (OCT2) and SLC47A1 (MATE1) can influence metformin pharmacokinetics and response.</p>
<h2>Pharmacological Information</h2>
<h3>Mechanism of Action</h3>
<p>Metformin's mechanism of action is multifaceted and still being refined after 60+ years of use:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Mitochondrial Complex I Inhibition</strong>: Metformin accumulates in mitochondria and mildly inhibits Complex I of the electron transport chain, reducing ATP production and increasing the AMP:ATP ratio. This is considered the primary initiating event.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>AMPK Activation</strong>: The increased AMP:ATP ratio activates AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), the master cellular energy sensor. Activated AMPK switches off energy-consuming anabolic pathways (gluconeogenesis, lipogenesis, protein synthesis) and switches on energy-producing catabolic pathways (fatty acid oxidation, glucose uptake).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Hepatic Gluconeogenesis Suppression</strong>: The primary antidiabetic effect. Metformin reduces hepatic glucose output by 25-30% through AMPK-mediated suppression of gluconeogenic gene expression (PEPCK, G6Pase) and direct effects on the mitochondrial glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Improved Peripheral Insulin Sensitivity</strong>: Metformin enhances insulin-stimulated glucose uptake in skeletal muscle, partly through AMPK-mediated GLUT4 translocation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Reduced Intestinal Glucose Absorption</strong>: Metformin increases intestinal glucose utilization and may increase GLP-1 secretion, contributing to its glucose-lowering and appetite effects.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>mTOR Pathway Inhibition</strong>: AMPK activation leads to indirect inhibition of mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1) through TSC2 phosphorylation. This anti-growth signaling is hypothesized to contribute to metformin's potential anti-cancer and anti-aging effects.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<h3>Pharmacokinetics</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Absorption</strong>: 50-60% oral bioavailability; absorbed primarily in the small intestine via OCT1 and PMAT transporters</li>
<li><strong>Distribution</strong>: Widely distributed; concentrates in GI mucosa, liver, kidneys. Negligible protein binding</li>
<li><strong>Metabolism</strong>: <strong>NOT metabolized</strong>. Excreted unchanged in urine</li>
<li><strong>Elimination</strong>: Renal clearance via OCT2 (basolateral uptake) and MATE1/MATE2-K (apical secretion). t1/2 ~4-9 hours (plasma), longer in erythrocytes</li>
<li><strong>Dose</strong>: 500-2550 mg/day in divided doses; extended-release formulations available</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Features</h2>
<h3>Type 2 Diabetes</h3>
<p>The <strong>UKPDS (UK Prospective Diabetes Study)</strong> established metformin's position as first-line therapy. In overweight T2D patients, metformin reduced diabetes-related mortality by 42%, all-cause mortality by 36%, and any diabetes-related endpoint by 32% compared to conventional therapy. It achieves HbA1c reductions of 1.0-1.5% as monotherapy.</p>
<h3>Longevity and Anti-Aging</h3>
<p>The TAME trial (NCT02432287) is enrolling 3,000 adults aged 65-79 to test whether metformin 1500 mg/day delays the onset of age-related conditions (cardiovascular disease, cancer, dementia, mortality) over 6 years. The biological rationale includes AMPK activation, mTOR inhibition, reduced oxidative stress, anti-inflammatory effects, and improved cellular stress responses. Population studies suggest 3-15% reduced mortality in metformin users compared to matched non-diabetic controls.</p>
<h3>Cancer Prevention</h3>
<p>Multiple observational studies and meta-analyses report 10-40% reduced cancer incidence in metformin users across breast, colorectal, prostate, and lung cancers. Proposed mechanisms include AMPK-mediated mTOR suppression (anti-proliferative), reduced insulin/IGF-1 signaling, and direct effects on cancer cell metabolism. Randomized trials are ongoing.</p>
<h3>Vitamin B12 Deficiency</h3>
<p>Long-term metformin use (&gt;4 years) causes vitamin B12 deficiency in 10-30% of patients through impaired calcium-dependent B12-intrinsic factor complex absorption in the terminal ileum. Annual B12 monitoring is recommended. Supplementation with 1000 mcg oral B12 prevents deficiency.</p>
<h2>Classification</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Drug Class</strong>: Biguanide (dimethylbiguanide)</li>
<li><strong>Therapeutic Category</strong>: Oral antihyperglycemic agent</li>
<li><strong>FDA Pregnancy Category</strong>: B (being revised under new labeling system)</li>
<li><strong>DEA Schedule</strong>: Not scheduled</li>
<li><strong>Available Forms</strong>: Immediate-release tablets (500, 850, 1000 mg), extended-release tablets (500, 750, 1000 mg), oral solution (500 mg/5 mL)</li>
</ul>
