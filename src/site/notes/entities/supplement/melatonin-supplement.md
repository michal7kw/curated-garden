<h1>Melatonin Supplement</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Melatonin (N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine) is an indoleamine neurohormone synthesized from the amino acid tryptophan via a two-step enzymatic pathway in the pineal gland. Its secretion is tightly regulated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) — the brain's master circadian clock — with production beginning at dusk, peaking between 2:00-4:00 AM, and declining to near-zero levels by morning. Light exposure (particularly blue wavelengths, 460-480 nm) suppresses melatonin production via the retinohypothalamic tract, a mechanism critical for circadian entrainment.</p>
<p>As a dietary supplement, exogenous melatonin is one of the most widely used and well-studied sleep aids worldwide. A landmark meta-analysis (Brzezinski et al., 2005) of 17 studies demonstrated significant reductions in sleep onset latency and increased sleep efficiency, with effect sizes comparable to some prescription hypnotics for sleep initiation. The Cochrane Collaboration review (Herxheimer &amp; Petrie, 2002) found melatonin &quot;remarkably effective&quot; for preventing and treating jet lag in 9 of 10 randomized trials. Notably, low physiological doses (0.3-1 mg) are often as effective as higher pharmacological doses for sleep onset, challenging the common misconception that &quot;more is better.&quot;</p>
<p>Beyond sleep, melatonin is a uniquely versatile molecule: it is one of the most potent endogenous antioxidants known, accumulating in mitochondria at concentrations approximately 100 times higher than plasma. It scavenges hydroxyl radicals directly, upregulates endogenous antioxidant enzymes, and inhibits pro-inflammatory NF-kB signaling. These properties have generated substantial research interest in neuroprotection, cardiovascular health, and immune modulation.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level: 5/5</strong> — Strong evidence from multiple meta-analyses and Cochrane review for sleep and jet lag applications. Extensive mechanistic evidence for antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Melatonin exerts its effects through receptor-mediated and non-receptor (direct antioxidant) mechanisms:</p>
<p><strong>1. MT1 Receptor Activation (Sleep Onset):</strong> MT1 (MTNR1A) receptors are densely expressed in the SCN. Activation by melatonin suppresses SCN neuronal firing rate, reducing arousal signals and promoting sleep initiation. This is the primary mechanism by which exogenous melatonin reduces sleep onset latency.</p>
<p><strong>2. MT2 Receptor Activation (Circadian Entrainment):</strong> MT2 (MTNR1B) receptors mediate circadian phase-shifting. Melatonin binding advances or delays the circadian clock depending on timing — evening administration phase-advances the clock (earlier sleep), while morning administration phase-delays it. This is the basis for jet lag and shift-work applications.</p>
<p><strong>3. Direct Antioxidant Activity:</strong> Unlike most antioxidants, melatonin scavenges reactive oxygen species (hydroxyl, peroxyl, hydrogen peroxide) and reactive nitrogen species (peroxynitrite) without undergoing redox cycling that generates pro-oxidant intermediates. Its metabolite AFMK (N1-acetyl-N2-formyl-5-methoxykynuramine) is also an active antioxidant, creating a &quot;free radical scavenging cascade.&quot;</p>
<p><strong>4. Mitochondrial Protection:</strong> Melatonin accumulates in mitochondria at concentrations ~100x plasma levels. It maintains mitochondrial membrane potential, sustains electron transport chain efficiency, reduces mitochondrial ROS production, and inhibits cytochrome c release (preventing apoptosis). This mitochondrial targeting underlies neuroprotective effects.</p>
<p><strong>5. Anti-Inflammatory and Immunomodulatory:</strong> Melatonin suppresses NF-kB signaling and reduces pro-inflammatory cytokine production (TNF-alpha, IL-6, IL-1beta). It simultaneously enhances innate immunity by stimulating natural killer cell activity and T-helper cell function, creating a nuanced immunomodulatory profile.</p>
<p><strong>6. Thermoregulatory Effects:</strong> Melatonin modestly reduces core body temperature (~0.2-0.5°C) via peripheral vasodilation, contributing to sleep propensity. This hypothermic effect is part of the normal physiological sleep initiation cascade.</p>
<h2>Research Summary</h2>
<h3>Sleep Onset and Insomnia</h3>
<p>The foundational evidence for melatonin as a sleep aid comes from a comprehensive meta-analysis (Brzezinski et al., <em>Sleep Medicine Reviews</em>, 2005) of 17 double-blind placebo-controlled trials, which found significant reductions in sleep onset latency (mean decrease: 3.9 minutes), increased sleep duration (13.7 minutes), and improved sleep quality. A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis (Fatemeh et al., <em>Journal of Neurology</em>) confirmed these findings across a broader range of populations, establishing melatonin as effective for both primary and secondary insomnia. Critically, research demonstrates that low physiological doses (0.1-0.3 mg) produce equivalent or superior sleep onset effects compared to higher doses (5-10 mg), while causing fewer morning-after effects.</p>
<h3>Jet Lag and Circadian Rhythm Disorders</h3>
<p>The Cochrane Collaboration review (Herxheimer &amp; Petrie, 2002) of 10 trials with 639 participants found melatonin &quot;highly effective&quot; for preventing and reducing jet lag when taken at the target destination bedtime (0.5-5 mg). The beneficial effect was consistent across flight directions, though eastward travelers (requiring phase advance) benefited most. Evidence also supports melatonin for delayed sleep-wake phase disorder, shift-work disorder, and free-running circadian rhythm disorder in blind individuals.</p>
<h3>Antioxidant and Neuroprotective Effects</h3>
<p>A comprehensive review (Reiter et al., <em>Journal of Pineal Research</em>, 2016) detailed melatonin's extraordinary antioxidant capacity, emphasizing its mitochondrial accumulation and ability to resist oxidative stress-induced apoptosis. A 2025 review (Zhang et al., <em>Molecular Psychiatry</em>) examined melatonin as a &quot;nighttime guardian&quot; against Alzheimer's disease pathology, including amyloid-beta aggregation and tau phosphorylation, supported by both preclinical and early clinical evidence.</p>
<h3>Non-Sleep Applications</h3>
<p>A meta-analysis (Chen et al., <em>Journal of the Formosan Medical Association</em>, 2023) found melatonin significantly improved IBS severity scores, suggesting gut-brain axis benefits. A network meta-analysis (Tseng et al., <em>Journal of Pineal Research</em>, 2020) identified melatonin as comparable to pharmacotherapy for migraine prophylaxis. A systematic review (Lee et al., <em>Journal of Clinical Hypertension</em>, 2022) demonstrated that controlled-release melatonin reduced nocturnal blood pressure, potentially lowering cardiovascular risk.</p>
<h3>Safety Profile</h3>
<p>A broad review (Minich et al., <em>Nutrients</em>, 2022) concluded that melatonin has an excellent safety profile at typical supplemental doses, with no evidence of dependence, tolerance, or withdrawal. However, the authors noted significant variability in supplement quality, with many commercial products containing substantially more or less melatonin than labeled.</p>
<h2>Administration Guide</h2>
<h3>Dosage</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sleep onset:</strong> 0.3-1 mg (physiological replacement dose), 30-60 minutes before bed</li>
<li><strong>Sleep maintenance:</strong> 1-5 mg extended-release formulation</li>
<li><strong>Jet lag:</strong> 0.5-5 mg at destination bedtime on arrival and for 2-4 nights after</li>
<li><strong>Circadian phase-shifting:</strong> 0.3-3 mg timed according to desired phase shift</li>
<li><strong>Antioxidant / higher-dose applications:</strong> 5-20 mg (under clinical guidance)</li>
<li><strong>Important:</strong> Start with the lowest effective dose. Higher doses do NOT necessarily produce better sleep and increase morning grogginess risk</li>
</ul>
<h3>Timing and Food</h3>
<ul>
<li>30-60 minutes before desired sleep time for immediate-release</li>
<li>Take at the beginning of the desired &quot;biological night&quot;</li>
<li>For jet lag: take at destination bedtime, not at departure timezone bedtime</li>
<li>May be taken with or without food; food delays absorption but increases total exposure</li>
<li>Avoid bright light exposure (especially blue light) after taking melatonin</li>
</ul>
<h3>Form Selection</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Immediate-release tablets/capsules:</strong> Best for sleep onset; 0.3-3 mg</li>
<li><strong>Extended-release (sustained-release):</strong> Best for sleep maintenance / early awakening; 1-5 mg</li>
<li><strong>Sublingual tablets / liquid:</strong> Faster onset (15-30 min); bypasses first-pass metabolism</li>
<li><strong>Liposomal formulations:</strong> Enhanced bioavailability; useful for those with poor absorption</li>
<li><strong>Transdermal patches:</strong> Sustained delivery over 8-12 hours; avoids GI side effects</li>
<li><strong>Gummies:</strong> Convenient but often contain inaccurate doses; check third-party testing</li>
</ul>
<h3>Duration</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Jet lag:</strong> 2-4 nights at destination</li>
<li><strong>Shift work:</strong> Use on night shifts only; avoid daytime use</li>
<li><strong>Chronic insomnia:</strong> Can be used nightly long-term (no evidence of tolerance or dependence)</li>
<li><strong>Circadian entrainment:</strong> 1-3 months for stable phase-shifting</li>
</ul>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Autoimmune disorders:</strong> Melatonin stimulates immune function; theoretical risk of exacerbation</li>
<li><strong>Depression:</strong> May worsen dysphoric mood in some individuals (paradoxical response)</li>
<li><strong>Pregnancy and lactation:</strong> Insufficient safety data; avoid</li>
<li><strong>Children:</strong> Use only under medical supervision; may affect hormonal development</li>
<li><strong>Epilepsy:</strong> Conflicting reports on seizure threshold; use under neurologist supervision</li>
</ul>
<h3>Drug Interactions</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Warfarin / anticoagulants:</strong> Potential additive anticoagulant effect; monitor INR</li>
<li><strong>Immunosuppressants:</strong> Counteracts immunosuppression (cyclosporine, tacrolimus)</li>
<li><strong>CNS depressants:</strong> Additive sedation with benzodiazepines, alcohol, antihistamines</li>
<li><strong>CYP1A2 inhibitors:</strong> Fluvoxamine significantly increases melatonin levels (up to 20-fold); ciprofloxacin and caffeine also affect levels</li>
<li><strong>Antidiabetic medications:</strong> May affect glucose metabolism; monitor blood glucose</li>
<li><strong>Hormonal contraceptives:</strong> May increase endogenous melatonin levels additively</li>
<li><strong>Antihypertensives:</strong> May potentiate blood pressure-lowering effects</li>
</ul>

<h3>Monitoring Recommendations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Sleep diary to assess efficacy (onset latency, total sleep time, wake after sleep onset)</li>
<li>Morning alertness assessment (should not impair next-day function at proper dose)</li>
<li>Blood glucose monitoring in diabetic patients</li>
<li>INR monitoring if on warfarin</li>
<li>Mood monitoring in patients with depression history</li>
<li>Supplement quality: choose third-party tested products (actual melatonin content varies widely)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Quality Considerations</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Dose accuracy:</strong> Studies show commercial melatonin products vary from -83% to +478% of labeled content. Choose third-party tested brands (USP, NSF, ConsumerLab)</li>
<li><strong>Purity:</strong> Look for pharmaceutical-grade melatonin without unnecessary fillers</li>
<li><strong>Form-specific selection:</strong> Immediate-release for onset, extended-release for maintenance</li>
<li><strong>Source:</strong> Synthetic melatonin preferred over animal-derived (risk of viral contamination)</li>
<li><strong>Storage:</strong> Protect from light and heat; melatonin is photosensitive and degrades with UV exposure</li>
<li><strong>Gummy products:</strong> Convenient but least reliable for dose accuracy</li>
<li><strong>Avoid products with:</strong> Added serine, valerian, or other sedatives unless intentionally combining</li>
</ul>
