<h1>Spirulina</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Spirulina (<em>Arthrospira platensis</em>) is a nutrient-dense cyanobacterium (blue-green alga) that contains 60-70% protein by weight, making it one of the most protein-rich whole foods on Earth. It provides a comprehensive nutritional profile including B vitamins (especially a B12 analogue, though bioavailability in humans is debated), iron, manganese, gamma-linolenic acid (GLA), and beta-carotene. Its signature bioactive compound, C-phycocyanin (the blue pigment), is a potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory agent that inhibits NADPH oxidase and COX-2.</p>
<p>Clinical evidence supports spirulina for improving lipid profiles, lowering blood pressure, enhancing antioxidant status, modulating immune function, and reducing allergic rhinitis symptoms. A 2016 meta-analysis of 7 RCTs confirmed significant lipid-lowering effects: total cholesterol -46.76 mg/dL, LDL -41.32 mg/dL, triglycerides -44.23 mg/dL, and HDL +6.06 mg/dL. A double-blind RCT in hypertensive patients showed SBP reduction of 6 mmHg at 2g/day. NASA and the European Space Agency have studied spirulina as a complete food source for long-duration space missions.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 3/5</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Spirulina's primary bioactive is phycocyanin (C-phycocyanin), a blue pigment protein that inhibits NADPH oxidase, the major source of superoxide radicals in inflammatory cells. Phycocyanin also inhibits COX-2 and scavenges peroxynitrite, hydroxyl, and peroxyl radicals. The lipid-lowering effects involve inhibition of intestinal cholesterol absorption and upregulation of hepatic LDL receptor expression, lowering circulating LDL. Spirulina's high gamma-linolenic acid (GLA) content modulates prostaglandin balance toward anti-inflammatory PGE1. The blood pressure reduction is mediated through nitric oxide (NO) production enhancement and ACE inhibition by bioactive peptides released during digestion. Immune modulation occurs through activation of NK cells and enhancement of interferon-gamma production — one study showed a 40% increase in NK cell cytotoxicity after just 7 days. Spirulina also chelates heavy metals through its sulfated polysaccharides (calcium spirulan), supporting detoxification.</p>

<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>PKU (phenylketonuria):</strong> Contains phenylalanine</li>
<li><strong>Autoimmune conditions:</strong> May stimulate immune system (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, MS)</li>
<li><strong>Bleeding disorders:</strong> May have mild anticoagulant properties</li>
</ul>
<h3>Drug Interactions</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Anticoagulants (warfarin, etc.):</strong> Additive antiplatelet effects — monitor INR</li>
<li><strong>Immunosuppressants:</strong> Spirulina stimulates immune function, potentially opposing treatment</li>
<li><strong>Antihypertensives:</strong> Additive blood pressure reduction — monitor BP</li>
</ul>
<h3>Side Effects</h3>
<p>Generally well-tolerated. Initial GI upset and headache are common during the first week and resolve with continued use. Green stool is harmless and normal. Contamination with heavy metals (lead, mercury, arsenic) or microcystins (hepatotoxic cyanobacterial toxins) is a serious concern with low-quality sources.</p>
<h3>Special Populations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Pregnancy/breastfeeding: Generally considered safe at food doses, but avoid due to contamination risk unless using certified tested products</li>
<li>Children: Safe at lower doses (0.5-1 g/day), choose verified contaminant-free sources</li>
</ul>
<h2>Quality Considerations</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Heavy metal contamination is the primary safety concern</strong> — always choose third-party tested products</li>
<li>Microcystin contamination can occur when spirulina is grown alongside wild cyanobacteria</li>
<li>Organic certification does not guarantee heavy metal testing — look specifically for third-party lab results</li>
<li>Hawaii, California, and India are major commercial production regions with varying quality standards</li>
<li>Avoid spirulina harvested from wild lakes (higher contamination risk)</li>
<li>GMP-certified, ISO-certified producers preferred</li>
<li>Check for certificates of analysis (COA) showing lead &lt; 1 ppm, arsenic &lt; 1 ppm, microcystins &lt; 1 ppb</li>
</ul>
