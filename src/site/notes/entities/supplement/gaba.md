<h1>GABA</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter in the mammalian central nervous system. Discovered in the brain by Roberts and Frankel in 1950, it is now recognized as the most abundant inhibitory neurotransmitter, present at an estimated 20-30% of all synapses in the cerebral cortex. GABA opposes the excitatory action of glutamate — together, these two amino acid neurotransmitters maintain the critical excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) balance that underlies normal brain function. Disruption of this balance is implicated in epilepsy, anxiety disorders, and neurodegenerative conditions.</p>
<p>GABA acts through two major receptor families: GABA-A receptors (ionotropic, ligand-gated chloride channels producing fast inhibition) and GABA-B receptors (metabotropic, Gi/Go-coupled GPCRs producing slower, prolonged inhibition). A third class formerly called GABA-C, now reclassified as GABA-A-rho receptors, consists of homomeric rho-subunit channels found primarily in the retina. The GABA-A receptor is the molecular target of several major drug classes including benzodiazepines, barbiturates, and many general anesthetics, making it one of the most pharmacologically important receptor systems in neurology and psychiatry.</p>
<p>An important clinical caveat: GABA does not readily cross the blood-brain barrier when taken orally as a supplement (Boonstra et al., 2015, PMID: 26500584). While peripheral GABA may influence the enteric nervous system and vagal signaling, claims that oral GABA supplements directly increase brain GABA levels are not well supported by evidence. Legitimate strategies to enhance central GABAergic tone involve drugs that modulate GABA receptors (benzodiazepines, gabapentinoids) or inhibit GABA degradation (vigabatrin).</p>
<p>Evidence Level: 5/5</p>
<p><strong>Chemical Formula:</strong> C4H9NO2<br />
<strong>Molecular Weight:</strong> 103.12 g/mol</p>
<h2>Primary Functions</h2>
<h3>Central Nervous System</h3>
<ul>
<li>Primary inhibitory neurotransmission — GABA reduces neuronal excitability by increasing chloride conductance (GABA-A) or increasing potassium conductance and decreasing calcium conductance (GABA-B)</li>
<li>Anxiety and fear modulation — GABAergic inhibition in the amygdala and prefrontal cortex is the primary neural mechanism of anxiolysis; reduced GABAergic tone is consistently found in anxiety disorders</li>
<li>Sleep-wake regulation — GABAergic neurons in the ventrolateral preoptic area (VLPO) and thalamus promote sleep onset and slow-wave sleep; most prescription hypnotics target GABA-A receptors</li>
<li>Seizure prevention — Adequate GABAergic inhibitory tone prevents runaway neuronal excitation; loss of GABAergic interneurons or GABA-A receptor dysfunction predisposes to seizures</li>
<li>Cognitive gating — GABAergic interneurons in the cortex and hippocampus regulate the timing and synchronization of neural ensembles, essential for working memory and attention</li>
</ul>
<h3>Motor Control</h3>
<ul>
<li>Cerebellar Purkinje cells are GABAergic — they provide the sole output of the cerebellar cortex, inhibiting deep cerebellar nuclei</li>
<li>Striatal medium spiny neurons (MSNs) are GABAergic — they form the direct and indirect pathways of the basal ganglia, critical for voluntary movement initiation and suppression</li>
<li>Loss of GABAergic neurons in the striatum is the hallmark pathology of Huntington's disease</li>
</ul>
<h3>Pain Modulation</h3>
<ul>
<li>GABAergic interneurons in the spinal dorsal horn gate nociceptive transmission (the &quot;gate control&quot; mechanism)</li>
<li>GABA-B receptor agonists (e.g., baclofen) reduce spasticity and neuropathic pain</li>
<li>Gabapentinoids (gabapentin, pregabalin) indirectly modulate GABAergic systems, though their primary mechanism involves alpha-2-delta subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels</li>
</ul>
<h3>Endocrine Regulation</h3>
<ul>
<li>Hypothalamic GABA neurons regulate growth hormone (GH), prolactin, and adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) release</li>
<li>GABA inhibits corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) neurons, providing an inhibitory input to the HPA stress axis</li>
</ul>
<h3>Neurodevelopment</h3>
<ul>
<li>During early cortical development, GABA acts as an excitatory neurotransmitter (due to high intracellular Cl- in immature neurons) and serves as a trophic signal promoting neuronal migration, differentiation, and synapse formation (Owens et al., 1999, PMID: 10444657)</li>
</ul>

<h3>GABA-A Receptor Details</h3>
<p>GABA-A receptors are pentameric assemblies drawn from 19 known subunits: alpha(1-6), beta(1-3), gamma(1-3), delta, epsilon, theta, pi, and rho(1-3). The most common composition in the brain is 2alpha + 2beta + 1gamma (Olsen &amp; Sieghart, 2008, PMID: 18790874).</p>
<p>Key pharmacological binding sites:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>GABA site</strong> — at beta-alpha subunit interface; agonized by muscimol, antagonized by bicuculline</li>
<li><strong>Benzodiazepine site</strong> — at gamma-alpha subunit interface; requires a gamma subunit; positive allosteric modulators (diazepam, lorazepam) enhance GABA-gated Cl- conductance</li>
<li><strong>Barbiturate site</strong> — distinct from benzodiazepine site; prolongs channel opening; can directly activate the channel at high concentrations (unlike benzodiazepines)</li>
<li><strong>Neurosteroid site</strong> — on alpha subunits; allopregnanolone and THDOC are endogenous positive modulators; synthetic brexanolone (Zulresso) for postpartum depression</li>
<li><strong>Ethanol site</strong> — interacts with delta-subunit-containing extrasynaptic GABA-A receptors mediating tonic inhibition</li>
<li><strong>Picrotoxin site</strong> — within the channel pore; non-competitive antagonist, blocks Cl- flux</li>
</ul>
<p>GABA-A receptors mediate both <strong>phasic inhibition</strong> (fast, synaptic, mediated by gamma-containing receptors) and <strong>tonic inhibition</strong> (slow, extrasynaptic, mediated by delta-containing receptors, particularly sensitive to ethanol and neurosteroids).</p>
<h3>GABA-B Receptor Details</h3>
<p>GABA-B receptors are obligate heterodimers of GABA-B1 and GABA-B2 subunits, both seven-transmembrane proteins (Bowery et al., 2002, PMID: 12037141). GABA-B1 contains the GABA binding site; GABA-B2 is required for surface expression and G protein coupling.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Presynaptic GABA-B autoreceptors</strong> — inhibit neurotransmitter release (both GABA and glutamate) by inhibiting voltage-gated Ca2+ channels</li>
<li><strong>Postsynaptic GABA-B receptors</strong> — activate Kir3 (GIRK) potassium channels, producing slow IPSPs</li>
<li><strong>Selective agonist</strong>: baclofen</li>
<li><strong>Selective antagonist</strong>: saclofen, CGP 55845</li>
</ul>

                                                     [SSADH]
                                                        ↓

</code></pre>
<h3>Step 1 — Glutamate Decarboxylation (Rate-Limiting)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Enzyme:</strong> Glutamic acid decarboxylase (GAD)</li>
<li><strong>Reaction:</strong> L-Glutamate → GABA + CO2</li>
<li><strong>Cofactor:</strong> Pyridoxal phosphate (PLP), the active form of vitamin B6</li>
<li><strong>Two isoforms:</strong>
<ul>
<li><strong>GAD65</strong> (GAD2 gene) — concentrated in nerve terminals, associated with synaptic vesicles, regulated by neuronal activity and PLP availability; the primary driver of activity-dependent GABA release</li>
<li><strong>GAD67</strong> (GAD1 gene) — widely distributed throughout the cytoplasm, constitutively active, responsible for basal GABA production and non-synaptic (tonic) GABA levels; knockout is lethal (cleft palate, seizures)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Autoantibodies against GAD65 are a biomarker in type 1 diabetes, stiff-person syndrome, and limbic encephalitis</li>
</ul>
<h3>GABA-Glutamine Cycle</h3>
<p>Released GABA is taken up by astrocytes via GAT-3 transporter, where it is converted to succinic semialdehyde (via GABA-T) and enters the TCA cycle, ultimately regenerating glutamate and then glutamine (via glutamine synthetase). Glutamine is shuttled back to neurons for reconversion to glutamate. This cycle couples GABAergic neurotransmission to cellular energy metabolism.</p>
<h2>Degradation Pathway</h2>
<pre><code>GABA → Succinic Semialdehyde → Succinate → TCA cycle
 [GABA-T]            [SSADH]
</code></pre>
<ul>
<li><strong>GABA transaminase (GABA-T)</strong> — mitochondrial enzyme; transfers amino group from GABA to alpha-ketoglutarate, producing succinic semialdehyde and glutamate; requires PLP (vitamin B6) as cofactor</li>
<li><strong>Succinic semialdehyde dehydrogenase (SSADH)</strong> — converts succinic semialdehyde to succinate, which enters the TCA cycle</li>
<li><strong>SSADH deficiency</strong> — rare autosomal recessive disorder causing GHB accumulation, developmental delay, seizures, and ataxia</li>
<li><strong>Alternative minor pathway</strong> — succinic semialdehyde → gamma-hydroxybutyrate (GHB) via succinic semialdehyde reductase</li>
</ul>
<h3>Pharmacological Targeting</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Vigabatrin</strong> (Sabril) — irreversible GABA-T inhibitor; increases brain GABA levels by 2-3x; used for refractory epilepsy and infantile spasms; risk of irreversible peripheral visual field constriction (in up to 30-40% of patients)</li>
<li><strong>Tiagabine</strong> — GAT-1 reuptake inhibitor; increases synaptic GABA availability; used as adjunctive antiepileptic</li>
</ul>

<h2>Interactions with Other Neurotransmitters</h2>
<p>GABA does not operate in isolation — it is part of a dynamic neurochemical network:</p>

<thead>
<tr>
<th>Neurotransmitter</th>
<th>Interaction</th>
<th>Mechanism</th>
</tr>
</thead>

<tr>
<td><strong>Glutamate</strong></td>
<td>Functional antagonist</td>
<td>GABA and glutamate are the brain's primary inhibitory/excitatory pair; the E/I balance between them is critical for normal cognition and seizure prevention</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Dopamine</strong></td>
<td>Indirect modulation</td>
<td>GABAergic interneurons in the striatum and prefrontal cortex regulate dopaminergic neuron firing; disrupted GABA-dopamine balance contributes to motor disorders and psychosis</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Serotonin</strong></td>
<td>Indirect modulation</td>
<td>Serotonergic raphe neurons modulate GABAergic interneuron activity; some anxiolytic effects of SSRIs may involve downstream GABAergic enhancement</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Norepinephrine</strong></td>
<td>Bidirectional</td>
<td>Locus coeruleus norepinephrine neurons stimulate GABA release in cortex; GABAergic interneurons provide feedback inhibition to the locus coeruleus</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Glycine</strong></td>
<td>Co-agonist at NMDA receptors</td>
<td>While glycine is itself an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the spinal cord, it also functions as a co-agonist at NMDA glutamate receptors — linking inhibitory amino acid systems</td>
</tr>
</tbody>

<p>The GABA-glutamate balance is particularly important clinically: excessive glutamatergic excitation relative to GABAergic inhibition underlies seizures, excitotoxicity, and certain anxiety states. Many antiepileptic and anxiolytic drugs work by either enhancing GABA or suppressing glutamate.</p>

<p><strong>Important caveat on oral GABA supplementation</strong>: GABA does not readily cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB) when taken orally (Boonstra et al., 2015, PMID: 26500584). However, oral GABA may influence the enteric nervous system and vagal afferent signaling. Strategies to enhance central GABAergic tone include receptor modulators (benzodiazepines, gabapentinoids), enzyme inhibition (vigabatrin), and lifestyle approaches (exercise, meditation) rather than direct GABA ingestion.</p>
<h2>Biomarkers and Measurement</h2>
<p>GABA levels in the living human brain can be measured non-invasively using <strong>magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS)</strong>, typically at 3T or 7T field strengths. The GABA peak at ~3.0 ppm (overlapping with macromolecule signals and creatine) is resolved using specialized techniques:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>MEGA-PRESS</strong> (MEscher-GArwood Point RESolved Spectroscopy) — the most widely used clinical MRS method for GABA; uses frequency-selective editing to separate GABA from overlapping signals</li>
<li><strong>J-edited MRS</strong> — similar principle; measures GABA relative to creatine or water reference</li>
</ul>
