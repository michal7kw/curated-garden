<h1>Sulforaphane</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Sulforaphane is a potent isothiocyanate compound found in cruciferous vegetables, with the highest concentrations in 3-day-old broccoli sprouts (10-100x more glucoraphanin than mature broccoli). It is the most powerful known natural activator of the Nrf2 (nuclear factor erythroid 2-related factor 2) transcription pathway, which governs the expression of over 200 cytoprotective genes involved in phase II detoxification, glutathione synthesis, and antioxidant defense.</p>
<p>Clinical trials have demonstrated sulforaphane's efficacy across diverse conditions. A landmark double-blind RCT at Johns Hopkins showed 34% improvement in Aberrant Behavior Checklist (ABC) scores in young males with autism spectrum disorder. A 2017 Science Translational Medicine study demonstrated reduced fasting blood glucose and HbA1c in patients with type 2 diabetes through NRF2-mediated suppression of hepatic glucose production. A large detoxification RCT in China showed 61% increased excretion of airborne benzene conjugates and 23% increased acrolein excretion with daily broccoli sprout beverage consumption. The compound requires the enzyme myrosinase to convert from its inactive precursor glucoraphanin — cooking destroys myrosinase, but gut bacteria and exogenous myrosinase (from mustard seed) can perform this conversion.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 3/5</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Sulforaphane exerts its effects through multiple interconnected pathways:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Nrf2-Keap1 pathway activation:</strong> Sulforaphane covalently modifies critical cysteine residues (C151, C273, C288) on Keap1, the cytoplasmic repressor that normally targets Nrf2 for proteasomal degradation. This frees Nrf2 to translocate to the nucleus where it binds Antioxidant Response Elements (ARE), inducing transcription of over 200 cytoprotective genes.</li>
<li><strong>Phase II detoxification:</strong> Upregulates glutathione S-transferases (GSTs), NAD(P)H:quinone oxidoreductase 1 (NQO1), and UDP-glucuronosyltransferases (UGTs) — the body's primary conjugation enzymes for neutralizing toxins, carcinogens, and xenobiotics.</li>
<li><strong>Glutathione synthesis:</strong> Induces gamma-glutamylcysteine ligase (GCLC, GCLM), the rate-limiting enzyme in glutathione biosynthesis, substantially increasing intracellular glutathione levels.</li>
<li><strong>Anti-inflammatory:</strong> Inhibits NF-kB signaling, reducing pro-inflammatory cytokine production (TNF-alpha, IL-6, IL-1beta).</li>
<li><strong>Epigenetic modulation:</strong> Acts as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor, particularly HDAC3 and HDAC6, re-expressing tumor suppressor genes silenced in cancer cells.</li>
<li><strong>Autophagy enhancement:</strong> Promotes cellular clearance of damaged proteins and organelles via autophagy induction.</li>
<li><strong>Glucose metabolism:</strong> Suppresses hepatic glucose production through NRF2-mediated downregulation of gluconeogenic enzymes (G6PC, PCK1).</li>
</ul>

<h3>Enhancing Bioavailability</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Add myrosinase:</strong> If using glucoraphanin supplements, add 1/4 tsp raw mustard seed powder (rich in myrosinase)</li>
<li><strong>Avoid cooking</strong> raw sprouts — heat destroys myrosinase; let sprouts sit for 5 minutes after chewing/cutting to allow myrosinase reaction</li>
<li><strong>Chew thoroughly</strong> to release myrosinase from plant cells</li>
<li><strong>Take with food</strong> for better tolerance and absorption</li>
</ul>
<h3>Timing</h3>
<ul>
<li>Take with meals for GI tolerance</li>
<li>Morning dosing may optimize Nrf2 activation</li>
<li>Consistency matters more than specific timing</li>
</ul>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Thyroid conditions:</strong> Goitrogenic compounds in high doses may interfere with iodine uptake; dietary amounts from normal vegetable consumption are safe</li>
<li><strong>Pregnancy:</strong> High supplemental doses should be avoided; dietary intake from vegetables is safe</li>
<li><strong>Hemochromatosis:</strong> Iron chelation properties may affect iron status</li>
</ul>
<h3>Drug Interactions</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>CYP-metabolized drugs:</strong> Sulforaphane induces phase II enzymes and may alter metabolism of drugs processed through these pathways</li>
<li><strong>Anticoagulants:</strong> Theoretical additive antiplatelet effects</li>
<li><strong>Chemotherapy drugs:</strong> May interfere with some chemotherapeutic agents that rely on oxidative mechanisms; consult oncologist</li>
</ul>
<h3>Side Effects</h3>
<p>Gas and bloating are the most common side effects, typical of cruciferous vegetables. GI discomfort at higher doses can be mitigated by taking with food and starting at lower doses. In the autism trial, weight gain was reported in some participants (reversed after discontinuation). Very high doses may affect thyroid function due to goitrogenic effects.</p>
<h2>Quality Considerations</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Prostaphane</strong> (France) is the most clinically validated stabilized sulforaphane supplement</li>
<li>Fresh broccoli sprouts can be home-grown from seeds (most economical and potent approach)</li>
<li>Many commercial supplements contain only glucoraphanin without active myrosinase — check labels carefully</li>
<li>Sulforaphane is inherently unstable; stabilized forms (prostaphane, Avmacol) are preferred over generic extracts</li>
<li>For glucoraphanin-only supplements, combine with raw mustard seed powder to ensure conversion</li>
</ul>
