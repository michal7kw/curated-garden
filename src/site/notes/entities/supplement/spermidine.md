<h1>Spermidine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Spermidine is a naturally occurring polyamine found in all living cells that plays a central role in cellular growth, differentiation, and autophagy regulation. Endogenous spermidine levels decline significantly with age, and this decline correlates with reduced autophagic capacity and increased age-related pathology. Supplementing spermidine or increasing dietary intake is hypothesized to counteract this age-related decline and promote healthspan.</p>
<p>The foundational discovery by Eisenberg et al. (2009) demonstrated that spermidine administration extends lifespan across yeast, flies, worms, and human immune cells by inducing autophagy. In mice, oral spermidine supplementation extended median lifespan by approximately 10% and exerted cardioprotective effects dependent on autophagy gene Atg5. The Bruneck Study, a 20-year prospective cohort of 829 humans, found that individuals in the highest tertile of dietary spermidine intake had 5.7 years of additional life expectancy compared to the lowest tertile, with particularly strong reductions in cardiovascular mortality. The SmartAge trial (2022), a 12-month RCT in 100 older adults with subjective cognitive decline, showed exploratory evidence of improved verbal memory with spermidine-rich wheat germ extract supplementation.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 3/5</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Spermidine exerts its health-promoting effects primarily through autophagy induction via multiple interconnected mechanisms:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>EP300 (p300) inhibition:</strong> Spermidine inhibits the acetyltransferase EP300, causing hypoacetylation of cytoplasmic proteins including histone H3. This epigenetic deacetylation upregulates transcription of autophagy-related genes (ATG genes) and triggers autophagosome formation.</li>
<li><strong>TFEB nuclear translocation:</strong> Promotes nuclear translocation of TFEB (Transcription Factor EB), the master regulator of lysosomal biogenesis and autophagy, enhancing cellular clearance capacity.</li>
<li><strong>AMPK activation:</strong> Activates AMPK signaling, which inhibits mTOR complex 1 (the primary autophagy suppressor) and directly phosphorylates ULK1 to initiate autophagosome formation.</li>
<li><strong>Mitophagy induction:</strong> Stimulates selective mitochondrial autophagy via the PINK1/Parkin pathway, improving mitochondrial quality control and reducing oxidative stress.</li>
<li><strong>Anti-inflammatory effects:</strong> Suppresses NF-kB-mediated inflammatory signaling, reducing age-associated chronic inflammation (inflammaging).</li>
<li><strong>Immune function:</strong> Enhances anticancer immunosurveillance by improving autophagy in immune cells, potentially explaining reduced cancer incidence in supplemented animals despite theoretical cancer risk from polyamine biology.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Active cancer:</strong> Polyamines can promote growth of existing tumors. While spermidine enhances anticancer immunosurveillance in preclinical models (reducing cancer incidence), it may accelerate growth of established tumors. This remains controversial.</li>
<li><strong>Pregnancy/breastfeeding:</strong> Insufficient safety data; avoid supplementation.</li>
<li><strong>Organ transplant recipients:</strong> Potential immune-stimulating effects via enhanced autophagy in immune cells may theoretically increase rejection risk.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Drug Interactions</h3>
<ul>
<li>No significant drug interactions have been reported in clinical trials at physiological doses</li>
<li>Theoretical interaction with drugs affecting autophagy (rapamycin, chloroquine)</li>
<li>May have additive anti-inflammatory effects with NSAIDs or corticosteroids</li>
</ul>
<h3>Side Effects</h3>
<p>Spermidine is well-tolerated at physiological doses. No serious adverse events have been reported in clinical trials. Mild GI discomfort (bloating, soft stools) may occur at higher supplemental doses (&gt;6 mg/day). Endogenous spermidine is continuously produced by the body via ornithine decarboxylase pathway.</p>
<h2>Quality Considerations</h2>
<ul>
<li>Wheat germ extract products should specify spermidine content per serving</li>
<li>spermidineLIFE is the most clinically validated commercial product</li>
<li>Synthetic spermidine supplements (as trihydrochloride) offer precise dosing</li>
<li>Dietary polyamine intake can be significantly increased through food choices alone</li>
<li>Gut microbiome also produces spermidine; probiotic supplementation may enhance endogenous levels</li>
</ul>
