<h1>Taurine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Taurine is a conditionally essential sulfur-containing amino acid and the most abundant free amino acid in the heart, retina, brain, and white blood cells. A landmark 2023 Science paper demonstrated that taurine deficiency drives aging in mice, and supplementation extended lifespan by 10-12% while improving healthspan across every organ system. Taurine levels decline 80% with age in humans. It is not incorporated into proteins but acts as a free intracellular osmolyte, membrane stabilizer, and signaling molecule.</p>
<p>Taurine is synthesized endogenously from cysteine and methionine via the cysteine sulfinic acid pathway, but this synthesis may be insufficient under conditions of stress, illness, or rapid growth. Dietary intake comes primarily from animal products (meat, fish, shellfish), making vegans and vegetarians particularly susceptible to suboptimal taurine status. The body pool is approximately 1 g/kg body weight, with intracellular concentrations 20-100 times higher than plasma levels due to active transport via the TauT transporter.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 4/5</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Taurine functions through multiple interconnected pathways:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Bile acid conjugation:</strong> Forms taurocholate for fat digestion and cholesterol elimination, contributing to its lipid-lowering effects.</li>
<li><strong>Osmoregulation:</strong> Acts as a critical intracellular osmolyte, protecting cells from swelling and shrinking — especially important in the brain (neuroprotection) and heart (cardiomyocyte volume regulation).</li>
<li><strong>Calcium signaling modulation:</strong> Stabilizes cardiomyocyte calcium handling through SERCA2a pump regulation and ryanodine receptor modulation, producing antiarrhythmic effects and improving cardiac contractility.</li>
<li><strong>Mitochondrial function:</strong> Required for the post-transcriptional modification of mitochondrial tRNA (5-taurinomethyluridine formation), which is essential for proper mitochondrial protein synthesis and oxidative phosphorylation.</li>
<li><strong>Antioxidant activity:</strong> Directly scavenges hypochlorous acid (HOCl) produced by activated neutrophils, generating taurine chloramine (TauCl), which has its own anti-inflammatory properties and suppresses NF-kB signaling.</li>
<li><strong>GABAergic activity:</strong> Acts as a weak GABA-A receptor agonist, producing anxiolytic and calming effects without sedation. Also functions as a glycine receptor agonist.</li>
<li><strong>Anti-aging:</strong> Reverses age-related DNA hypermethylation patterns, increases telomerase activity, and reduces senescent cell burden (demonstrated in the 2023 Science paper).</li>
</ul>

<h3>Form Selection</h3>
<p>Taurine is available as a stable, odorless, slightly sweet-tasting powder or capsules. There is only one chemical form (taurine is not chiral despite the &quot;L-taurine&quot; marketing label). Powder form offers better value and easier dose adjustment.</p>
<h3>Timing</h3>
<ul>
<li>Can be taken any time with or without food</li>
<li>For exercise: 1-2 hours pre-workout</li>
<li>For sleep/calming: 30-60 minutes before bed (response varies)</li>
<li>Often combined with magnesium, caffeine (in energy drinks), or BCAAs</li>
</ul>
<h3>Dietary Considerations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Abundant in meat, fish, shellfish (especially dark meat poultry, scallops, mussels)</li>
<li>Essentially absent from plant foods — vegans and vegetarians typically have lower taurine status</li>
<li>Cooking does not significantly degrade taurine</li>
</ul>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Bipolar disorder (theoretical concern of mood destabilization)</li>
<li>Known hypersensitivity to taurine</li>
</ul>
<h3>Drug Interactions</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Antihypertensives:</strong> Additive blood pressure reduction — monitor BP when combining</li>
<li><strong>Lithium:</strong> Theoretical interaction affecting lithium levels</li>
<li><strong>Sedatives:</strong> Potential additive GABAergic effects</li>
</ul>
<h3>Side Effects</h3>
<p>Taurine is remarkably well-tolerated. Clinical trials have used doses up to 6g/day for months without significant adverse effects. Mild GI upset occurs rarely at higher doses. Some individuals report stimulation while others report calming — response is idiosyncratic.</p>
<h3>Pregnancy and Lactation</h3>
<p>Taurine is naturally present in breast milk and is added to infant formula. Supplementation during pregnancy appears safe at typical doses (1-3g/day). Consult healthcare provider for high-dose use.</p>
<h2>Quality Considerations</h2>
<ul>
<li>Taurine is synthetically produced (not derived from animal sources in most commercial supplements), making it suitable for vegans</li>
<li>Extremely stable compound with long shelf life</li>
<li>No significant quality concerns have been reported in the literature</li>
<li>Third-party testing (NSF, USP) recommended for competitive athletes due to prevalence in energy drinks</li>
</ul>
