<h1>Magnesium</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Magnesium is the fourth most abundant mineral in the human body and an essential cofactor for over 300 enzymatic reactions, including ATP metabolism, protein synthesis, nucleic acid synthesis, and ion transport. Approximately 50-60% of total body magnesium is stored in bone, with the remainder distributed in skeletal muscle, soft tissue, and extracellular fluid. The RDA ranges from 310 mg/day for adult women to 420 mg/day for adult men, yet nearly two-thirds of the Western population fails to meet this intake, making subclinical deficiency widespread.</p>
<p>In the brain, magnesium plays a critical role in neuronal excitability by acting as a voltage-dependent blocker of NMDA receptors at resting membrane potential, preventing excitotoxicity. Magnesium L-threonate (Magtein) is the only form demonstrated to cross the blood-brain barrier and significantly elevate brain magnesium concentrations in preclinical studies. Magnesium also supports GABAergic inhibitory tone, modulates the HPA axis stress response, and is essential for the synthesis of serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 5/5</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Magnesium exerts its effects through multiple interconnected mechanisms:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>NMDA receptor modulation:</strong> Voltage-dependent blockade of the NMDA receptor channel at resting potential prevents excessive calcium influx and excitotoxic neuronal damage. Elevation of brain magnesium enhances synaptic plasticity and increases NR2B-containing NMDA receptor expression.</li>
<li><strong>GABA enhancement:</strong> Magnesium potentiates GABA-A receptor activity, promoting inhibitory neurotransmission and anxiolytic effects.</li>
<li><strong>Ion channel regulation:</strong> Blocks voltage-gated calcium channels and modulates potassium channels, affecting muscle contraction, cardiac conduction, and vascular tone.</li>
<li><strong>Enzymatic cofactor:</strong> Required for all ATP-dependent reactions as the Mg-ATP complex; essential for kinases, cyclases, and DNA/RNA polymerases.</li>
<li><strong>Neurotransmitter synthesis:</strong> Cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase (dopamine/norepinephrine) and tryptophan hydroxylase (serotonin).</li>
<li><strong>Endocrine regulation:</strong> Required for parathyroid hormone secretion and the conversion of vitamin D to its active form (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D), which in turn affects calcium and magnesium homeostasis.</li>
<li><strong>Anti-inflammatory:</strong> Suppresses NF-kB activation and reduces pro-inflammatory cytokine production (TNF-alpha, IL-6, CRP).</li>
</ul>

<h3>Timing</h3>
<ul>
<li>Take with or without food (food may reduce GI side effects)</li>
<li>For sleep benefits: take 30-60 minutes before bed</li>
<li>Separate from medications by at least 2 hours before or 4-6 hours after (especially bisphosphonates, tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, levothyroxine)</li>
<li>Divide doses throughout the day to improve absorption and reduce GI effects</li>
</ul>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Kidney disease or renal impairment (risk of hypermagnesemia)</li>
<li>Myasthenia gravis (may worsen muscle weakness)</li>
<li>Heart block (affects cardiac conduction)</li>
<li>Severe hypotension</li>
</ul>
<h3>Drug Interactions</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Gabapentin:</strong> May reduce absorption; separate dosing by 2+ hours</li>
<li><strong>Levothyroxine:</strong> May reduce absorption; separate by 4+ hours</li>
<li><strong>Bisphosphonates:</strong> Significantly reduces absorption</li>
<li><strong>Fluoroquinolones/Tetracyclines:</strong> Chelation reduces antibiotic absorption</li>
<li><strong>Proton pump inhibitors:</strong> Long-term PPI use impairs magnesium absorption; may cause refractory hypomagnesemia</li>
<li><strong>Blood thinners:</strong> Theoretical increased bleeding risk at high doses (800-1200 mg/day)</li>
<li><strong>Blood pressure medications:</strong> Additive hypotensive effect</li>
</ul>
<h3>Side Effects</h3>
<p>Diarrhea is the most common side effect, particularly with oxide and citrate forms. Taking in divided doses or switching to glycinate form may reduce GI effects. Hypermagnesemia is rare in individuals with normal kidney function but can occur at doses exceeding 1,000 mg/day.</p>
<h3>Pregnancy and Lactation</h3>
<p>Magnesium supplementation is safe during pregnancy and lactation. May reduce preeclampsia risk. The UL for supplemental magnesium remains 350 mg/day during pregnancy.</p>
<h2>Quality Considerations</h2>
<ul>
<li>Choose supplements tested by third-party organizations (NSF, USP, ConsumerLab)</li>
<li>Studies have found that up to 60% of tested magnesium supplements may contain amounts differing significantly from labels</li>
<li>Some natural-source supplements have been found to contain trace amounts of lead</li>
<li>Avoid magnesium oxide due to extremely poor bioavailability (~4%)</li>
</ul>
