<h1>Nucleus</h1>
<h2>Description</h2>
<p>Membrane-bound organelle containing the cell's genetic material (DNA). Controls gene expression, DNA replication, and RNA processing. Enclosed by a double nuclear envelope with nuclear pores for selective transport. Contains the nucleolus for ribosomal RNA synthesis.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<p>The nucleus is the command center of the eukaryotic cell, performing several critical functions:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Genetic information storage</strong>: Houses the cell's complete genome organized into chromosomes (46 in humans, 23 pairs). DNA is packaged around histone proteins into chromatin, which dynamically condenses/decondenses to regulate gene accessibility.</li>
<li><strong>Gene expression regulation</strong>: Controls which genes are transcribed into mRNA through transcription factors, epigenetic modifications (DNA methylation, histone acetylation), and chromatin remodeling complexes. This determines cell identity and function.</li>
<li><strong>DNA replication</strong>: Coordinates precise genome duplication during the S-phase of the cell cycle, ensuring each daughter cell receives a complete copy. Replication origins are licensed and fired in a tightly regulated sequence.</li>
<li><strong>RNA processing</strong>: Newly transcribed pre-mRNA undergoes 5' capping, splicing (removal of introns), and 3' polyadenylation within the nucleus before export to the cytoplasm. Alternative splicing generates protein diversity from a single gene.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Key Processes</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Transcription</strong>: RNA polymerase II synthesizes mRNA from DNA templates. RNA polymerase I produces rRNA, and RNA polymerase III produces tRNA and other small RNAs.</li>
<li><strong>DNA repair</strong>: Multiple pathways (NER, BER, HR, NHEJ) maintain genomic integrity. Defects in nuclear DNA repair underlie many cancers and premature aging disorders (progeria).</li>
<li><strong>Cell cycle control</strong>: The nucleus orchestrates cell division through cyclin-CDK complexes. Checkpoint mechanisms (G1/S, G2/M, spindle assembly) ensure genomic stability.</li>
<li><strong>Nuclear-cytoplasmic transport</strong>: Nuclear pore complexes (NPCs) regulate bidirectional transport of proteins, RNA, and other macromolecules. Each NPC contains ~30 different nucleoporins (Nups).</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cancer</strong>: Nearly all cancers involve nuclear abnormalities — chromosomal translocations (BCR-ABL in CML), gene amplifications (HER2 in breast cancer), aneuploidy, and mutations in tumor suppressors (TP53, RB1) that regulate the nucleus.</li>
<li><strong>Laminopathies</strong>: Mutations in nuclear lamins (LMNA) cause diverse diseases including Hutchinson-Gilford progeria (premature aging), Emery-Dreifuss muscular dystrophy, and cardiomyopathy. Lamins provide structural integrity to the nuclear envelope.</li>
<li><strong>Autoimmune diseases</strong>: Anti-nuclear antibodies (ANA) are the hallmark of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) and other autoimmune conditions, where the immune system targets nuclear antigens.</li>
<li><strong>Viral infection</strong>: Many viruses (HIV, HSV, adenovirus) must enter the nucleus to replicate. HIV integrates into the host genome via nuclear import; understanding nuclear pore transport is key to antiviral strategies.</li>
</ul>
