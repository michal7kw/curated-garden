<h1>Telomere</h1>
<h2>Description</h2>
<p>Telomeres are repetitive nucleotide sequences (TTAGGG in vertebrates) and associated shelterin protein complexes that cap the ends of linear chromosomes. They prevent chromosome ends from being recognized as DNA double-strand breaks, protecting against end-to-end fusion, degradation, and inappropriate DNA damage responses. With each cell division, telomeres shorten by 50-200 base pairs due to the end-replication problem. When telomeres reach a critical length (~5 kb), cells enter replicative senescence (Hayflick limit) or apoptosis. Telomere length is now recognized as a biomarker of biological (vs. chronological) aging, with shorter telomeres associated with cardiovascular disease, cancer, diabetes, and mortality.</p>
<h2>Key Concepts</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Telomerase</strong> — Reverse transcriptase enzyme that rebuilds telomeres; active in stem cells, germ cells, and ~90% of cancers; silenced in most somatic cells</li>
<li><strong>Shelterin complex</strong> — Six-protein complex (TRF1, TRF2, POT1, TIN2, TPP1, RAP1) protecting telomere structure</li>
<li><strong>Hayflick limit</strong> — ~50-70 divisions before telomere-induced senescence in human fibroblasts</li>
<li><strong>Telomere length inheritance</strong> — 40-80% heritable; significant individual variation at birth</li>
</ul>
<h2>Factors Affecting Telomere Length</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Shorten telomeres</strong>: Chronic psychological stress, smoking, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, poor sleep, oxidative stress, chronic inflammation</li>
<li><strong>Preserve/lengthen telomeres</strong>: Exercise, meditation, omega-3 fatty acids, healthy diet, social connection, adequate sleep</li>
</ul>
