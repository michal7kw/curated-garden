<h1>Ribosome</h1>
<h2>Description</h2>
<p>Macromolecular machine composed of ribosomal RNA and proteins that catalyzes protein synthesis (translation). Eukaryotic ribosomes (80S) consist of a large 60S and small 40S subunit. Found free in the cytoplasm or bound to the endoplasmic reticulum. One of the most abundant cellular structures.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<p>Ribosomes are the molecular machines that translate mRNA into proteins, a process fundamental to all life:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Protein synthesis (translation)</strong>: The ribosome reads mRNA codons sequentially and catalyzes the formation of peptide bonds between amino acids delivered by tRNA molecules. The large subunit contains the peptidyl transferase center (a ribozyme — the catalytic activity is RNA-based, not protein-based), while the small subunit decodes mRNA-tRNA interactions.</li>
<li><strong>Translation phases</strong>: Initiation (assembly of ribosomal subunits on mRNA with initiator tRNA at the start codon), elongation (sequential amino acid addition), and termination (release of the completed polypeptide at a stop codon).</li>
<li><strong>Co-translational processing</strong>: In rough ER-bound ribosomes, the nascent polypeptide is threaded through the ER membrane for post-translational modifications (glycosylation, disulfide bond formation) and secretion.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Key Processes</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Ribosome biogenesis</strong>: A massive cellular effort involving all three RNA polymerases. The nucleolus produces rRNA (transcribed by Pol I), which is processed, modified (methylation, pseudouridylation), and assembled with ribosomal proteins. Over 200 assembly factors participate. This is the most energy-intensive process in growing cells.</li>
<li><strong>Translational regulation</strong>: mTOR signaling controls ribosome biogenesis and translation initiation via phosphorylation of 4E-BP1 and S6K. This couples nutrient availability to protein synthesis rates — a key mechanism in cell growth, aging, and cancer.</li>
<li><strong>Ribosomal quality control</strong>: Surveillance mechanisms (no-go decay, non-stop decay) detect and resolve stalled ribosomes, preventing production of truncated or aberrant proteins.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Antibiotics</strong>: Many antibiotics target bacterial ribosomes (70S) selectively over eukaryotic ribosomes (80S): macrolides bind the 50S subunit, tetracyclines block the A-site on 30S, aminoglycosides cause misreading. Mitochondrial ribosomes (similar to bacterial) are an off-target concern.</li>
<li><strong>Ribosomopathies</strong>: Mutations in ribosomal proteins or assembly factors cause tissue-specific diseases despite ribosomes being ubiquitous: Diamond-Blackfan anemia (RPS19 mutations), Shwachman-Diamond syndrome (SBDS), and 5q- syndrome. The paradox of tissue specificity is explained by differential sensitivity to reduced ribosome production.</li>
<li><strong>Cancer</strong>: Ribosome biogenesis is upregulated in virtually all cancers (nucleolar enlargement is a classic histological marker). MYC directly drives rRNA transcription. mTOR inhibitors (rapamycin) partially work by suppressing translation.</li>
<li><strong>Aging</strong>: Declining ribosome quality and translational fidelity contribute to aging. Reduced protein quality control leads to accumulation of misfolded proteins. Dietary restriction extends lifespan partly through modulating translation rates via mTOR/AMPK.</li>
</ul>
