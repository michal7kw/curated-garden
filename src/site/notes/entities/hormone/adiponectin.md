<h1>Adiponectin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Adiponectin is a protein hormone (244 amino acids) secreted exclusively by adipocytes, circulating at remarkably high concentrations (5-30 μg/mL) in healthy individuals. Unlike most adipokines, adiponectin levels are paradoxically inversely correlated with adiposity — lean individuals have higher levels, while obese individuals have lower levels. It signals through AdipoR1 (skeletal muscle) and AdipoR2 (liver) receptors to activate AMPK and PPARα pathways, enhancing insulin sensitivity, fatty acid oxidation, and glucose uptake while suppressing inflammation and atherogenesis.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Insulin sensitization (enhances muscle glucose uptake via AMPK)</li>
<li>Hepatic fatty acid oxidation (PPARα activation)</li>
<li>Anti-inflammatory effects (suppresses NF-κB, TNF-α, IL-6)</li>
<li>Anti-atherogenic (protects endothelium, reduces foam cell formation)</li>
<li>Cardioprotective (reduces cardiac hypertrophy and fibrosis)</li>
<li>Ceramidase activity (reduces toxic ceramide levels)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li>Low adiponectin is an independent risk factor for type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers</li>
<li>Weight loss, exercise, and certain supplements (omega-3, berberine) can increase levels</li>
<li>Thiazolidinediones (pioglitazone) increase adiponectin as a primary mechanism</li>
</ul>
