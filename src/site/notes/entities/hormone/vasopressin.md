<h1>Vasopressin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Vasopressin (arginine vasopressin, AVP), also known as antidiuretic hormone (ADH), is a nonapeptide hormone synthesized in the paraventricular and supraoptic nuclei of the hypothalamus and released from the posterior pituitary. Its primary peripheral role is water conservation — promoting water reabsorption in the renal collecting ducts via V2 receptors and aquaporin-2 channels. Centrally, vasopressin acts as a neurotransmitter/neuromodulator influencing social behavior (pair bonding, aggression, paternal behavior), stress response (synergizes with CRH to release ACTH), and circadian rhythms (expressed in the suprachiasmatic nucleus).</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Renal water reabsorption (V2 receptors → aquaporin-2 insertion)</li>
<li>Vasoconstriction and blood pressure maintenance (V1a receptors)</li>
<li>ACTH release from anterior pituitary (V1b receptors — HPA axis)</li>
<li>Social behavior modulation (pair bonding, territorial aggression in males)</li>
<li>Circadian rhythm signaling (suprachiasmatic nucleus)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Diabetes insipidus</strong> — Deficiency (central) or resistance (nephrogenic) → massive dilute urine output</li>
<li><strong>SIADH</strong> — Excess vasopressin → water retention, hyponatremia</li>
<li><strong>Heart failure</strong> — Elevated vasopressin contributes to fluid overload (vaptans used therapeutically)</li>
<li><strong>Autism</strong> — Vasopressin pathway variants linked to social behavior deficits</li>
<li><strong>Septic shock</strong> — Vasopressin used as a vasopressor when catecholamines insufficient</li>
</ul>
