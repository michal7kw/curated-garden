<h1>Parkinson's Disease</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Parkinson's Disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disorder after Alzheimer's Disease. It primarily affects the dopaminergic neurons in the <strong>substantia nigra pars compacta</strong>, leading to motor dysfunction. The pathological hallmark is the presence of <strong>Lewy bodies</strong> containing aggregated <strong>alpha-synuclein</strong> protein.</p>
<h2>Cardinal Motor Symptoms</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Tremor</strong>: Resting tremor, typically &quot;pill-rolling&quot;</li>
<li><strong>Rigidity</strong>: Increased muscle tone, &quot;cogwheel&quot; rigidity</li>
<li><strong>Bradykinesia</strong>: Slowness of movement initiation and execution</li>
<li><strong>Postural Instability</strong>: Balance and gait disturbances (late stage)</li>
</ol>
<h2>Non-Motor Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>REM sleep behavior disorder (often prodromal)</li>
<li>Anosmia (loss of smell)</li>
<li>Constipation and autonomic dysfunction</li>
<li>Depression and anxiety</li>
<li>Cognitive impairment progressing to dementia</li>
</ul>
<h2>Key Mechanisms</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Alpha-Synuclein Aggregation</strong>: Misfolded alpha-synuclein forms toxic oligomers and Lewy bodies</li>
<li><strong>Mitochondrial Dysfunction</strong>: Complex I deficiency and oxidative stress</li>
<li><strong>Neuroinflammation</strong>: Microglial activation and pro-inflammatory cytokines</li>
<li><strong>Impaired Proteostasis</strong>: Dysfunction in autophagy-lysosomal and ubiquitin-proteasome systems</li>
</ol>
<h2>Genetic Risk Factors</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>SNCA</strong>: Encodes alpha-synuclein; duplications/triplications cause familial PD</li>
<li><strong>LRRK2</strong>: Most common cause of familial PD; kinase activity inhibitors in development</li>
<li><strong>GBA1</strong>: Glucocerebrosidase mutations; strongest genetic risk factor for sporadic PD</li>
<li><strong>PINK1/Parkin</strong>: Mitophagy pathway genes; early-onset PD</li>
</ul>
