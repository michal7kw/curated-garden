<h1>Alzheimer's Disease</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Alzheimer's Disease (AD) is a fatal neurodegenerative condition characterized by the progressive loss of memory and cognitive function. Pathologically, it is defined by the accumulation of <strong>Amyloid-Beta (Aβ)</strong> plaques and <strong>Tau</strong> neurofibrillary tangles, leading to synaptic failure and neuronal death.</p>
<h2>Key Mechanisms</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Amyloid Cascade Hypothesis</strong>: Imbalance between Aβ production (via BACE1/Gamma-secretase) and clearance leads to oligomerization and plaque formation.</li>
<li><strong>Tau Pathology</strong>: Hyperphosphorylation of Tau causes microtubule disintegration and tangle formation.</li>
<li><strong>Cholinergic Hypothesis</strong>: Loss of cholinergic neurons in the basal forebrain contributes to cognitive decline.</li>
<li><strong>Genetic Risk</strong>: APOE4 allele is the strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset AD.</li>
</ol>
